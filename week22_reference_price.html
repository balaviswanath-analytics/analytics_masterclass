Week 22 · Q2: Pricing, Promotion & Revenue Optimization

The Reference Price Effect: How Anchoring Shapes Demand

A $5 price increase doesn't lose the same customers as a $5 decrease gains. Losses loom larger than gains. This asymmetry — rooted in Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory — means that how your price compares to what customers expect to pay matters more than the price itself. This week formalizes the reference price mechanism, estimates its asymmetric impact on demand, and builds the models that predict when a price change will trigger disproportionate churn.

Part 1: The 5% Increase That Cost 18% of the Base

In early 2020, a regional US telecom with 1.6 million broadband subscribers implemented a 5% rate increase across its base plans. The pricing team modeled the impact using the company's historically estimated price elasticity of −1.2, projecting a 6% volume loss offset by the 5% price gain for a net revenue improvement of roughly $14 million annually. The board approved the increase as routine — the company had raised prices by 3–5% every 18 months for a decade.

What the model missed was context. This was the first price increase since a highly publicized competitor had entered the market six months earlier, offering comparable service at 15% below the incumbent's pre-increase price. The competitor's existence had reset customers' reference price — the price they considered "fair" and "expected" — from the incumbent's rate to the lower competitive rate. The 5% increase didn't register as a modest inflation adjustment in customers' minds. It registered as a 20% premium over the alternative, pushing the price well outside the latitude of acceptance.

Within 90 days, the company lost 288,000 subscribers — an 18% churn rate on the affected base, triple what the elasticity model predicted. Post-hoc analysis using the reference price framework revealed why: the standard elasticity model treats a price increase and a price decrease symmetrically. But the loss coefficient (customers' sensitivity to prices above their reference point) was 2.4x the gain coefficient (sensitivity to prices below reference). The true predicted churn, using the asymmetric model, was 16.8% — almost exactly what occurred. The company had the data to forecast the disaster. They just used the wrong model.

The $14 million in projected revenue gains evaporated. Instead, the lost subscribers represented $112 million in annual recurring revenue. The net annual impact was −$98 million. It took 22 months and a $30 million win-back campaign to recover half the lost base — at a lower ARPU than the original cohort, because win-back offers required promotional pricing that further eroded the reference point.

Why Standard Elasticity Models Fail at Price Increases

The log-log elasticity model from Week 14 assumes that demand response is symmetric: a 5% increase and a 5% decrease produce equal-magnitude volume changes in opposite directions. Behavioral economics has known since 1979 (Kahneman & Tversky) that this is empirically false. Losses are felt roughly 2–2.5x more intensely than equivalent gains. When you raise prices, you're not moving along a demand curve — you're triggering a loss-aversion response whose magnitude depends on the gap between the new price and the customer's internal reference price. The models in this week capture this asymmetry explicitly.

Part 2: Kahneman & Tversky's Prospect Theory (1979)

Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky's 1979 Econometrica paper "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk" is the most cited paper in the history of economics. Its central insight: people evaluate outcomes not in absolute terms but relative to a reference point, and their sensitivity to losses (outcomes below the reference) is roughly twice their sensitivity to gains (outcomes above the reference).

PROSPECT THEORY VALUE FUNCTION
v(x) = xα    if x ≥ 0 (gains)
v(x) = −λ(−x)β    if x < 0 (losses)
α ≈ β ≈ 0.88 | λ ≈ 2.25 (loss aversion coefficient) | Tversky & Kahneman (1992) cumulative prospect theory estimates

Three features of the value function matter for pricing:

FeatureMathematical PropertyPricing Implication
Reference DependenceUtility is evaluated as v(p − r), not v(p), where r is the reference priceA $50 price feels cheap if the reference is $70 but expensive if the reference is $40 — even though the price is identical
Loss Aversionλ ≈ 2.25: the slope of the value function is steeper for losses than for gainsA $5 price increase triggers 2.25x the behavioral response of a $5 decrease
Diminishing Sensitivityα, β < 1: the function is concave for gains and convex for lossesThe first $1 above the reference price hurts the most; subsequent dollars have diminishing marginal sting
From Lab to Market: The Empirical Evidence

Hardie, Johnson, and Fader (1993) applied prospect theory to actual brand-choice data (orange juice scanner panel data) and confirmed that loss aversion operates in real purchase decisions, not just laboratory gambles. Their estimates: the loss coefficient for price was 2.48x the gain coefficient. Subsequent studies across categories (Putler 1992 on eggs, Bell & Lattin 2000 on grocery, Mazumdar et al. 2005 meta-analysis of 52 studies) consistently find loss-to-gain ratios between 1.5 and 3.0, with a median around 2.0–2.5. This is one of the most robust findings in behavioral economics.

Part 3: The Reference Price Demand Model

Russell Winer's 1986 Marketing Science paper "A Reference Price Model of Brand Choice for Frequently Purchased Products" formalized how to incorporate reference prices into demand estimation. The core insight: split the price variable into two components — the price itself and its deviation from the reference price — and estimate separate coefficients for each.

What Is the Reference Price?

The reference price is the price a customer expects to pay based on their prior experience. Several formation mechanisms have been proposed and empirically tested:

Formation RuleFormulaWhen It DominatesKey Paper
Exponential Smoothingrt = φ · pt-1 + (1−φ) · rt-1Frequently purchased products (grocery, CPG)Winer (1986)
Last Price Paidrt = pt-1Infrequent purchases; subscription servicesLattin & Bucklin (1989)
Average of Last N Pricesrt = (1/N) ∑ pt-kProducts with volatile pricing (fashion, travel)Mazumdar & Papatla (2000)
Contextual (Competitor)rt = competitive price or market averageCommoditized markets with transparent pricingHardie et al. (1993)

The exponential smoothing model is the most commonly used because it captures a key behavioral fact: recent prices have more influence on expectations than distant prices, but the influence decays gradually rather than dropping to zero. The parameter φ (typically 0.3–0.7) governs the speed of adaptation. A high φ means customers quickly update their reference to the most recent price (short memory); a low φ means the reference is sticky and slow to move (long memory).

WINER (1986) REFERENCE PRICE DEMAND MODEL
Uit = β0 + βp pit + βgain max(rit − pit, 0) + βloss min(rit − pit, 0) + εit
U = utility (or log demand) | p = actual price | r = reference price | gain = price below ref | loss = price above ref

The model splits the reference price effect into two separate variables: a gain variable (activated when the actual price is below the reference — the customer perceives a deal) and a loss variable (activated when the actual price is above the reference — the customer perceives a penalty). By estimating βgain and βloss separately, the model captures the asymmetry that standard elasticity models miss.

Interpreting the Coefficients

βp is the baseline price sensitivity (equivalent to the standard elasticity coefficient). βgain is the additional positive demand response when price is below reference. βloss is the additional negative demand response when price is above reference. If |βloss| > |βgain|, loss aversion is confirmed. The loss aversion ratio λ = |βloss| / |βgain| typically falls between 1.5 and 3.0. A ratio of 2.0 means a $1 price increase above the reference destroys twice as much demand as a $1 decrease below the reference creates.

Part 4: Modeling Asymmetric Price Response

The practical consequence of loss aversion is that the demand response to a price change depends on its direction relative to the reference price. This creates a kinked demand curve: steep above the reference price (customers are very sensitive to increases) and shallow below it (customers are less responsive to decreases).

Worked Example: Telecom Broadband Plan

Suppose your broadband plan is currently $59.99/month and the reference price (based on exponential smoothing of the last 12 months of market prices) is $57.00. Your estimated coefficients from the Winer model:

ParameterEstimateInterpretation
βp (base price effect)−0.042Each $1 in price reduces demand by 4.2% (baseline)
βgain+0.018Each $1 below reference adds 1.8% demand (deal bonus)
βloss−0.041Each $1 above reference destroys 4.1% demand (loss penalty)
Loss aversion ratio (λ)2.28Losses hurt 2.28x more than gains help

Current state: the actual price ($59.99) is $2.99 above the reference ($57.00), so the loss variable is active. Now consider two scenarios:

ScenarioNew PriceGap from RefActive VariableTotal Effect on Demand
A: Raise to $64.99$64.99+$7.99 (loss)βp × 5 + βloss × 5−0.042(5) + (−0.041)(5) = −41.5%
B: Drop to $54.99$54.99−$2.01 (gain)βp × (−5) + βgain × 5+0.042(5) + 0.018(5) = +30.0%
Standard model (no ref)——βp × 5 only±21.0% (symmetric)

The standard elasticity model predicts a symmetric 21% demand change for a $5 move in either direction. The reference price model reveals the truth: a $5 increase destroys 41.5% of demand (almost double the standard prediction), while a $5 decrease only recovers 30.0% (less than 50% more than the standard prediction). This asymmetry is why naive elasticity models consistently underpredict the damage from price increases and overpredict the benefit of price decreases.

The Ratchet Effect

Because losses are felt more strongly than gains, a cycle of "increase $5, then decrease $5 to win them back" does not return you to the starting point. The increase loses more customers than the decrease recovers. Each cycle ratchets demand downward. This is why "raise now, promotional discount later" strategies are insidious: each iteration erodes the base. Mazumdar, Raj, and Sinha (2005) documented this in their meta-analysis, finding that the long-run effect of a price increase followed by an equivalent decrease is a net loss of 8–15% of the affected customer base.

Part 5: Latitude of Acceptance — How Far Can You Push?

Kalyanaram and Little (1994) introduced the concept of the latitude of acceptance (also called the "zone of indifference") — a range around the reference price within which price changes have little or no effect on purchase probability. Only when the price moves outside this zone does the reference price effect activate.

LATITUDE OF ACCEPTANCE
If |pt − rt| ≤ δ, the reference price effect is dormant
If pt − rt > δ, the loss effect activates
If rt − pt > δ, the gain effect activates
δ = width of the indifference zone (typically 5–15% of the reference price)

This is why small, frequent price increases (2–3% annually) often pass without resistance while a single large increase (8–10%) triggers disproportionate churn. The small increases stay within the latitude of acceptance; the large increase breaches it, activating loss aversion.

Price Change StrategyLatitude StatusExpected Customer ResponseWhen to Use
Small, annual increase (2–3%)Likely within zoneMinimal churn; reference price gradually adjusts upwardDefault strategy for mature products
Large, infrequent increase (8–15%)Likely outside zoneSignificant churn spike; reference price takes 6–12 months to catch upAvoid unless justified by major value addition
Increase bundled with feature additionShifts reference upwardIncrease is absorbed if perceived value increase ≥ price increaseBest practice for above-zone increases
Competitive price match (decrease)Moves into gain zoneModerate demand boost; establishes new lower referenceDefensive response to market entry
Estimating the Latitude Width

Kalyanaram and Winer (1995) proposed a practical estimation approach: fit a threshold regression model where the reference price effect has a "dead zone" of width δ around rt. Use grid search over δ values (1% to 20% of mean price) and select the δ that maximizes model fit (AIC). Alternatively, segment customers by their price sensitivity decile and examine at which price-gap percentile churn first spikes — the inflection point estimates the latitude boundary. In telecom, typical estimates are 5–8% for contract customers and 10–15% for prepaid/no-contract.

Part 6: Implementation in Python

Python · Reference Price Formation + Winer Model
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression
import statsmodels.api as sm
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# =============================================
# STEP 1: Simulate Weekly Price & Demand Data
# For real data: IRI Academic Dataset (store-level
# scanner data) or your company's plan pricing history
# =============================================
np.random.seed(42)
n_weeks = 156  # 3 years of weekly data

# Realistic telecom pricing: base $59.99, occasional changes
base_price = 59.99
prices = np.full(n_weeks, base_price)
# Price events: increase at week 52, promo at week 80, increase at week 120
prices[52:] = 62.99    # +5% increase
prices[80:92] = 54.99   # 12-week promotional price
prices[92:] = 62.99     # back to new standard
prices[120:] = 65.99    # second increase

# =============================================
# STEP 2: Compute Reference Price (Exp. Smoothing)
# =============================================
phi = 0.4  # smoothing parameter (0=sticky, 1=immediate)
ref_price = np.zeros(n_weeks)
ref_price[0] = prices[0]
for t in range(1, n_weeks):
    ref_price[t] = phi * prices[t-1] + (1 - phi) * ref_price[t-1]

# Gain and Loss variables
gap = ref_price - prices  # positive = price below ref (gain)
gain = np.maximum(gap, 0)
loss = np.minimum(gap, 0)  # negative values = price above ref

# =============================================
# STEP 3: Generate Demand with Asymmetric Response
# =============================================
base_demand = 50000
beta_p = -300      # base price sensitivity
beta_gain = 150    # bonus demand per $1 below ref
beta_loss = -350   # penalty per $1 above ref
true_lambda = abs(beta_loss) / abs(beta_gain)  # = 2.33

demand = (base_demand + beta_p * (prices - base_price)
          + beta_gain * gain + beta_loss * loss
          + np.random.normal(0, 500, n_weeks)).clip(0)

df = pd.DataFrame({'week': range(n_weeks), 'price': prices,
                    'ref_price': ref_price, 'gain': gain,
                    'loss': loss, 'demand': demand})

# =============================================
# STEP 4: Estimate Standard vs. Winer Model
# =============================================
X_standard = sm.add_constant(df[['price']])
model_standard = sm.OLS(df['demand'], X_standard).fit()

X_winer = sm.add_constant(df[['price', 'gain', 'loss']])
model_winer = sm.OLS(df['demand'], X_winer).fit()

print("=== Model A: Standard Price Model ===")
print(f"  R-squared: {model_standard.rsquared:.4f}  AIC: {model_standard.aic:.1f}")

print(f"\n=== Model B: Winer Reference Price Model ===")
print(f"  R-squared: {model_winer.rsquared:.4f}  AIC: {model_winer.aic:.1f}")
print(f"  Price coef:  {model_winer.params['price']:.1f}")
print(f"  Gain coef:   {model_winer.params['gain']:.1f}")
print(f"  Loss coef:   {model_winer.params['loss']:.1f}")
est_lambda = abs(model_winer.params['loss']) / abs(model_winer.params['gain'])
print(f"\n  Estimated loss aversion ratio: {est_lambda:.2f}x")
print(f"  True loss aversion ratio:      {true_lambda:.2f}x")
Python · Latitude of Acceptance + Scenario Simulator
# =============================================
# STEP 5: Latitude Width via Grid Search
# =============================================
results = []
for delta_pct in np.arange(0.01, 0.21, 0.01):
    delta = delta_pct * df['ref_price']
    gap = df['ref_price'] - df['price']
    gain_lat = np.where(gap > delta, gap - delta, 0)
    loss_lat = np.where(gap < -delta, gap + delta, 0)
    X_lat = sm.add_constant(pd.DataFrame({
        'price': df['price'], 'gain': gain_lat, 'loss': loss_lat}))
    try:
        m = sm.OLS(df['demand'], X_lat).fit()
        results.append({'delta_pct': delta_pct, 'aic': m.aic})
    except: pass

best = pd.DataFrame(results).sort_values('aic').iloc[0]
print(f"=== Latitude of Acceptance ===")
print(f"  Optimal delta: {best['delta_pct']:.0%} of reference price")

# =============================================
# STEP 6: Scenario Analysis
# =============================================
current_price = 62.99; current_ref = 60.50; current_demand = 44000
b_p = model_winer.params['price']
b_g = model_winer.params['gain']
b_l = model_winer.params['loss']

scenarios = [
    ('Hold price', 62.99), ('Small increase +3%', 64.88),
    ('Large increase +8%', 68.03), ('Competitive match -5%', 59.84)]

print(f"\n{'Scenario':30s} {'Price':>7s} {'Gap':>7s} {'Demand':>8s} {'Revenue':>10s}")
for name, p in scenarios:
    g = max(current_ref - p, 0); l = min(current_ref - p, 0)
    g0 = max(current_ref - current_price, 0); l0 = min(current_ref - current_price, 0)
    dd = b_p*(p - current_price) + b_g*(g - g0) + b_l*(l - l0)
    nd = max(current_demand + dd, 0)
    print(f"{name:30s} ${p:>6.2f} ${current_ref-p:>+6.2f} {nd:>8,.0f} ${nd*p:>10,.0f}")
