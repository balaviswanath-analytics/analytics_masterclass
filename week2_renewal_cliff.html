Week 02 · Q1: Customer Lifetime & Retention Economics

The Renewal Cliff: Predicting Contract Churn 90 Days Before It Happens

Last week you learned WHEN customers die (Kaplan-Meier) and WHETHER they're alive (BG/NBD). This week you learn WHY they die. The Cox Proportional Hazards model lets you identify which factors accelerate or decelerate churn — and build an early-warning system that flags at-risk contracts months in advance.

Part 1: The Business Problem

You're the Head of Customer Success at a B2B SaaS company with 3,000 enterprise contracts. The CEO's question this time: "Which contracts are we going to lose at renewal, and what can we do about it?"

Last week's tools won't cut it here. Kaplan-Meier tells you the overall survival curve, but it doesn't tell you why certain customers churn faster. BG/NBD is designed for non-contractual settings where churn is invisible — but in B2B SaaS, you know exactly when a contract is up for renewal. You need a different weapon.

Why Kaplan-Meier Isn't Enough

KM curves can compare segments (e.g., enterprise vs. SMB), but they handle one variable at a time. In reality, churn is driven by dozens of factors simultaneously: product usage, support ticket volume, executive sponsor changes, competitive pricing, contract size, NPS scores, feature adoption. You need a model that evaluates all these factors together, controlling for confounders, and gives you a single interpretable metric for each factor's impact.

The $2M Scenario

Imagine a telecom company with 500,000 subscribers, each on a 12 or 24-month contract. If usage drops by 30% in the 3 months before renewal, the churn probability triples. If a customer calls support more than 5 times per quarter, they're 2.1x more likely to leave. The Cox model quantifies these multipliers precisely — the hazard ratio. Now your retention team knows exactly which signals to monitor and when to intervene. One telecom client using this approach reduced contract churn by 18%, saving $2.3M in annual recurring revenue.

Part 2: The Cox Proportional Hazards Model

Sir David Cox published this model in 1972. It remains the most widely used regression technique in survival analysis. What makes it special is that it's semi-parametric: it makes no assumption about the shape of the baseline hazard function (how churn risk changes over time in general), but it assumes that covariates have a multiplicative effect on that hazard.

THE COX PROPORTIONAL HAZARDS MODEL
h(t | X) = h0(t) · exp(β1X1 + β2X2 + ... + βpXp)
h0(t) = baseline hazard  |  X = covariates  |  β = learned coefficients

In plain English: the risk of churning at any time t equals a baseline risk (which can be any shape) multiplied by an exponential function of your risk factors. The beauty is that the baseline hazard cancels out when comparing two individuals — you only need to estimate the β coefficients.

What Goes Into the Model

Your covariates (X variables) are the factors you believe might influence churn. In a typical B2B SaaS or telecom context:

CategoryExample CovariatesExpected Direction
UsageMonthly active users, feature adoption rate, login frequencyHigher usage → lower churn (protective)
SupportTickets filed, escalations, resolution timeMore tickets → higher churn (risk)
FinancialContract value, discount depth, payment delaysBigger contracts → more switching cost → lower churn
RelationshipNPS score, executive sponsor changes, CSM engagementSponsor change → dramatically higher churn
CompetitiveCompetitor evaluation signals, RFP participationDirect churn accelerator
The Model's Elegance

The Cox model estimates its parameters using partial likelihood — a method that only looks at the ordering of events, not their exact timing. At each moment someone churns, the model asks: "Among everyone still at risk, what covariate profile best explains why THIS person was the one to churn?" This is why it doesn't need to know the shape of h0(t) — it never directly estimates it. The partial likelihood approach also handles censoring naturally: customers who haven't churned yet contribute to the risk pool until their censoring time, then exit.

Part 3: Hazard Ratios — The Currency of Risk

The key output of the Cox model is the hazard ratio (HR) for each covariate. This is exp(β), and it tells you the multiplicative effect on churn risk.

INTERPRETING HAZARD RATIOS
HR = exp(β) = multiplicative change in hazard per unit change in X
HR ValueMeaningBusiness Translation
HR = 1.0No effectThis factor doesn't influence churn
HR = 1.550% higher hazardThis factor INCREASES churn risk by 50%
HR = 2.0Double the hazardDoubles the chance of churning at any point
HR = 0.730% lower hazardThis factor PROTECTS against churn by 30%
HR = 0.5Half the hazardCuts churn risk in half (strong protection)

Reading This Like a Strategist

Contract term is the most powerful lever. Customers on 2-year contracts have only 2.5% of the churn hazard compared to month-to-month. That's a 97.5% risk reduction. The business implication: incentivize longer contracts aggressively, even at a modest discount.

Support tickets are a red flag. Customers with >5 tickets per quarter are 2.1x more likely to churn. This is your early-warning signal. Set up an automated alert when a customer crosses 3 tickets in a quarter — that's the intervention window.

Higher monthly charges increase churn. Each $10 increase raises churn risk by 20%. This doesn't mean "lower prices" — it means high-paying customers need proportionally more value demonstration.

Paperless billing surprisingly increases churn. HR = 1.4. Why? These customers may be less "locked in" to established payment routines, or paperless billing correlates with lower engagement. Worth investigating further.

Confidence Intervals — When to Trust the HR

Always check the 95% confidence interval. If it crosses 1.0 (e.g., [0.85, 1.15]), the effect is not statistically significant. The p-value tells you the same thing (p < 0.05 = significant), but the CI gives you the range of plausible effect sizes, which is more useful for business decisions. A variable with HR = 1.5 and CI [1.1, 2.0] is much less precise than one with HR = 1.5 and CI [1.4, 1.6].

Part 4: Time-Varying Covariates — When Risk Changes Over Time

This is where the Cox model becomes truly powerful — and where most analysts stop short. In the basic model, covariates are measured once (at baseline). But in reality, customer behavior changes over time. Usage might drop gradually. Support tickets might spike. The executive sponsor might leave. These are time-varying covariates, and they require a special data format.

The Data Transformation

Instead of one row per customer, you create multiple rows per customer, each representing a time interval where the covariate values are constant. This is called the "counting process" or "start-stop" format.

customer_idstartstopchurnedusage_scoretickets_qtr
A0010900851
A001901800723
A0011802700456
A0012703201288

Look at Customer A001: usage steadily dropped from 85 to 28, while support tickets climbed from 1 to 8. The churn event happened at day 320. With time-varying covariates, the Cox model can pick up on this trajectory — it's not just the level of usage that matters, it's the trend. A customer at usage 45 who was previously at 85 is in much more danger than a customer who's always been at 45.

The 90-Day Early Warning System

Here's the practical gold: if you update your Cox model monthly with fresh covariate values, you can generate a "churn risk score" for every customer that updates in real-time. When a customer's predicted hazard crosses a threshold (say, 2x the baseline), trigger a retention intervention. Companies using this approach commonly report 15-25% reduction in preventable churn because they're catching the slide early, not reacting after the customer has already mentally checked out.

When Time-Varying Covariates Are Essential

Use them when your covariates genuinely change over the customer lifecycle: usage metrics, billing behavior, support interactions, engagement scores. Don't use them for static demographics (age, industry, acquisition channel) — those are fine as baseline covariates.

Part 5: The Proportional Hazards Assumption — And What to Do When It Fails

The model's core assumption is right there in the name: proportional hazards. It means that the hazard ratio between any two individuals remains constant over time. If premium customers have half the churn risk of basic customers at month 1, they should still have half the risk at month 12.

Testing the Assumption

The standard test uses Schoenfeld residuals. After fitting the model, you plot scaled Schoenfeld residuals against time for each covariate. If the assumption holds, the plot should show a random scatter around zero (no trend). A clear upward or downward trend means the covariate's effect changes over time — the assumption is violated.

In the lifelines Python library, this is beautifully simple:

Python
cph.check_assumptions(df, p_value_threshold=0.05, show_plots=True)
# Lifelines will automatically:
# 1. Run the Schoenfeld residual test for each covariate
# 2. Generate diagnostic plots
# 3. Print warnings for any violations
# 4. Suggest remedies

Three Fixes When the Assumption Fails

FixWhen to UseHow
StratificationA categorical variable (e.g., contract type) has non-proportional hazards but you don't care about its HRFit separate baseline hazards per stratum: strata=['contract_type']
Time interactionA covariate's effect genuinely changes over time and you want to quantify howAdd X * log(t) as a covariate; significant interaction means effect changes over time
Time-varying covariateThe covariate itself changes over time (e.g., usage score)Restructure data into start-stop format (covered in Part 4)
The Common Trap

Many analysts ignore the proportional hazards check entirely, treating Cox regression like ordinary logistic regression. This can produce wildly misleading hazard ratios. If the assumption is violated for a key variable, your HR is essentially an "average" of a changing effect, which may not represent reality at any specific time point. Always run the diagnostic. In lifelines, it's literally one line of code.

Part 6: Implementation in Python

Python · Complete Cox PH Pipeline
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from lifelines import CoxPHFitter
from lifelines import KaplanMeierFitter
from lifelines.statistics import logrank_test

# ============================================
# STEP 1: Prepare Data
# Required: duration column, event column, covariates
# ============================================

df = pd.read_csv('contracts.csv')

# Ensure binary event column (1 = churned, 0 = censored)
# Duration = months from contract start to churn/censor
print(df[['tenure_months', 'churned', 'monthly_charges',
        'contract_type', 'support_tickets']].head())

# Encode categoricals
df = pd.get_dummies(df, columns=['contract_type'], drop_first=True)


# ============================================
# STEP 2: Fit the Cox PH Model
# ============================================

cph = CoxPHFitter(penalizer=0.01)  # small penalty prevents overfitting

covariates = ['monthly_charges', 'support_tickets',
              'usage_score', 'has_partner',
              'contract_type_One year', 'contract_type_Two year']

cph.fit(
    df[covariates + ['tenure_months', 'churned']],
    duration_col='tenure_months',
    event_col='churned'
)

cph.print_summary()


# ============================================
# STEP 3: Check Proportional Hazards Assumption
# THIS IS NON-NEGOTIABLE
# ============================================

cph.check_assumptions(
    df[covariates + ['tenure_months', 'churned']],
    p_value_threshold=0.05,
    show_plots=True
)


# ============================================
# STEP 4: Visualize Hazard Ratios (Forest Plot)
# ============================================

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(8, 5))
cph.plot(ax=ax)
ax.set_title('Hazard Ratios (95% CI)')
ax.axvline(x=0, color='grey', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()


# ============================================
# STEP 5: Generate Individual Risk Scores
# ============================================

# Partial hazard = exp(beta * X) for each customer
df['risk_score'] = cph.predict_partial_hazard(
    df[covariates]
)

# Predict survival probability at specific times
# "What's the probability this customer survives 12 more months?"
survival_12m = cph.predict_survival_function(
    df[covariates].iloc[:5]
)

# Rank customers by risk
top_risk = df.nlargest(20, 'risk_score')[
    ['customer_id', 'risk_score', 'tenure_months',
     'support_tickets', 'usage_score']
]
print("Top 20 At-Risk Customers:")
print(top_risk)

The Concordance Index

After fitting, check the concordance index (C-index) in the summary output. This is the survival analysis equivalent of AUC. It measures how often the model correctly ranks who churns first. C-index = 0.5 is random, 0.7+ is acceptable, 0.8+ is strong. For customer churn models, expect 0.65-0.80 depending on data quality and feature richness.

Common Pitfalls

Top 5 Week 2 Mistakes

1. Skipping the PH assumption check. This is the most common error. Run check_assumptions() every time. Period.

2. Including too many covariates. Rule of thumb: at least 10-15 events (churns) per covariate. With 200 churns, limit yourself to 13-20 variables. Overfitting makes hazard ratios unreliable.

3. Confusing hazard ratio with probability. HR = 2.0 does NOT mean "twice as likely to churn." It means the instantaneous churn RATE is doubled. Over a long period, the cumulative effect depends on the baseline hazard shape.

4. Using future information. With time-varying covariates, ensure the covariate value at time t was knowable BEFORE time t. Using data from after the event is "lookahead bias" and will produce impossibly good results.

5. Ignoring multicollinearity. If monthly charges and contract value are highly correlated, their individual HRs become unstable. Check VIF or correlation matrices before fitting.
