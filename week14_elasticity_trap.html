The Elasticity Trap: Why a 10% Price Cut Costs More Than You Think

A VP proposes a 10% price reduction to "boost volume." Sounds reasonable. But without measuring price elasticity, you can't know if the volume increase will offset the margin loss. Most of the time, it won't. This week you'll learn to estimate demand elasticity from observational data, understand the log-log regression framework, compute cross-price effects, and build the break-even analysis that prevents pricing disasters.

Part 1: The $6M Pricing Mistake

A mid-market SaaS company dropped its monthly price from $99 to $89 (a 10.1% cut) expecting a surge in new subscriptions. Six months later: new subscriptions rose 7%, but revenue per customer fell 10.1%. Net effect: revenue dropped 3.8%, which on a $160M base meant a $6.1M annual loss. The volume gain didn't come close to compensating for the margin sacrifice.

The mistake? They assumed demand was elastic (elasticity > 1 in absolute terms). It was actually inelastic at ε = -0.7. Meaning a 10% price cut only generated a 7% volume increase — mathematically guaranteed to lose money.

The Break-Even Rule

For a price cut to be revenue-neutral, volume must increase by more than the percentage price cut, adjusted for margin. Specifically: %ΔVolume ≥ %ΔPrice / (1 + %ΔPrice). For a 10% price cut, volume must increase by at least 11.1%. For a 20% price cut, volume must increase by 25%. Most products aren't elastic enough to clear this bar, which is why the majority of price cuts destroy value.

Part 2: The Log-Log Demand Model

The standard framework for estimating price elasticity is the constant-elasticity (log-log) model. By taking the natural log of both price and quantity, the regression coefficient directly gives you the elasticity.

LOG-LOG DEMAND MODEL
ln(Q) = α + ε · ln(P) + β'X + u
Q = quantity demanded | P = price | ε = price elasticity | X = controls | u = error

The beauty of this specification: the coefficient ε on ln(P) is directly interpretable as "a 1% increase in price leads to an ε% change in quantity demanded." No additional transformation needed. This works because the derivative of ln(Q) with respect to ln(P) is dQ/Q ÷ dP/P = percentage change in Q per percentage change in P, which is exactly the definition of elasticity.

Why Log-Log and Not Linear?

ModelSpecificationElasticityAssumption
Log-Log (constant elasticity)ln(Q) = a + ε ln(P)Constant across all price levelsMultiplicative demand: same % response everywhere
LinearQ = a + bPVaries: ε = b · P/QAbsolute response is constant; elasticity changes
Semi-logln(Q) = a + bPVaries: ε = b · PElasticity increases linearly with price
When Constant Elasticity Fails

The log-log model assumes the same elasticity at $50 and $500. This is often reasonable within a normal operating range (say, ±20% of current price) but breaks down for extreme price changes. If you're considering a 50%+ price change, use a semi-log or piecewise model instead. Also: elasticity is not a fixed property of the product — it varies by segment, season, competitive environment, and how recently the price changed.

Part 3: Arc Elasticity — When You Only Have Two Data Points

In practice, you often don't have continuous price variation. You have: "We charged $99 for 12 months and sold X units. We moved to $89 and sold Y units." Arc elasticity handles this discrete case by using the midpoint method, avoiding the asymmetry of simple percentage changes.

ARC ELASTICITY (MIDPOINT METHOD)
εarc = [(Q2 - Q1) / ((Q1 + Q2)/2)] ÷ [(P2 - P1) / ((P1 + P2)/2)]
Uses average of start/end as base → symmetric, direction-independent
Why Midpoint?

Simple elasticity using starting values gives different answers depending on direction: going from $100 to $90 is a -10% change, but going from $90 to $100 is an +11.1% change. The midpoint method uses the average as the base, giving the same elasticity regardless of direction. This matters when you're comparing elasticities across products or time periods.

Part 4: Cross-Price Elasticity

Your product's demand doesn't exist in isolation. When a competitor drops their price, your demand changes too. Cross-price elasticity measures how sensitive your demand is to a competitor's (or substitute's or complement's) price.

CROSS-PRICE ELASTICITY
εij = %ΔQi / %ΔPj
εij > 0 → substitutes (competitor price up, your demand up) | εij < 0 → complements

In the log-log framework, cross-price elasticity is estimated simply by adding the competitor's log-price as an additional regressor:

EXTENDED LOG-LOG WITH CROSS-PRICE
ln(Qi) = α + εown · ln(Pi) + εcross · ln(Pj) + β'X + u
εcross RangeInterpretationStrategic Implication
> 0.5Strong substituteCompetitor price drops will hurt you significantly; match or differentiate
0.1 to 0.5Moderate substituteMonitor competitor pricing; respond selectively
-0.1 to 0.1IndependentCompetitor pricing is largely irrelevant to your demand
< -0.1ComplementPartner pricing matters; co-promote

Part 5: The Break-Even Volume Formula

Before any price change, run the break-even analysis. The question: "How much additional volume do I need to maintain the same profit?"

BREAK-EVEN VOLUME CHANGE
%ΔQBE = −%ΔP / (CM% + %ΔP)
CM% = contribution margin percentage (revenue minus variable costs / revenue)

For a product with 60% contribution margin:

Price CutBreak-Even Volume Increase RequiredImplied Minimum Elasticity
5%+9.1%-1.82
10%+20.0%-2.00
15%+33.3%-2.22
20%+50.0%-2.50
Most Products Are Inelastic

Empirical studies consistently find that most B2B products have elasticities between -0.5 and -2.0, and most consumer goods between -1.0 and -3.0 (Tellis, 1988; Bijmolt et al., 2005). At a 60% margin, you need elasticity of -2.0 or stronger just to break even on a 10% price cut. This means the majority of price cuts destroy profit, even when they increase volume. The math is unforgiving.

Part 6: Implementation in Python

Python · Price Elasticity Estimation
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import statsmodels.api as sm

df = pd.read_csv('pricing_data.csv')
# Columns: date, product, price, quantity_sold,
#          competitor_price, promo_flag, season

# === Log-Log Demand Model ===
df['ln_q'] = np.log(df['quantity_sold'])
df['ln_p'] = np.log(df['price'])
df['ln_p_comp'] = np.log(df['competitor_price'])

X = sm.add_constant(df[['ln_p', 'ln_p_comp',
                        'promo_flag', 'season']])
model = sm.OLS(df['ln_q'], X).fit(cov_type='HC1')
print(model.summary())

own_elasticity = model.params['ln_p']
cross_elasticity = model.params['ln_p_comp']
print(f"\nOwn-price elasticity:   {own_elasticity:.3f}")
print(f"Cross-price elasticity: {cross_elasticity:.3f}")

# === Break-Even Analysis ===
CM = 0.60  # contribution margin
price_cuts = [0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20]

print("\n=== Break-Even Analysis ===")
for cut in price_cuts:
    be_vol = cut / (CM - cut)
    predicted_vol = -own_elasticity * cut
    profitable = predicted_vol > be_vol
    print(f"  {cut:.0%} cut: need +{be_vol:.1%} volume, "
          f"model predicts +{predicted_vol:.1%} → "
          f"{'PROFITABLE' if profitable else 'DESTROYS VALUE'}")

# === Arc Elasticity (two-period comparison) ===
def arc_elasticity(q1, q2, p1, p2):
    pct_q = (q2 - q1) / ((q1 + q2) / 2)
    pct_p = (p2 - p1) / ((p1 + p2) / 2)
    return pct_q / pct_p

# Example: price went from 99 to 89, units from 1000 to 1070
arc_e = arc_elasticity(1000, 1070, 99, 89)
print(f"\nArc elasticity: {arc_e:.3f}")

Part 7: Your Week 14 Playbook

QuestionMethod
"How sensitive is demand to our price?"Log-log regression: coefficient on ln(P) = own-price elasticity
"Will a price cut increase revenue?"Break-even formula: compare required vs. predicted volume change
"How does competitor pricing affect us?"Add ln(P_competitor) to the log-log model → cross-price elasticity
"Is elasticity the same for all segments?"Run separate models by segment or add interaction terms
"We only have before/after data"Arc elasticity (midpoint method)

Top 5 Mistakes

#MistakeConsequence
1Using correlation (price vs. quantity) without controlsConfounders (seasonality, promotions) bias the estimate
2Assuming constant elasticity across huge price rangesLog-log is a local approximation; breaks at extremes
3Ignoring endogeneity (prices set in response to demand)OLS estimates are biased; need IV or natural experiments
4Forgetting the margin in break-even analysisRevenue-neutral ≠ profit-neutral; margin is the denominator
5Treating elasticity as permanentElasticity changes with competitive context, season, and customer awareness
