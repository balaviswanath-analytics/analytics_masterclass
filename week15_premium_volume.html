Week 15 · Q2: Pricing, Promotion & Revenue Optimization

Premium vs. Volume: The Statistical Case for Raising Prices

Most companies underprice. They're terrified of losing customers but have never actually measured what customers would willingly pay. The Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter is a lightweight survey method that maps the entire demand curve from four simple questions. This week, you'll learn to design, execute, and analyze a pricing study that reveals your optimal price point, acceptable range, and whether your product has premium pricing power.

Part 1: The Underpricing Epidemic

A McKinsey study found that a 1% improvement in price (without volume loss) improves operating profit by 8-11%, compared to just 3-4% for a 1% improvement in volume. Yet most product teams spend 10x more energy on acquisition than on pricing optimization. Why? Because price increases feel risky and irreversible, while growth initiatives feel safe and expansive. The data tells a different story.

The Asymmetry of Pricing Errors

Underpricing costs you on every single transaction, forever, silently. Overpricing costs you on lost transactions, which are visible and alarming. This asymmetry makes companies systematically underprice. Van Westendorp helps quantify exactly how much room you have above your current price before you hit the "too expensive" threshold — and the answer is almost always more room than you'd expect.

Part 2: The Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter

Developed by Dutch economist Peter van Westendorp in 1976, this technique uses four questions to map the psychological price boundaries of your market. Each respondent gives four prices, creating four cumulative distribution curves whose intersections define pricing zones.

The Four Questions

#QuestionMeasuresCurve Label
1"At what price would this product be so cheap that you'd question its quality?"Lower bound of credibilityToo Cheap
2"At what price would this product be a bargain — a great buy for the money?"Lower bound of acceptable rangeCheap / Good Value
3"At what price would this product start to feel expensive but you'd still consider it?"Upper bound of considerationExpensive / High
4"At what price would this product be too expensive to consider, regardless of quality?"Upper bound of credibilityToo Expensive
Why It Works

Each question maps to a distinct psychological threshold. Together they produce four cumulative curves whose intersections reveal pricing landmarks that no single "what would you pay?" question can capture. The method is lightweight (4 questions, no conjoint complexity), works with small samples (n=100-300 is typically sufficient), and produces results that are intuitive for non-technical stakeholders.

Part 3: Survey Design Best Practices

Before the Price Questions

Context matters enormously. Before showing the price questions, present the product with enough detail that respondents can form a genuine value judgment: product description, key features, competitive alternatives, and the specific use case. Without this anchoring, responses become meaningless guesses.

Sample Requirements

ParameterRecommendation
Minimum sample size100-150 per segment you want to analyze separately
Target respondentsActual buyers or qualified prospects (not random consumers)
Data quality checksRemove responses where Too Cheap > Bargain or Expensive > Too Expensive (logical inconsistencies)
Price rangeLeave open-ended (don't constrain with dropdowns); use integer input fields
The Hypothetical Bias

Van Westendorp (and all stated-preference methods) suffer from hypothetical bias: people say they'd pay more than they actually will. Calibrate by comparing results to actual transaction data where possible. A common rule of thumb: discount the "optimal price point" by 10-15% for real-world pricing. Alternatively, use the "Point of Marginal Cheapness" as a more conservative anchor.

Part 4: Analysis — The Four Intersections

Plot four cumulative distribution curves from the responses. The key intersections define your pricing map:

IntersectionDefinitionMeaning
Point of Marginal Cheapness (PMC)Too Cheap = ExpensiveBelow this, you're leaving money on the table for everyone
Point of Marginal Expensiveness (PME)Too Expensive = CheapAbove this, you're losing too many price-sensitive buyers
Indifference Price Point (IDP)Cheap = ExpensiveThe "normal" price; equal numbers find it cheap vs. expensive
Optimal Price Point (OPP)Too Cheap = Too ExpensiveMinimizes the share who reject on price (either direction)
The Acceptable Price Range

The range between PMC and PME is your acceptable pricing zone. You can price anywhere in this range without significant demand destruction. The IDP is where the market "expects" your price. The OPP is the theoretically optimal point. If your current price is below the PMC, you are definitively underpriced — even the most price-sensitive respondents think you could charge more.

Part 5: Implementation in Python

Python · Van Westendorp Analysis
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import interpolate

survey = pd.read_csv('vw_survey.csv')
# Columns: too_cheap, bargain, expensive, too_expensive

# === Data Quality ===
valid = survey[
    (survey['too_cheap'] <= survey['bargain']) &
    (survey['bargain'] <= survey['expensive']) &
    (survey['expensive'] <= survey['too_expensive'])
]
print(f"Valid responses: {len(valid)}/{len(survey)}")

# === Build CDFs ===
prices = np.arange(valid[['too_cheap']].min().min(),
                    valid[['too_expensive']].max().max() + 1)

def ecdf_above(data, price_grid):
    """% of respondents who gave a value >= each price"""
    return np.array([(data >= p).mean() for p in price_grid])

def ecdf_below(data, price_grid):
    """% of respondents who gave a value <= each price"""
    return np.array([(data <= p).mean() for p in price_grid])

too_cheap_cdf  = ecdf_below(valid['too_cheap'], prices)
bargain_cdf    = ecdf_below(valid['bargain'], prices)
expensive_cdf  = ecdf_above(valid['expensive'], prices)
too_exp_cdf    = ecdf_above(valid['too_expensive'], prices)

# === Find Intersections ===
def find_intersect(curve1, curve2, price_grid):
    diff = curve1 - curve2
    idx = np.where(np.diff(np.sign(diff)))[0]
    if len(idx) == 0: return None
    return price_grid[idx[0]]

PMC = find_intersect(too_cheap_cdf, expensive_cdf, prices)
PME = find_intersect(too_exp_cdf, bargain_cdf, prices)
IDP = find_intersect(bargain_cdf, expensive_cdf, prices)
OPP = find_intersect(too_cheap_cdf, too_exp_cdf, prices)

print(f"Point of Marginal Cheapness (PMC):      ${PMC}")
print(f"Point of Marginal Expensiveness (PME):   ${PME}")
print(f"Indifference Price Point (IDP):          ${IDP}")
print(f"Optimal Price Point (OPP):               ${OPP}")
print(f"Acceptable Range: ${PMC} - ${PME}")

# === Plot ===
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 7))
ax.plot(prices, too_cheap_cdf, '--', color='#e63946', lw=2, label='Too Cheap')
ax.plot(prices, bargain_cdf, color='#2d6a4f', lw=2, label='Bargain')
ax.plot(prices, expensive_cdf, color='#e9c46a', lw=2, label='Expensive')
ax.plot(prices, too_exp_cdf, '--', color='#c45d3e', lw=2, label='Too Expensive')
for pt, lbl in [(PMC,'PMC'),(PME,'PME'),(IDP,'IDP'),(OPP,'OPP')]:
    if pt: ax.axvline(x=pt, ls=':', alpha=0.4, color='gray')
    if pt: ax.annotate(lbl, (pt, 0.5), fontsize=11, fontweight='bold')
ax.set_title('Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter', fontsize=14, fontweight='bold')
ax.set_xlabel('Price ($)'); ax.set_ylabel('Cumulative Proportion')
ax.legend(); plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()
