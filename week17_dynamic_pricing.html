Dynamic Pricing: From Littlewood to Contextual Bandits

Static pricing leaves money on the table when demand fluctuates. Airlines, hotels, ride-sharing, and increasingly SaaS and e-commerce companies adjust prices in real-time based on demand signals. This week covers the full spectrum: from classical revenue management (Littlewood's Rule, EMSR) to modern machine learning approaches (Thompson Sampling, contextual bandits) for learning optimal prices online.

Part 1: The Case for Dynamic Pricing

An event ticketing company sells 10,000 seats at a fixed $75. They sell out 3 weeks before the event. Problem: the last 2,000 buyers would have paid $120+. Meanwhile, early birds who bought at $75 might have bought at $60. By pricing dynamically, the company could capture an additional $300K-$500K in revenue from the same 10,000 seats. The product is identical — only the timing of the transaction changes.

When Dynamic Pricing Makes Sense

Perishable inventory: Unsold airline seats, hotel rooms, event tickets, ad impressions — value drops to zero after the deadline. Heterogeneous willingness-to-pay: Some customers will pay much more than others for the same product. Demand uncertainty: You don't know demand in advance and need to learn from market signals. Low marginal cost: Selling one more unit costs almost nothing (digital goods, seats, subscriptions).

Part 2: Littlewood's Rule — The Foundation

Littlewood (1972) posed the simplest revenue management problem: you have C seats, two fare classes (high and low), and low-fare demand arrives before high-fare demand. How many seats should you protect for the high fare?

LITTLEWOOD'S RULE
Accept a low-fare booking if: Plow ≥ Phigh · P(Dhigh ≥ C − already sold)
Protect seats for high fare when the expected marginal revenue from waiting exceeds the low fare

In practice: if your low fare is $200, high fare is $500, and there's a 45% chance that high-fare demand will fill the remaining seats, then the expected value of protecting a seat is $500 × 0.45 = $225. Since $225 > $200, protect the seat. If the probability drops to 35%, expected value = $175 < $200, so sell it at the low fare.

The Protection Level

The protection level y* for the high fare is the number of seats where Littlewood's inequality flips: P(Dhigh ≥ y*) = Plow / Phigh. If demand is normally distributed: y* = μ + σ · Φ-1(1 − Plow/Phigh). This single formula is the foundation of all revenue management systems. Everything else is a generalization.

Part 3: EMSR — Multiple Fare Classes

Real businesses have more than two fare classes. EMSR (Belobaba, 1987, 1989) extends Littlewood to n fare classes using a nested protection level structure.

EMSR-b (The Industry Standard)

For each pair of adjacent fare classes, EMSR-b aggregates all higher-fare demand into a single virtual class, then applies Littlewood's rule once. This is computationally simpler than the original EMSR-a (which applies Littlewood iteratively) and produces near-optimal results.

Part 4: Thompson Sampling for Price Optimization

Classical revenue management assumes you know the demand distribution. What if you don't? This is the exploration-exploitation trade-off: you must simultaneously learn the demand curve (exploration) and maximize revenue (exploitation). Multi-armed bandits, specifically Thompson Sampling, solve this elegantly.

THOMPSON SAMPLING FOR PRICING
For each price pk: sample θ̃k ~ Posterior(conversion rate | data so far)
Offer the price that maximizes pk · θ̃k (expected revenue per visitor)

Thompson Sampling naturally balances exploration and exploitation: uncertain prices (wide posteriors) get sampled more often (exploration), while prices with strong evidence of high revenue get selected more frequently (exploitation). Over time, the algorithm converges to the revenue-maximizing price.

Contextual Bandits: Personalized Dynamic Pricing

Standard bandits choose the same price for everyone. Contextual bandits observe customer features (segment, device, time of day, purchase history) and learn price-response curves for each context. This enables personalized pricing: a price-insensitive enterprise buyer sees $149, while a price-sensitive startup founder sees $99.

The Fairness Constraint

Personalized pricing raises ethical and legal concerns. Price discrimination based on protected characteristics (race, gender, age, location in some jurisdictions) is illegal or heavily regulated. Even proxy-based discrimination (using device type as a proxy for income) creates reputational risk. Best practice: allow personalization on behavioral signals (feature usage, engagement level, purchase history) but not demographic or geographic signals. Always maintain a publicly visible "list price" as an anchor.

Part 5: Implementation in Python

Python · EMSR-b & Thompson Sampling
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm, beta

# === EMSR-b: Protection Levels ===
fares = [800, 400, 200]  # highest to lowest
means = [30, 60, 100]
stds  = [12, 20, 35]
capacity = 200

print("=== EMSR-b Protection Levels ===")
for j in range(len(fares) - 1):
    # Aggregate demand for classes 0..j
    agg_mean = sum(means[:j+1])
    agg_std = np.sqrt(sum(s**2 for s in stds[:j+1]))
    # Weighted average fare
    avg_fare = np.average(fares[:j+1], weights=means[:j+1])
    # Critical ratio
    ratio = fares[j+1] / avg_fare
    # Protection level
    y_star = agg_mean + agg_std * norm.ppf(1 - ratio)
    print(f"  Protect {y_star:.0f} seats for classes 1-{j+1} "
          f"(avg fare ${avg_fare:.0f}, ratio {ratio:.2f})")

# === Thompson Sampling for Price Testing ===
prices = [79, 89, 99, 109, 119]
# Beta priors for conversion rate at each price
alphas = np.ones(len(prices))   # successes + 1
betas  = np.ones(len(prices))   # failures + 1

np.random.seed(42)
# True conversion rates (unknown to the algorithm)
true_rates = [0.12, 0.10, 0.08, 0.06, 0.04]

total_revenue = 0
for t in range(5000):
    # Sample from posterior
    samples = [beta.rvs(a, b) for a, b in zip(alphas, betas)]
    # Expected revenue = price * sampled conversion rate
    exp_rev = [p * s for p, s in zip(prices, samples)]
    chosen = np.argmax(exp_rev)

    # Simulate customer response
    converted = np.random.rand() < true_rates[chosen]
    if converted:
        alphas[chosen] += 1
        total_revenue += prices[chosen]
    else:
        betas[chosen] += 1

print(f"\n=== Thompson Sampling Results (5000 visitors) ===")
print(f"Total revenue: ${total_revenue:,.0f}")
for i, p in enumerate(prices):
    n = alphas[i] + betas[i] - 2
    rate = (alphas[i]-1) / n if n > 0 else 0
    print(f"  ${p}: shown {n:.0f}x, conv rate {rate:.3f}, "
          f"rev/visitor ${p*rate:.2f}")

Top 5 Mistakes
