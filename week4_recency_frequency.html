Week 04 · Q1: Customer Lifetime & Retention Economics

The Recency-Frequency Trade-off: Which Matters More?

Week 1 introduced BG/NBD. This week we go deeper into the full family of "buy till you die" models — the Pareto/NBD (the original) and the Gamma-Gamma model for monetary value. You'll build a complete CLV engine from scratch and master the diagnostics that separate amateurs from experts.

Part 1: The Recency-Frequency Paradox

Here's a question that trips up even experienced analysts. Who is more valuable — a customer who made 20 purchases over 2 years but hasn't bought in 8 months, or a customer who made 3 purchases but their last one was yesterday?

Most RFM scoring systems rank the 20-purchase customer higher because of the massive frequency advantage. But probabilistic models tell a completely different story.

The Paradox Explained

Customer A: 20 purchases, last purchase 8 months ago, observed for 24 months. High frequency, low recency.

Customer B: 3 purchases, last purchase 1 day ago, observed for 12 months. Low frequency, high recency.

The BG/NBD model calculates P(Alive) for Customer A at approximately 0.15 (85% chance they've churned) and for Customer B at approximately 0.92 (very likely still active). Why? Customer A's pattern screams "binge and burn" — they bought furiously and then stopped. The 8-month gap far exceeds their average inter-purchase time of ~5 weeks. Customer B's 3 purchases are spaced consistently, and the most recent is fresh.

Why Traditional RFM Fails

ApproachHow It Handles R vs. FFatal Flaw
Simple RFM ScoringAssigns arbitrary points (R=5, F=5, M=5) and sums themTreats R and F as independent; equal weighting is arbitrary
Rule-Based ("Active if bought in 12 months")Binary threshold on recency onlySame threshold for weekly and yearly buyers makes no sense
BG/NBD / Pareto/NBDModels the joint distribution of R and F, conditional on individual buying ratesCorrect — evaluates recency relative to each customer's personal rhythm

The critical insight: recency must be evaluated relative to frequency. A customer who buys weekly and hasn't bought in 2 months is in trouble. A customer who buys quarterly and hasn't bought in 2 months is right on schedule. Probabilistic models capture this automatically; scoring systems do not.

Part 2: The Pareto/NBD Model — The Original

Before Fader and Hardie's BG/NBD (Week 1), Schmittlein, Morrison, and Colombo published the Pareto/NBD model in 1987. It was the original "counting your customers" framework and remains important for two reasons: it's the theoretical foundation for everything that followed, and in some settings it provides a better fit.

How Pareto/NBD Differs from BG/NBD

AspectBG/NBD (Week 1)Pareto/NBD (Original)
When can a customer "die"?Only immediately after a purchaseAt any point in continuous time
Dropout processGeometric (discrete coin-flip after each buy)Exponential (continuous hazard)
EstimationSimple MLE, works in ExcelComplex numerical integration
Prediction accuracyVery similar in most settingsSlightly better when dropout is truly continuous
Parametersr, α (buying), a, b (dying)r, α (buying), s, β (dying)
PARETO/NBD: THE DROPOUT PROCESS
Customer lifetime ~ Exponential(μ)   where μ ~ Gamma(s, β)
Marginal lifetime follows a Pareto (Type II) distribution — hence the name

In plain English: each customer has a personal "death rate" μ (how quickly they disengage). This rate varies across the population following a Gamma distribution. A customer with low μ sticks around for years; a customer with high μ drops off within weeks. The model learns the population-level distribution of these death rates from the aggregate data.

When Pareto/NBD Beats BG/NBD

In businesses where customer "death" genuinely happens between purchases — not at a decision point after buying — Pareto/NBD is theoretically more appropriate. Think gym memberships (people stop going before they cancel), media subscriptions (reading declines before renewal), or app usage (engagement drops across weeks before formal churn). However, the practical difference is usually less than 5% in prediction accuracy, which is why BG/NBD dominates in industry — it's dramatically easier to implement.

Part 3: Head-to-Head — Choosing Your Model

Fader and Hardie (2005) directly compared both models on the classic CDNOW dataset (online CD retailer, 23,570 customers over 78 weeks). The results were remarkably close.

The Decision Framework

Which Model Should You Use?

Start with BG/NBD in all cases. It's faster, more stable, easier to explain, and gives nearly identical results to Pareto/NBD in 90%+ of real-world datasets.

Switch to Pareto/NBD if: (a) your holdout validation shows BG/NBD consistently underperforms on your specific data, (b) your business model genuinely involves continuous-time dropout, or (c) you're publishing research and need to benchmark against the original formulation.

Consider PyMC-Marketing Bayesian CLV if you want: uncertainty quantification on individual CLV estimates, the ability to incorporate prior domain knowledge, or more flexibility in model specification. This is the cutting edge.

Part 4: The Gamma-Gamma Model — Putting Dollars on Transactions

BG/NBD and Pareto/NBD predict how many future transactions a customer will make. To calculate CLV, you also need how much each transaction is worth. Enter the Gamma-Gamma model.

The Model Structure

Each customer has a latent "true" average transaction value (ν). Observed transaction amounts vary randomly around this mean. Across the population, these true averages follow a Gamma distribution. The model learns the population parameters and then produces a Bayesian-updated estimate for each individual.

GAMMA-GAMMA MODEL
zij | νi ~ Gamma(p, p/νi)   |   νi ~ Gamma(q, γ)
z = transaction value | ν = true mean value | p, q, γ = population parameters

The Critical Independence Assumption

Check This BEFORE Using Gamma-Gamma

The model assumes monetary value is independent of purchase frequency. If frequent buyers also spend more (or less) per transaction, the assumption is violated and CLV estimates will be biased.

How to check: Compute Pearson correlation between frequency and average monetary value.

|r| < 0.15 → Safe to proceed

|r| 0.15 - 0.30 → Proceed with caution, note limitation

|r| > 0.30 → Need an alternative (segment first, or use a monetary model that handles correlation)

Bayesian Shrinkage in Action

A customer who made 1 purchase of $500 looks like a whale. But with only one observation, how confident are you? The Gamma-Gamma model says: "Given the population distribution and this single observation, the best estimate of their true average is probably closer to $320" (pulled toward the population mean). A customer with 15 purchases averaging $500 gets an estimate much closer to $500 because 15 observations provide strong evidence.

CustomerRepeat PurchasesObserved Avg SpendGamma-Gamma EstimateShrinkage
Alice1$500$320Strong (toward population mean of $250)
Bob5$500$430Moderate
Carol15$500$485Minimal (data dominates)
Dave50$500$498Nearly zero
Why This Matters Commercially

Without shrinkage, you'd overvalue low-frequency high-spenders and undervalue high-frequency moderate-spenders. This directly impacts acquisition budget allocation, loyalty tier assignment, and customer prioritization. Bayesian shrinkage is the "secret sauce" that makes probabilistic CLV more reliable than simple averages, especially for the long tail of customers who have limited purchase history.

Model Validation: The Holdout Test

The gold standard for validating CLV models is the calibration-holdout split. Split your transaction data at a point in time: fit the model on the first period (calibration), predict for the second period (holdout), and compare predictions to actuals.

Validation CheckWhat to CompareGood Result
Aggregate transactionsTotal predicted purchases vs. actual in holdoutWithin 5-10% at aggregate level
Individual-level accuracyPredicted vs. actual purchases per customerMAE better than simple heuristics (e.g., "last period's count")
Frequency bin comparisonGroup by calibration frequency, compare predicted vs. actual holdout transactions per groupClose tracking across all frequency bins
Conditional expectationsPlot predicted vs. actual for each recency/frequency cellDiagonal pattern (predicted ~ actual)
The lifetimes Calibration-Holdout Workflow

The lifetimes library has a built-in function for this: calibration_and_holdout_data(). It automatically splits your transaction data at a specified date, computes RFM summaries for both periods, and lets you compare model predictions against holdout actuals. This is the single most important diagnostic step in your CLV pipeline. Never deploy a CLV model without a holdout test.

Part 6: Complete Production Code

Python · Full CLV Pipeline
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from lifelines import BetaGeoFitter, ParetoNBDFitter
from lifelines import GammaGammaFitter
from lifelines.utils import (
    summary_data_from_transaction_data,
    calibration_and_holdout_data
)
from lifelines.plotting import (
    plot_frequency_recency_matrix,
    plot_probability_alive_matrix,
    plot_calibration_purchases_vs_holdout_purchases
)


# ============================================
# STEP 1: Load and Prepare Transaction Data
# ============================================

txns = pd.read_csv('transactions.csv', parse_dates=['date'])

# Full RFM summary
rfm = summary_data_from_transaction_data(
    txns,
    customer_id_col='customer_id',
    datetime_col='date',
    monetary_value_col='revenue',
    observation_period_end='2026-01-31'
)

# Calibration-holdout split for validation
cal_hold = calibration_and_holdout_data(
    txns,
    customer_id_col='customer_id',
    datetime_col='date',
    calibration_period_end='2025-09-30',
    observation_period_end='2026-01-31',
    monetary_value_col='revenue'
)


# ============================================
# STEP 2: Fit BG/NBD (Primary Model)
# ============================================

bgf = BetaGeoFitter(penalizer_coef=0.001)
bgf.fit(
    cal_hold['frequency_cal'],
    cal_hold['recency_cal'],
    cal_hold['T_cal']
)
print("BG/NBD Parameters:")
print(bgf.summary())


# ============================================
# STEP 3: Fit Pareto/NBD (Comparison)
# ============================================

pnbd = ParetoNBDFitter(penalizer_coef=0.001)
pnbd.fit(
    cal_hold['frequency_cal'],
    cal_hold['recency_cal'],
    cal_hold['T_cal']
)
print("\nPareto/NBD Parameters:")
print(pnbd.summary())


# ============================================
# STEP 4: Holdout Validation
# ============================================

# Compare predicted vs actual holdout purchases
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(14, 5))

# BG/NBD validation
plot_calibration_purchases_vs_holdout_purchases(
    bgf, cal_hold, ax=axes[0],
    n=90  # holdout period in days
)
axes[0].set_title('BG/NBD: Calibration vs Holdout')

# Pareto/NBD validation
plot_calibration_purchases_vs_holdout_purchases(
    pnbd, cal_hold, ax=axes[1],
    n=90
)
axes[1].set_title('Pareto/NBD: Calibration vs Holdout')
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()


# ============================================
# STEP 5: Gamma-Gamma for Monetary Value
# ============================================

# Filter to repeat customers
rfm_repeat = rfm[rfm['frequency'] > 0]

# CHECK INDEPENDENCE ASSUMPTION
corr = rfm_repeat[['frequency', 'monetary_value']].corr()
print(f"\nFreq-Monetary Correlation: {corr.iloc[0,1]:.3f}")
print("(Should be near 0 for Gamma-Gamma to work)")

ggf = GammaGammaFitter(penalizer_coef=0.001)
ggf.fit(rfm_repeat['frequency'], rfm_repeat['monetary_value'])

# Expected average transaction value per customer
rfm_repeat['expected_avg_value'] = ggf.conditional_expected_average_profit(
    rfm_repeat['frequency'],
    rfm_repeat['monetary_value']
)


# ============================================
# STEP 6: Complete CLV Calculation
# ============================================

# Refit BG/NBD on full dataset (not cal-hold split)
bgf_full = BetaGeoFitter(penalizer_coef=0.001)
bgf_full.fit(rfm['frequency'], rfm['recency'], rfm['T'])

# 12-month CLV with 1% monthly discount rate (~12.7% annual)
rfm['clv_12m'] = ggf.customer_lifetime_value(
    bgf_full,
    rfm['frequency'],
    rfm['recency'],
    rfm['T'],
    rfm['monetary_value'],
    time=12,           # months
    discount_rate=0.01  # monthly
)

# P(Alive) for every customer
rfm['p_alive'] = bgf_full.conditional_probability_alive(
    rfm['frequency'], rfm['recency'], rfm['T']
)

# Segment by CLV
rfm['segment'] = pd.qcut(
    rfm['clv_12m'], 5,
    labels=['Bronze', 'Silver', 'Gold',
            'Platinum', 'Diamond']
)

# Customer Health Score (composite)
rfm['health_score'] = (
    rfm['p_alive'] * 0.5 +
    rfm['clv_12m'].rank(pct=True) * 0.5
)

print("\n=== CLV Summary by Segment ===")
print(rfm.groupby('segment').agg(
    customers=('clv_12m', 'count'),
    avg_clv=('clv_12m', 'mean'),
    avg_p_alive=('p_alive', 'mean'),
    total_clv=('clv_12m', 'sum')
).round(2))

Common Pitfalls

Top 5 Week 4 Mistakes

1. Deploying without holdout validation. A model that fits calibration data perfectly can still produce terrible forecasts. Always split and validate.

2. Ignoring the Gamma-Gamma independence check. If frequency and monetary value are correlated (>0.3), your CLV estimates are biased. Segment first or find an alternative monetary model.

3. Using the wrong discount rate. A 1% monthly rate (~12.7% annual) is standard. Using your company's exact WACC is better. Using 0% (no discounting) vastly overstates long-horizon CLV.

4. Treating CLV as deterministic. CLV is a probabilistic estimate with uncertainty. A customer with CLV = $500 might have a 95% confidence interval of [$200, $1,200]. For high-stakes decisions, consider Bayesian approaches (PyMC-Marketing) that give you full posterior distributions.

5. Forgetting to convert revenue CLV to margin CLV. Revenue CLV is not profit. Multiply by gross margin % before making acquisition or retention budget decisions.
