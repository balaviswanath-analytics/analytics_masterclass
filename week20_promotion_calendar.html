Week 20 · Q2: Pricing, Promotion & Revenue Optimization

The Promotion Calendar Trap: Why More Isn't Better

A CPG company ran 47 promotions last year across its product portfolio. Revenue during promoted weeks was up 25%. The VP of Sales pointed to this as proof that the strategy was working. But full-year revenue was flat year-over-year. How can 47 weeks of 25% lifts produce zero annual growth? Because promotions weren't creating demand — they were shifting it. This week teaches you to decompose promotional impact from baseline demand using time series intervention analysis, detect the saturation point, and find the optimal promotion frequency.

Part 1: The Promotion Addiction Cycle

The CPG company's story follows a pattern so common it has a name in industry: the promotion death spiral. It begins innocuously. A brand runs a quarterly promotion and sees strong results. Leadership asks for monthly promotions. Results hold, but non-promoted weeks start declining. By year two, the brand is running biweekly promotions, and the "promoted week" lift has shrunk from 25% to 12%. More troublingly, non-promoted weeks are now 15% below where they were two years ago.

What happened? Three compounding mechanisms drove the spiral. First, pull-forward: customers learned the promotion cadence and shifted purchases to promoted weeks. No new demand was created; it was simply redistributed. Second, stockpiling: customers bought extra units during promotions, reducing their need for weeks afterward. Third, and most insidiously, the training effect: repeated promotions taught customers that the promoted price was the "real" price, permanently lowering their reference price and eroding willingness to pay at full price.

Van Heerde, Leeflang, and Wittink (2004) decomposed the "promotion bump" for 560 brands and found that on average, 33% of the bump was pull-forward from adjacent weeks, 25% was category expansion (true incremental), and 42% was brand switching. After accounting for the pull-forward and the margin cost, the average promotion had negative net profit contribution.

The Compounding Trap

Revenue during promotions looks great → leadership demands more promotions → customers learn to wait → non-promotion revenue drops → you need more promotions to hit quarterly targets → the baseline erodes further → repeat. Breaking this cycle requires measuring net incremental revenue (promotion lift minus pull-forward minus stockpiling) and baseline erosion (the training effect over time). If your year-over-year non-promoted-week revenue is declining, you're already in the spiral.

The Grocery Store Benchmark

Research by Ailawadi et al. (2007) in the Journal of Marketing found that the average grocery retailer spent 59% of its trade promotion budget on subsidizing purchases that would have occurred anyway. The brands that broke the cycle shared one trait: they measured the post-promotion trough as rigorously as they measured the promotional peak. When the trough exceeded 50% of the peak, they cut promotion frequency regardless of promotional-week performance.

Part 2: Three Temporal Effects Your Dashboard Doesn't Show

Standard business reporting shows "promoted week vs. same week last year." This metric captures the gross lift but hides the three temporal effects that determine true profitability:

Most companies only measure the first term. The second and third terms often turn profit negative.

The pull-forward and post-promotion dip are temporary and roughly cancel the gross lift for many promotions. Baseline erosion is the truly dangerous one because it's permanent and cumulative. Each promotion trains a few more customers to expect discounts, imperceptibly shifting the demand curve leftward at full price. By the time it shows up in annual revenue, you're years into the spiral.

Part 3: ARIMA — Modeling the Counterfactual Baseline

To isolate promotional impact, you first need a model of what sales would have been without the promotion. This counterfactual baseline is what ARIMA provides: a mathematical model of your business's natural demand patterns — trend, seasonality, and autocorrelation — cleaned of promotional effects.

The ARIMA(p, d, q) Framework

ARIMA MODEL
(1 − φ1B − ... − φpBp)(1 − B)d yt = c + (1 + θ1B + ... + θqBq)εt
B = backshift operator (Byt = yt-1) | φ = AR coefficients | θ = MA coefficients | d = differencing order
ComponentParameterWhat It CapturesHow to Identify
AR(p)p autoregressive termsMomentum: this week's sales depend on the last p weeks' salesPartial ACF shows significant spikes at lags 1...p then cuts off
I(d)d differencing stepsNon-stationarity: the series has a trend or unit rootADF test p-value > 0.05 → needs differencing
MA(q)q moving average termsShock persistence: residual effects that decay over q periodsACF shows significant spikes at lags 1...q then cuts off

Seasonal Extension: SARIMA(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)m

Most business time series have seasonality. The seasonal ARIMA adds a second set of parameters at the seasonal lag. For weekly data with annual seasonality, m = 52. The seasonal AR(P) and MA(Q) terms capture patterns like "sales this week depend on sales the same week last year." In practice, SARIMA(1,1,1)(1,1,0)52 is a strong default starting point for weekly retail or telecom data.

Model Selection: The Box-Jenkins Protocol

(1) Identify: Plot the series, ACF, and PACF. Check stationarity with ADF test. Determine d and D. (2) Estimate: Fit candidate models with different p, q, P, Q. Use AIC/BIC to compare. (3) Diagnose: Check that residuals are white noise (Ljung-Box test, p > 0.05). No significant ACF spikes in residuals. (4) Forecast: Generate counterfactual baseline by predicting what sales would be in promoted weeks without the promotion exogenous variable.

Part 4: Intervention Analysis (Box & Tiao, 1975)

George Box and George Tiao developed intervention analysis in their landmark 1975 JASA paper to model the effect of known external events (policy changes, strikes, environmental interventions) on time series. The approach extends ARIMA by adding exogenous indicator variables for the intervention periods.

The SARIMAX with Promotions

SARIMAX INTERVENTION MODEL
yt = SARIMA(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)m + ω0Pt + ω1Pt-1 + ω2Pt-2 + δDt
Pt = promotion indicator at time t | ωk = impact at lag k | Dt = post-promotion dip indicator

The ω coefficients capture the promotion's temporal profile:

CoefficientInterpretationExpected Sign
ω0Immediate lift during the promoted weekPositive (the promotional bump)
ω1Carryover effect: residual lift one week after promotionPositive but smaller than ω0 (decaying)
ω2Second-week carryoverNear zero for most promotions
δPost-promotion dip in the first non-promoted weekNegative (demand payback from pull-forward)

The net incremental impact of a promotion = ω0 + ω1 + ω2 + δ. If this sum is close to zero, the promotion simply shifted demand across weeks without creating any new revenue. If it's negative (the dip exceeds the lift), the promotion actually destroyed value by training customers and eroding baseline.

Transfer Function Shapes

Box and Tiao classified intervention effects by their temporal shape. Each shape has a different business interpretation:

ShapeMathematical FormBusiness Example
Pulseω0 spike, zero afterFlash sale: one-day lift with no carryover or dip
Pulse + Decayω0 spike with exponentially decaying carryoverTV ad campaign: awareness lifts sales for 2-3 weeks
Pulse + Dipω0 spike followed by below-baseline dipDiscount promotion with pull-forward: the most common pattern
Step ChangePermanent shift in levelPermanent price change or product launch (not a typical promotion)
Gradual OnsetEffect builds over multiple periodsLoyalty program introduction: cumulative enrollment drives gradual lift
The Pulse + Dip Signature

When you see ω0 = +$50K, ω1 = +$5K, δ = −$40K, the net impact is +$15K. But the company reported a $50K lift. The $35K difference between reported and actual is the "phantom lift" — demand that was merely shifted, not created. If your margin sacrifice on the promoted volume exceeds $15K, the promotion lost money despite looking like a success.

Part 5: Promotion Saturation — Finding the Optimal Frequency

Even when individual promotions are profitable, there exists a frequency beyond which each additional promotion yields diminishing (and eventually negative) incremental returns. The marketing science literature models this using the ADBUDG saturation function developed by John D.C. Little (1979).

ADBUDG SATURATION MODEL
Lift(n) = α · nβ / (γβ + nβ)
n = promotion count in period | α = maximum achievable lift | γ = half-saturation point | β = steepness

The function is S-shaped: the first few promotions generate large lifts, the middle promotions add incrementally, and beyond the half-saturation point γ, each additional promotion adds negligibly. The optimal frequency is where the marginal incremental lift equals the marginal cost (promotion execution cost + margin sacrifice + baseline erosion).

Worked Example

A brand estimates α = $500K (maximum quarterly lift), γ = 4 (half-saturation at 4 promotions per quarter), β = 2. Each promotion costs $30K in execution and generates an average $8K in baseline erosion per subsequent quarter.

Promos / QtrCumulative LiftMarginal LiftMarginal CostMarginal Profit
1$29K$29K$38K−$9K

4$250K$75K$38K+$37K

The optimal frequency is approximately 6-7 promotions per quarter. Beyond that, each additional promotion costs more than it generates. At 10 promotions, you've spent $380K to generate $431K in lift — a razor-thin margin before accounting for baseline erosion. The company running 47 annual promotions (~12 per quarter) was deep into the negative-marginal-return zone.

How to Estimate Your Saturation Curve

You need variation in promotion frequency across comparable periods. Options: (1) use natural variation across regions that receive different promotion schedules; (2) use historical variation if frequency has changed over time; (3) design a deliberate holdout experiment with different promotion intensities by geography. Fit the ADBUDG curve to the observed {frequency, lift} pairs using nonlinear least squares. The half-saturation point γ is your strategic anchor.

Part 6: Implementation in Python

Python · SARIMAX Intervention Analysis
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
from statsmodels.tsa.statespace.sarimax import SARIMAX
from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller
from statsmodels.stats.diagnostic import acorr_ljungbox
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

sales = pd.read_csv('weekly_sales.csv', parse_dates=['week'],
                    index_col='week')
# Columns: revenue, promo_flag, promo_depth_pct

# === Step 1: Stationarity Check ===
adf = adfuller(sales['revenue'])
print(f"ADF Statistic: {adf[0]:.4f}")
print(f"p-value: {adf[1]:.4f}")
print(f"Differencing needed: {'Yes' if adf[1] > 0.05 else 'No'}")

# === Step 2: Build Exogenous Variables ===
sales['promo_lag1'] = sales['promo_flag'].shift(1).fillna(0)
sales['promo_lag2'] = sales['promo_flag'].shift(2).fillna(0)
# Post-promo dip: first non-promoted week after a promoted week
sales['post_promo'] = ((sales['promo_flag'] == 0) &
                        (sales['promo_lag1'] == 1)).astype(int)

exog_cols = ['promo_flag', 'promo_lag1', 'post_promo']
exog = sales[exog_cols]

# === Step 3: Fit SARIMAX ===
model = SARIMAX(sales['revenue'], exog=exog,
                order=(2, 1, 1),
                seasonal_order=(1, 1, 0, 52),
                enforce_stationarity=False,
                enforce_invertibility=False)
results = model.fit(disp=False)

# === Step 4: Diagnose Residuals ===
lb = acorr_ljungbox(results.resid, lags=[10, 20])
print(f"\nLjung-Box p-values: {lb['lb_pvalue'].values}")
print(f"White noise? {'Yes' if lb['lb_pvalue'].min() > 0.05 else 'No'}")

# === Step 5: Decompose Promotion Impact ===
w0 = results.params['promo_flag']
w1 = results.params['promo_lag1']
dip = results.params['post_promo']
net = w0 + w1 + dip

print(f"\n{'='*50}")
print(f"PROMOTION DECOMPOSITION")
print(f"{'='*50}")
print(f"  Immediate lift (w0):    ${w0:>+12,.0f}")
print(f"  Week-1 carryover (w1):  ${w1:>+12,.0f}")
print(f"  Post-promo dip (delta): ${dip:>+12,.0f}")
print(f"  {'─'*40}")
print(f"  NET incremental:        ${net:>+12,.0f}")
print(f"  Phantom lift:           ${w0 - net:>+12,.0f}")
print(f"  Phantom as % of gross:  {(w0-net)/w0*100:>11.0f}%")

# === Visualization ===
fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 1, figsize=(14, 10), sharex=True)

# Actual vs. fitted baseline
axes[0].plot(sales.index, sales['revenue'], alpha=0.6,
             label='Actual', color='#264653')
axes[0].plot(sales.index, results.fittedvalues, alpha=0.8,
             label='SARIMAX Fitted', color='#c45d3e')
# Shade promoted weeks
promo_weeks = sales[sales['promo_flag'] == 1].index
for w in promo_weeks:
    axes[0].axvline(x=w, color='#e9c46a', alpha=0.2)
axes[0].set_title('Revenue: Actual vs. SARIMAX Fitted',
                 fontsize=13, fontweight='bold')
axes[0].legend()

# Residuals
axes[1].bar(sales.index, results.resid, color='#264653', alpha=0.5)
axes[1].axhline(y=0, color='gray', linestyle='--')
axes[1].set_title('Residuals (should be white noise)',
                 fontsize=13, fontweight='bold')
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()
Python · Saturation Curve Estimation
from scipy.optimize import curve_fit

# Quarterly data: promotion count and measured incremental lift
qtr_data = pd.DataFrame({
    'n_promos': [2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 10, 12],
    'incremental_lift': [85, 140, 210, 265, 310, 355, 370, 365]
})

def adbudg(n, alpha, beta, gamma):
    return alpha * n**beta / (gamma**beta + n**beta)

popt, pcov = curve_fit(adbudg, qtr_data['n_promos'],
                       qtr_data['incremental_lift'],
                       p0=[500, 2, 5], maxfev=5000)
alpha, beta, gamma = popt

print(f"=== Saturation Model ===")
print(f"  Max lift (alpha): ${alpha:,.0f}K")
print(f"  Half-saturation (gamma): {gamma:.1f} promos")
print(f"  Steepness (beta): {beta:.2f}")

# Find optimal frequency
n_range = np.arange(1, 15, 0.1)
lifts = adbudg(n_range, *popt)
marginal = np.gradient(lifts, n_range)
cost_per_promo = 38  # $38K per promo (execution + erosion)
optimal_n = n_range[np.argmin(np.abs(marginal - cost_per_promo))]
print(f"  Optimal frequency: {optimal_n:.0f} promos/quarter")

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 6))
ax.plot(n_range, lifts, color='#264653', linewidth=2,
        label='Cumulative Lift')
ax.scatter(qtr_data['n_promos'], qtr_data['incremental_lift'],
           color='#c45d3e', s=80, zorder=5, label='Observed')
ax.axvline(x=optimal_n, color='#2d6a4f', linestyle='--',
           label=f'Optimal: {optimal_n:.0f}/qtr')
ax.set_title('Promotion Saturation Curve', fontsize=14,
            fontweight='bold')
ax.set_xlabel('Promotions per Quarter')
ax.set_ylabel('Incremental Lift ($K)')
ax.legend(); plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()
