Customer Reactivation Windows: Timing Your Win-Back

Week 6 asked "should you chase lost customers?" This week dives deeper: "when exactly should you reach out, and when should you give up?" Using survival analysis on dormant customer data, you'll calculate the precise reactivation window for each segment and build an automated trigger system that fires at the optimal moment.

Part 1: The Dormancy Continuum

In non-contractual businesses, customers don't announce their departure. They just stop buying. The challenge: at any given moment, some inactive customers are genuinely gone while others are simply between purchases. Week 1's BG/NBD model gives you P(Alive), but that's a snapshot. What you need is a time-aware reactivation framework that understands how the probability of return decays with each passing day of inactivity.

The Three Zones

Active zone (0-1x average inter-purchase time): Customer is between normal purchases. No intervention needed.

Dormancy zone (1-3x average IPT): Customer is outside their normal rhythm. This is the reactivation window — the period where intervention has the highest ROI.

Lost zone (>3x average IPT): Customer has almost certainly churned. Win-back attempts here have very low probability of success (Week 6 framework applies).

Part 2: Defining Dormancy Thresholds Statistically

Most companies use arbitrary thresholds: "inactive for 90 days = dormant." This ignores the massive variation in customer buying rhythms. A weekly grocery buyer who hasn't purchased in 30 days is deeply dormant. A furniture buyer who hasn't purchased in 30 days is right on schedule.

The Percentile Method

For each customer, compute their historical average inter-purchase time (IPT). A customer is dormant when their current gap exceeds a threshold relative to their personal rhythm. Common thresholds: 90th percentile of their historical inter-purchase distribution, or 2x their median IPT.

The BG/NBD Method

Alternatively, use the P(Alive) from Week 1. Set the dormancy threshold at P(Alive) < 0.5 (more likely dead than alive). This is more sophisticated because it accounts for both frequency and recency simultaneously, and handles the recency-frequency interaction that simple IPT methods miss.

Part 3: Survival Analysis of Dormant Customers

Once you've identified dormant customers, model their "time-to-reactivation" using survival analysis. The event is reactivation (a purchase after the dormancy threshold was crossed). Censoring occurs when a customer remains dormant through the observation period.

What the Hazard Function Tells You

The reactivation hazard function typically shows: a brief spike in the first 2-4 weeks of dormancy (some customers were just on vacation or had temporary cash flow issues), a rapid decline, and then a long flat tail approaching zero. The inflection point where the hazard drops below a business-relevant threshold (e.g., 2% weekly reactivation probability) defines the boundary between "worth pursuing" and "let them go."

Part 4: Building the Automated Trigger System

The end product of this analysis is an automated system that: (1) monitors each customer's gap since last purchase relative to their personal rhythm, (2) triggers a reactivation campaign when they enter the dormancy zone, (3) escalates the intervention intensity over time (email → personalized offer → phone call for high-value), and (4) stops attempting reactivation when the customer exits the viable window.

The Escalation Ladder

Week 2 (dormancy entry): Personalized "we noticed you haven't visited" email with product recommendations based on purchase history. Cost: $0.10.

Week 4: Targeted offer (10% off, free shipping). Cost: $2-5 per customer.

Week 6: For high-CLV only: direct outreach (personal email from account manager or phone call). Cost: $15-30.

Week 8+: Move to passive win-back pool (low-cost batch campaigns). Stop active investment.

Part 5: Implementation in Python

import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
from lifelines import KaplanMeierFitter, WeibullAFTFitter

txns = pd.read_csv('transactions.csv', parse_dates=['date'])

# === Step 1: Personal dormancy thresholds ===
ipt = txns.sort_values(['customer_id','date'])
ipt['gap'] = ipt.groupby('customer_id')['date'].diff().dt.days
median_ipt = ipt.groupby('customer_id')['gap'].median()

# Current gap (days since last purchase)
last_purchase = txns.groupby('customer_id')['date'].max()
today = pd.Timestamp('2026-02-01')
current_gap = (today - last_purchase).dt.days

# Dormancy flag: gap > 2x median IPT
dormancy = pd.DataFrame({
    'median_ipt': median_ipt,
    'current_gap': current_gap,
    'threshold': median_ipt * 2,
})
dormancy['is_dormant'] = dormancy['current_gap'] > dormancy['threshold']
dormancy['dormancy_days'] = (dormancy['current_gap'] - dormancy['threshold']).clip(lower=0)

# === Step 2: Reactivation survival analysis ===
# Historical: among those who became dormant, time to reactivation
dormant_history = pd.read_csv('dormancy_episodes.csv')
# days_dormant: duration of dormancy episode
# reactivated: 1=purchased again, 0=still dormant at end of observation

kmf = KaplanMeierFitter()
kmf.fit(dormant_history['days_dormant'],
       dormant_history['reactivated'])
# This gives the "survival in dormancy" curve
# P(still dormant at day t) = how long dormancy lasts

# === Step 3: Trigger assignment ===
dormant_now = dormancy[dormancy['is_dormant']]
dormant_now['action'] = pd.cut(
    dormant_now['dormancy_days'],
    bins=[0, 14, 28, 42, 56, float('inf')],
    labels=['Email nudge', 'Targeted offer',
            'High-value outreach', 'Passive pool', 'Let go']
)
print(dormant_now['action'].value_counts())
