Week 01 · Q1: Customer Lifetime & Retention Economics

The $3M Question: When Will Your Best Customers Stop Buying?

You'll master the two foundational tools that every top customer analytics team uses: Kaplan-Meier survival curves and the BG/NBD probabilistic model. By the end of this week, you'll be able to calculate the probability that any customer is still "alive" and project their lifetime value.

Part 1: The Business Problem

Imagine you're the VP of Analytics at an online fashion retailer. Your CEO walks into your office and says: "We have 200,000 customers. How many of them are actually going to buy from us again? And what are they worth?"

This sounds simple, but it's actually one of the hardest questions in business analytics. Here's why:

The "Silent Churn" Problem

In a subscription business (Netflix, Spotify), you know exactly when someone leaves — they cancel. But in a non-contractual business (Amazon, Zara, your local coffee shop), customers don't announce their departure. They just... stop showing up. The challenge: you can't tell the difference between a customer who has permanently left and one who is simply between purchases.

The Core Question

Think about Customer A who bought 8 items in the first 3 months, then went silent for 6 months. And Customer B who bought 2 items spread evenly over 9 months, with their last purchase 2 months ago. Who is more likely to buy again?

Your gut might say Customer A (they were so active!). But the data says Customer B. Customer A looks like a "binge and burn" — a rapid purchaser who likely churned. Customer B shows a slow, steady rhythm that's still in motion. This is exactly the kind of intuition that the BG/NBD model formalizes mathematically.

Why This Matters Commercially

Getting this wrong has cascading consequences. If you overestimate how many customers are "alive," you'll overproject revenue, overspend on retention campaigns targeting dead customers, and misallocate acquisition budgets. If you underestimate, you'll neglect active customers and leave money on the table.

A real-world example: a mid-size e-commerce company discovered that 40% of customers they were treating as "active" (because they'd purchased in the last 12 months) had actually already churned per the BG/NBD model. They were spending retention dollars on people who were never coming back. Redirecting that budget to genuinely at-risk customers saved them an estimated $3.2M annually in wasted marketing spend.

Part 2: Survival Analysis — The Foundation

Survival analysis is a statistical discipline originally developed in medicine to answer the question: How long until a patient dies? We've borrowed it for business because the analogous question — How long until a customer churns? — has identical mathematical properties.

Three Concepts You Must Internalize

1. The Survival Function S(t)

This is the probability that a customer "survives" (stays active) beyond time t. At time zero, everyone is alive: S(0) = 1. As time passes, S(t) decreases. The shape of this decrease tells you everything about your business's retention dynamics.

THE SURVIVAL FUNCTION
S(t) = P(T > t) = probability of surviving past time t
Where T is the random variable representing "time until churn"

2. The Hazard Function h(t)

This is the instantaneous risk of churning at time t, given that the customer has survived up to that point. Think of it as the "danger level" at each moment. A constant hazard means customers churn at the same rate regardless of how long they've been around. An increasing hazard means the longer they stay, the more likely they are to leave (fatigue). A decreasing hazard means loyalty builds over time — early survivors become increasingly sticky.

THE HAZARD FUNCTION
h(t) = limΔt→0 P(t ≤ T < t+Δt | T ≥ t) / Δt
"What's the probability of churning RIGHT NOW, given you haven't churned yet?"
Strategic Insight

The shape of your hazard function reveals your business model's retention DNA. SaaS products often show a "bathtub curve" — high early churn (trial/onboarding failures), low middle-period churn (happy users), and sometimes rising late-period churn (contract fatigue). E-commerce businesses typically show a decreasing hazard — the first 90 days are a danger zone, but customers who make it past that threshold become increasingly loyal.

3. Censoring — The Concept That Makes Survival Analysis Special

Here's why you can't just use regular regression. In your customer database right now, many customers haven't churned yet. They might churn tomorrow, or they might buy from you for another 10 years. You don't know. In statistics, we call this right-censoring — you can observe the beginning of the story but not the ending.

Consider three customers who all signed up on January 1st:

Customer A: Churned in Month 4

Observed event. You know the exact "death" time. Duration = 4 months.

Customer B: Still active today (Month 9)

Right-censored. All you know is they survived AT LEAST 9 months. Their actual lifetime is unknown but ≥ 9.

Customer C: Left your observation window in Month 6 (moved abroad)

Right-censored for a different reason. They survived at least 6 months, but you lost track of them.

The Fatal Mistake

If you simply calculate "average customer lifetime" by averaging all durations, you get (4 + 9 + 6) / 3 = 6.3 months. But this dramatically underestimates true customer lifetime because you're treating Customer B's 9 months and Customer C's 6 months as their full lifetimes when they could continue much longer. Ignoring censoring is the single most common mistake analysts make with retention data. Kaplan-Meier and the BG/NBD model both handle censoring properly.

Part 3: The Kaplan-Meier Estimator

Edward Kaplan and Paul Meier published their method in 1958. It remains, to this day, the most widely used non-parametric survival estimator in both medicine and business. Its elegance lies in its simplicity.

How It Works: Step-by-Step

The Kaplan-Meier estimator calculates the survival probability at each time point where an event (churn) occurs. The key insight: it only "counts" at moments when something happens, and it properly adjusts for the people who are censored in between.

THE KAPLAN-MEIER ESTIMATOR
&Shat;(t) = ∏ti ≤ t (1 − di / ni)
di = number who churned at time ti   |   ni = number at risk just before ti

Let me walk through a concrete example. Suppose you have 10 customers and you observe them over 12 months:

Notice how the cumulative survival probability only drops at months where churns actually happen (months 2, 4, 8, 10). And notice how censored customers reduce the "at risk" pool but don't cause the curve to drop. This is the magic of KM — it uses all available information without bias.

Reading the KM Curve Like a Strategist

What the curve shape tells you

Steep early drop, then flattening: You have an onboarding problem. Customers who survive the first 90 days tend to stick around. Action: invest heavily in first-purchase experience.

Steady linear decline: Churn is constant regardless of tenure. This often indicates a competitive market where customers are always evaluating alternatives. Action: continuous engagement programs.

Staircase pattern with plateaus: Churn happens at predictable moments (contract renewals, seasonal patterns). Action: preemptive retention campaigns timed to these inflection points.

Late-stage acceleration: Long-tenured customers eventually fatigue. Action: loyalty program redesign, win-back before they're fully gone.

Log-Rank Test: Comparing Segments

One of the most powerful uses of KM curves is comparing survival across segments. Do contract customers survive longer than month-to-month? Do customers acquired via referral churn less than paid acquisition? The log-rank test gives you a formal statistical test (chi-squared) to determine if the difference between two survival curves is significant or just noise.

Part 4: The BG/NBD Model — "Counting Your Customers"

This is where things get genuinely powerful. The BG/NBD model, published by Peter Fader, Bruce Hardie, and Ka Lok Lee in Marketing Science (2005), is the industry standard for understanding customer behavior in non-contractual settings. It answers two questions simultaneously: Is this customer still alive? and If yes, how many transactions will they make?

The Behavioral Story

The model is built on a "story" about how customers behave. Understanding this story is more important than memorizing the math:

The BG/NBD Story in Plain English

While active, each customer shops at their own personal rate. Some people buy weekly, others buy quarterly. This buying rate follows a Poisson process (random timing, but with a personal average rate).

At some point, each customer "dies" (permanently stops buying). This happens right after a purchase — the customer makes a transaction and then decides they're done. Think of it as: after every purchase, there's a coin flip determining if the customer comes back.

Across the population, buying rates vary (some customers are naturally frequent buyers, others are infrequent), and churn probabilities vary (some customers are inherently loyal, others are one-and-done). These variations are modeled using probability distributions.

The Five Assumptions (Formally)

While "alive," transactions follow a Poisson process with rate λ

Each customer has a personal transaction rate λ. If λ = 0.5 per week, they buy on average once every two weeks. The timing of each purchase is random (not clockwork), but the average rate is consistent. This is a well-validated assumption for most retail and e-commerce settings.

Business implication: High-λ customers are your power buyers. Low-λ customers aren't necessarily bad — they might have high monetary values per transaction.

Heterogeneity in λ follows a Gamma distribution across the population

Not all customers buy at the same rate. The Gamma distribution is flexible enough to capture the reality that most customers are infrequent buyers (low λ) while a small number are very frequent (high λ). This is the famous "long tail" of customer activity.

The Gamma has two parameters: r (shape) and α (scale). Together they describe the distribution of purchase rates across your entire customer base.

After any transaction, a customer "dies" with probability p

This is the BG (Beta-Geometric) part. After each purchase, there's a probability p that the customer permanently stops buying. This is a geometric process — each purchase is an independent "trial" where the customer might drop out.

Key distinction from Pareto/NBD: In the original Pareto/NBD model, customers could die at any moment. In BG/NBD, death only happens immediately after a purchase. This simplification makes the model vastly easier to estimate while producing nearly identical results.

Heterogeneity in p follows a Beta distribution across the population

Different customers have different churn probabilities. Some customers are naturally one-time buyers (high p), while others are inherently loyal (low p). The Beta distribution captures this variation with parameters γ and δ.

Together with Assumptions 1-2, this means the model learns just four parameters (r, α, γ, δ) from your data that completely describe the buying and churning behavior of your entire customer base.

The transaction rate λ and churn probability p are independent

How often someone buys is unrelated to how likely they are to churn. This is an assumption that sometimes gets challenged — in some businesses, very frequent buyers might actually burn out faster. But in practice, this assumption holds well enough for the model to produce excellent predictions.

When to worry: If your business model specifically induces churn in heavy users (e.g., limited-time promotions that attract deal-seekers who then disappear), this assumption weakens.

What the Model Gives You

Once fitted to your transaction data (which only requires three fields per customer: recency, frequency, and tenure), the BG/NBD model produces:

The Recency-Frequency Insight

The model reveals a counterintuitive truth: a customer with 20 purchases whose last purchase was 8 months ago has a LOWER P(Alive) than a customer with 3 purchases whose last purchase was last week. Recency dominates frequency. A customer who bought a lot but stopped long ago has almost certainly churned. A customer who bought infrequently but recently is likely still in the game. This is something that simple RFM scoring often gets wrong because it treats high frequency as unconditionally good.

Part 5: From P(Alive) to Customer Lifetime Value

P(Alive) and expected transactions are valuable on their own, but the real prize is Customer Lifetime Value (CLV) — the discounted present value of all future cash flows from a customer. To get there, we combine two models:

The Two-Model Architecture

BG/NBD Model
Predicts NUMBER of future transactions
Input: Recency, Frequency, T
×
Gamma-Gamma Model
Predicts average VALUE per transaction
Input: Frequency, Monetary Value
CUSTOMER LIFETIME VALUE
CLV = ∑t=1T E[transactions in t] × E[value per transaction] × (1 / (1 + d)t)
d = discount rate (cost of capital, typically 8-15% annually)

The Gamma-Gamma Model (Quick Preview)

This companion model, also from Fader and Hardie, estimates the expected monetary value per transaction. It makes one key assumption: monetary value is independent of purchase frequency. You need to verify this in your data (check that the Pearson correlation between frequency and average transaction value is near zero). If the correlation is significant, the model's estimates will be biased.

The Discount Rate: A Strategic Choice

The discount rate isn't just a technical parameter — it's a strategic statement about how much you value future cash flows relative to today. A higher discount rate means you're more "impatient" and value near-term revenue more heavily. For most businesses, use the weighted average cost of capital (WACC) converted to a monthly rate. If your company's WACC is 12% annually, that's roughly 1% monthly.

Practical CLV Interpretation

Once you have individual-level CLV projections, you can answer questions like: "What is the maximum I should spend to acquire a customer in segment X?" (Answer: no more than their projected CLV minus your margin requirement). "Which customers deserve premium support?" (Answer: top decile by CLV). "Is this customer segment growing in value?" (Answer: compare CLV distributions across acquisition cohorts).

Part 6: Implementation in Python

Here's the complete, production-ready code. This uses the lifetimes library by Cameron Davidson-Pilon, which implements BG/NBD and Gamma-Gamma with a scikit-learn-style API.

Python · lifetimes
# ============================================
# STEP 1: Install & Import
# ============================================
# pip install lifetimes pandas matplotlib

import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from lifetimes import BetaGeoFitter
from lifetimes import GammaGammaFitter
from lifetimes.utils import summary_data_from_transaction_data
from lifetimes.plotting import (
    plot_frequency_recency_matrix,
    plot_probability_alive_matrix
)
Python · Data Prep
# ============================================
# STEP 2: Prepare Your Data
# Your raw data needs: customer_id, date, revenue
# ============================================

# Load transaction data
transactions = pd.read_csv('transactions.csv', parse_dates=['date'])

# Create RFM summary (this is the magic function)
# It computes: frequency, recency, T, monetary_value
rfm = summary_data_from_transaction_data(
    transactions,
    customer_id_col='customer_id',
    datetime_col='date',
    monetary_value_col='revenue',
    observation_period_end='2026-01-31'  # freeze date
)

# CRITICAL: Filter to repeat customers for Gamma-Gamma
rfm_repeat = rfm[rfm['frequency'] > 0]

print(rfm.head(10))
# frequency = number of REPEAT purchases (not total)
# recency   = time between first and last purchase
# T         = time between first purchase and end of observation
# monetary_value = average spend per repeat transaction
Python · BG/NBD Model
# ============================================
# STEP 3: Fit the BG/NBD Model
# ============================================

bgf = BetaGeoFitter(penalizer_coef=0.001)
bgf.fit(
    rfm['frequency'],
    rfm['recency'],
    rfm['T']
)

print(bgf.summary())
# Shows the 4 learned parameters: r, alpha, a, b
# r, alpha = Gamma params for transaction rate
# a, b     = Beta params for churn probability

# Visualize the frequency-recency matrix
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(14, 5))
plot_frequency_recency_matrix(bgf, ax=axes[0])
plot_probability_alive_matrix(bgf, ax=axes[1])
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
Python · Predictions & CLV
# ============================================
# STEP 4: Generate Predictions
# ============================================

# P(Alive) for each customer
rfm['p_alive'] = bgf.conditional_probability_alive(
    rfm['frequency'], rfm['recency'], rfm['T']
)

# Expected purchases in next 90 days
rfm['predicted_purchases_90d'] = \
    bgf.conditional_expected_number_of_purchases_up_to_time(
        90,  # days
        rfm['frequency'], rfm['recency'], rfm['T']
    )

# ============================================
# STEP 5: Fit Gamma-Gamma & Calculate CLV
# ============================================

# Verify independence assumption
corr = rfm_repeat[['frequency', 'monetary_value']].corr()
print(f"Correlation: {corr.iloc[0,1]:.3f}")  # Should be near 0

ggf = GammaGammaFitter(penalizer_coef=0.001)
ggf.fit(rfm_repeat['frequency'], rfm_repeat['monetary_value'])

# FULL CLV CALCULATION
rfm['clv_12_months'] = ggf.customer_lifetime_value(
    bgf,
    rfm['frequency'],
    rfm['recency'],
    rfm['T'],
    rfm['monetary_value'],
    time=12,          # months
    discount_rate=0.01  # monthly ~12.7% annual
)

# Segment customers by CLV
rfm['segment'] = pd.qcut(
    rfm['clv_12_months'], 4,
    labels=['Low', 'Medium', 'High', 'Platinum']
)

print(rfm.groupby('segment')['clv_12_months'].describe())

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Top 5 Mistakes

1. Confusing frequency definitions. In the lifetimes library, "frequency" means REPEAT purchases (total minus 1). A customer with 5 total purchases has frequency = 4. Get this wrong and your model is garbage.

2. Ignoring censoring. If you calculate "average customer lifetime" without accounting for currently-active customers, you'll underestimate by 30-50%.

3. Applying BG/NBD to contractual businesses. If customers explicitly cancel (SaaS, telecom with contracts), use survival models directly. BG/NBD is for non-contractual settings where churn is latent.

4. Skipping the independence check. If frequency and monetary value are correlated (r > 0.3), the Gamma-Gamma model will produce biased CLV estimates. You may need to segment first or use an alternative monetary model.

5. Using raw transaction count as "frequency." Remember: a customer who bought once has frequency = 0 in the model. They've had zero repeat purchases. The model can still estimate their P(Alive) based on how long it's been since that single purchase.
