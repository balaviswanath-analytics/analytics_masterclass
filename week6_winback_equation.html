Week 06 · Q1: Customer Lifetime & Retention Economics

The Win-Back Equation: Should You Chase Lost Customers?

The instinct to win back every lost customer is powerful — and often wrong. This week you'll build a rigorous cost-benefit framework that answers the question: for each churned customer, is the expected value of reactivation greater than the cost of trying? The answer changes everything about how you allocate retention and reacquisition budgets.

Part 1: The Win-Back Fallacy

Most companies treat win-back campaigns as a blanket activity: send a discount email to everyone who left in the last 6 months. The typical result? 3-8% response rate, with most reactivated customers churning again within 90 days. The campaign "works" in the sense that some people come back, but the ROI is often negative when you account for the discount cost, the second churn, and the opportunity cost of spending those dollars elsewhere.

The $600K Wake-Up Call

An e-commerce retailer ran a "we miss you" campaign to 50,000 lapsed customers offering 25% off their next order. They reactivated 2,100 customers (4.2% rate). Sounds decent. But the average reactivated order was $45, the discount cost was $11.25 per order, campaign cost was $3 per contact, and 72% of reactivated customers never purchased again. Net ROI: -$420,000 when you factor in full costs versus the CLV of the customers who actually stuck around. They would have been better off spending that budget on retaining at-risk customers who hadn't yet churned.

The Three Questions Before Any Win-Back

Before you spend a dollar on win-back, you need to answer three questions with data, not intuition:

1. What is the probability this specific customer will reactivate? (Logistic regression on churned customer features)

2. If reactivated, what is their expected second-life CLV? (Typically 40-60% of first-life CLV — reactivated customers rarely return to full engagement)

3. What does the win-back attempt cost? (Contact cost + offer/discount + staff time + opportunity cost)

Part 2: The Win-Back ROI Framework

THE WIN-BACK DECISION RULE
Win-Back Value = P(reactivate) × E[CLV2nd life] × margin − Costattempt
If Win-Back Value > 0, pursue. If negative, redirect budget elsewhere.

The Brutal Truth

In most businesses, only 15-25% of churned customers are worth pursuing. These are typically high-CLV customers who churned recently and for addressable reasons (price, not value failure). The remaining 75-85% have negative expected win-back value — every dollar spent on them is destroyed. The elite analysts redirect that budget to retention (preventing churn before it happens), where ROI is 3-5x higher than reacquisition.

Part 3: The Reactivation Probability Model

Use logistic regression on your churned customer base to predict who will respond to win-back attempts. The dependent variable is binary: 1 = reactivated (purchased again), 0 = stayed churned. Train on historical win-back campaigns.

The Decay Curve

Reactivation probability decays roughly exponentially with time since churn. A typical pattern: 15-20% reactivation rate at 1 month post-churn, 8-12% at 3 months, 4-6% at 6 months, and below 2% after 12 months. This means timing is everything — waiting even 30 days can cut your success rate in half. The optimal win-back window is typically 14-45 days post-churn.

Part 4: The Optimal Timing Window

This is where survival analysis re-enters. Model the "time to reactivation" using KM curves on your historical win-back data. The hazard function tells you when the highest-density reactivation window is.

Second-Life CLV: The Discount Factor

Reactivated customers are NOT the same as never-churned customers. Research consistently shows that second-life CLV is 40-70% of first-life CLV. Why? The psychological commitment is weaker, they've proven they're willing to leave, and competitive awareness is higher. Always discount projected CLV for reactivated customers — using first-life CLV will massively overstate win-back ROI.

The "Let Them Go" Threshold

Use your logistic model to score every churned customer. Set a threshold where expected win-back value equals zero. Everyone above the threshold gets a win-back campaign; everyone below gets classified as "natural attrition" — accepted loss. This threshold typically sits at P(reactivate) = 10-15% for most businesses. Customers below this probability are better served by investing the budget in preventing the next customer from churning.

Part 5: Implementation in Python

Python · Win-Back Framework
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.model_selection import cross_val_score
from sklearn.calibration import CalibratedClassifierCV

# ============================================
# STEP 1: Build Reactivation Model
# ============================================

churned = pd.read_csv('churned_customers.csv')
# reactivated: 1 = came back, 0 = stayed gone

features = ['days_since_churn', 'first_life_purchases',
            'first_life_clv', 'tenure_months',
            'churn_reason_price', 'churn_reason_competitor']

X = churned[features]
y = churned['reactivated']

# Calibrated model (probabilities must be accurate!)
base_lr = LogisticRegression(C=0.1, max_iter=1000)
model = CalibratedClassifierCV(base_lr, cv=5)
model.fit(X, y)

# Cross-validated AUC
auc = cross_val_score(base_lr, X, y, cv=5, scoring='roc_auc')
print(f"AUC: {auc.mean():.3f} +/- {auc.std():.3f}")


# ============================================
# STEP 2: Score & Apply Decision Rule
# ============================================

new_churned = pd.read_csv('recent_churners.csv')
new_churned['p_reactivate'] = model.predict_proba(
    new_churned[features]
)[:, 1]

# Win-back value calculation
MARGIN = 0.40
SECOND_LIFE_DISCOUNT = 0.55  # 2nd-life CLV = 55% of 1st
COST_PER_ATTEMPT = 25

new_churned['expected_2nd_clv'] = (
    new_churned['first_life_clv'] * SECOND_LIFE_DISCOUNT
)
new_churned['winback_value'] = (
    new_churned['p_reactivate']
    * new_churned['expected_2nd_clv']
    * MARGIN
    - COST_PER_ATTEMPT
)

# Decision: pursue only positive-value targets
pursue = new_churned[new_churned['winback_value'] > 0]
skip = new_churned[new_churned['winback_value'] <= 0]

print(f"Pursue: {len(pursue)} ({len(pursue)/len(new_churned)*100:.0f}%)")
print(f"Skip:   {len(skip)} ({len(skip)/len(new_churned)*100:.0f}%)")
print(f"Total expected value: ${pursue['winback_value'].sum():,.0f}")
