Subscription Tier Optimization: Designing Price Fences

In modern SaaS, Streaming, and Telecom, you rarely sell just one product. You sell a tiered architecture (Good-Better-Best). This week, we deploy clustering algorithms on conjoint utilities to design feature packages and price fences that mathematically force tier migration without triggering cannibalization.

Part 1: The Good-Better-Best Dilemma

You are the Pricing Strategist for a B2B SaaS company. Currently, you have a "Pro" tier at $49/mo and an "Enterprise" tier at $499/mo. 92% of your customers sit in the Pro tier. Your sales team complains that Enterprise is "too expensive," but your product data shows that hundreds of Pro users are utilizing the platform heavily across entire departments.

You have a tiering problem. You are leaving money on the table because your "Pro" tier is too generous, and your leap to "Enterprise" is too steep.

The Packaging Failure

Most companies design tiers by sitting in a conference room and guessing: "Let's put the basic features in Tier 1, the advanced features in Tier 2, and SSO in Tier 3." This is product-centric pricing, not customer-centric pricing. Optimal tiering requires Feature-Value Mapping: identifying clusters of customers with distinctly different willingness-to-pay (WTP) profiles, and building tiers explicitly designed to capture the maximum surplus from each cluster.

Part 2: The Architecture of a Price Fence

A Price Fence is a structural barrier designed to prevent a customer who is willing to pay a higher price from buying the cheaper tier. To make tiered pricing work, your fences must be uncomfortably tight for high-value users, but practically invisible to low-value users.

Types of Price Fences in Subscriptions

The Cannibalization Trap

If you include a feature in the "Good" tier that highly sophisticated users desperately need, those users will buy the "Good" tier, cannibalizing your "Best" tier revenue. Fencing is an exercise in intentional deprivation. You must restrict high-value features from lower tiers, even if they cost you $0 to provide.

Part 3: The Incentive Compatibility Constraint

To predict Tier Migration, we must model consumer choice behavior. A rational user evaluates a pricing page by calculating their Consumer Surplus for each tier.

CONSUMER SURPLUS
$$Surplus_{Tier} = Utility(Features_{Tier}) - Price_{Tier}$$

For a high-value "Enterprise" customer to actually buy the Enterprise tier, two mathematical conditions must hold:

  1. Individual Rationality: $Surplus_{Enterprise} \ge 0$. (The tier must be worth more than it costs).
  2. Incentive Compatibility (IC): $Surplus_{Enterprise} \ge Surplus_{Pro}$. (They must get a *better deal* by upgrading than by settling for the lower tier).

If $Surplus_{Pro} > Surplus_{Enterprise}$ for a corporate buyer, they will downgrade to Pro. Your price fence has failed.

Part 4: Conjoint + Clustering

How do we find the optimal packages? We return to Week 16's Choice-Based Conjoint analysis. Conjoint gives us individual-level part-worth utilities. But averaging utilities across 10,000 users results in a "mushy middle" product that nobody loves.

Instead, we apply K-Means Clustering to the conjoint utility scores. This is Elasticity Segmentation.

The Workflow

  1. Extract the individual part-worth utility matrix from your conjoint study.
  2. Run K-Means clustering (testing K=2, 3, 4) to group users with similar feature preferences.
  3. Profile the clusters. You will inevitably find natural segments (e.g., "The Solopreneur", "The Data Geek", "The Corporate IT Buyer").
  4. Map the tiers 1-to-1 with the clusters. Design the "Good" tier perfectly for Cluster 1, the "Better" tier for Cluster 2, and the "Best" tier for Cluster 3.

Part 6: Implementation in Python

Here we use `scikit-learn` to perform K-Means clustering on a matrix of conjoint part-worth utilities, identifying the optimal feature-value segments.

Python · scikit-learn
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
from sklearn.cluster import KMeans
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler

# ============================================
# STEP 1: Load Individual Conjoint Utilities
# ============================================
# Rows = Users, Columns = WTP ($) for specific features
df_utilities = pd.read_csv('conjoint_part_worths.csv')
features = ['core_app_wtp', 'api_access_wtp', 'sso_wtp', 'priority_support_wtp']

X = df_utilities[features]

# Standardize the data for K-Means (Crucial step)
scaler = StandardScaler()
X_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X)

# ============================================
# STEP 2: Elasticity Segmentation via K-Means
# ============================================
# We aim to design a Good-Better-Best architecture (K=3)
kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=3, random_state=42, n_init=10)
df_utilities['Tier_Segment'] = kmeans.fit_predict(X_scaled)

# ============================================
# STEP 3: Profile the Segments to Design Fences
# ============================================
# Look at the mean WTP for each feature within each cluster
segment_profiles = df_utilities.groupby('Tier_Segment')[features].mean()
segment_sizes = df_utilities.groupby('Tier_Segment').size()

print("--- Cluster WTP Profiles ($) ---")
print(segment_profiles.round(2))
print("\n--- Cluster Sizes ---")
print(segment_sizes)

# Interpretation Strategy:
# If Segment 2 has highly elevated WTP for 'sso_wtp', 
# SSO becomes the primary hard fence for the "Best" tier.

Part 7: Your Week 25 Playbook

Strategic QuestionAnalytical Approach
"What features go in which tier?"Run Conjoint + Clustering. Features with high variance in utility across segments make the best fences.
"Why aren't people upgrading to Pro?"Check Incentive Compatibility. Your Basic tier likely offers too much surplus. You must degrade the Basic tier (tighten the fence).
"How many tiers should we have?"Evaluate the sum of squared errors (Elbow method) in K-Means. Typically, 3 or 4 tiers capture 90% of available consumer surplus without causing choice paralysis.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Top Tiering Errors

1. The "Kitchen Sink" Top Tier. Throwing every feature into the top tier increases the cost to serve and dilutes messaging. Tiers should be tailored to specific buyer personas (clusters), not just "more stuff."

2. Fencing Core Value. Never put the core value proposition of your product behind a paywall in a freemium or entry tier. Fence the scaling vectors (usage, admin rights, integrations), not the "Aha!" moment.
