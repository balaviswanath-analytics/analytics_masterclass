Week 19 · Q2: Pricing, Promotion & Revenue Optimization

Why Your Discount Strategy Is Bleeding Profit

A retail chain ran a 20% off promotion and celebrated a 35% revenue lift. But what they didn't measure: 60% of discounted purchases came from customers who would have bought at full price anyway. The true incremental lift was 14%, and after accounting for the 20% margin sacrifice, the promotion actually lost money. This week introduces the causal framework for separating real from phantom lift.

Part 1: The $47M Phantom Lift

In Q4 2019, a mid-market e-commerce retailer ran a sitewide 20% discount for 10 days. Marketing reported a 35% lift in revenue compared to the same period the prior year: $182 million versus $135 million, a gain of $47 million. The CMO presented this as one of the most successful promotions in company history. Bonuses were paid. The promotion was added to the permanent Q4 calendar.

Eighteen months later, the new Head of Analytics rebuilt the analysis using propensity score matching. She matched every customer who purchased during the promotion to a statistically identical customer who didn't receive the promotional email (the company had accidentally held out 8% of its list due to a segmentation error — a natural experiment). The matched comparison revealed that the control group's purchase rate was already 21% higher than the prior year baseline, driven by organic growth, seasonal trends, and a new product launch that coincided with the promotion window.

The true incremental lift attributable to the promotion was 14%, not 35%. But the margin impact was devastating. On the 60% of purchases that would have happened anyway (the "Sure Things"), the company gave away 20% of margin for nothing. On the remaining 40% of purchases that were genuinely incremental, the net margin after the discount was thin. After accounting for the $6.2 million in margin sacrificed on Sure Things, the promotion's true net contribution was −$1.8 million. The most "successful" promotion in company history had actually lost money.

This is the central problem of promotional analytics: naive before/after or treated/untreated comparisons systematically overstate promotional effectiveness because they attribute organic growth, secular trends, and selection bias to the promotion itself. Two methodological frameworks solve this: propensity score matching (for retrospective analysis of past promotions) and uplift modeling (for prospective targeting of future promotions).

The Math That Kills Most Promotions

Suppose your promotion costs 20% of margin. If 50% of promoted customers are Sure Things who would have purchased anyway, you've sacrificed 10% of total margin on half your customer base just to reach the Persuadables in the other half. For the promotion to break even, the Persuadable segment must generate enough incremental profit (net of the 20% discount) to cover the entire margin giveaway to Sure Things. With typical Persuadable rates of 5–15%, most blanket promotions fail this arithmetic. The solution isn't to stop promoting — it's to stop promoting everyone.

Part 2: The Rubin Causal Model — Why This Is a Causal Inference Problem

Donald Rubin's potential outcomes framework (formalized in his 1974 paper and expanded with Paul Holland in 1986) provides the mathematical language for what we're trying to measure. For each customer i, there are two potential outcomes:

POTENTIAL OUTCOMES
Yi(1) = outcome if customer i receives the promotion
Yi(0) = outcome if customer i does NOT receive the promotion
The individual treatment effect: τi = Yi(1) − Yi(0)

The fundamental problem of causal inference is that we can only ever observe one of these outcomes for each customer. A customer either received the promotion or didn't — we can never see what would have happened in the alternative universe. This is why naive comparisons fail: the customers who received the promotion are systematically different from those who didn't (marketing targets its best customers, or self-selects engaged users), so comparing their outcomes confounds the treatment effect with selection bias.

THE QUANTITY WE WANT: AVERAGE TREATMENT EFFECT ON THE TREATED
ATT = E[Y(1) − Y(0) | T = 1] = E[Y(1) | T = 1] − E[Y(0) | T = 1]
The second term E[Y(0) | T = 1] is counterfactual: what would treated customers have done without treatment?

The naive comparison estimates E[Y(1) | T = 1] − E[Y(0) | T = 0], which introduces selection bias equal to E[Y(0) | T = 1] − E[Y(0) | T = 0]. In words: the difference in outcomes between treated and untreated groups that exists even without the treatment. If marketing targeted its most engaged customers (as it usually does), this bias is positive, and the naive estimate overstates the true ATT.

The Selection Bias Decomposition

Naive estimate = ATT + Selection Bias. In the e-commerce example above, the naive estimate was 35% lift. The true ATT was 14%. Therefore, selection bias was 21 percentage points — three-fifths of the reported result was phantom. This is typical. In a meta-analysis of 43 promotional studies by Gordon et al. (2019), the median selection bias was 50–80% of the reported effect. Most companies don't know this because they've never run the matching analysis.

Part 3: Propensity Score Matching (PSM)

Rosenbaum and Rubin (1983) proved a remarkable result: if you match on the propensity score — the probability of receiving treatment given observed covariates — the distribution of covariates is balanced between treated and control groups. This means matching on a single number (the propensity score) achieves the same covariate balance as matching on all covariates simultaneously.

PROPENSITY SCORE
e(Xi) = P(Ti = 1 | Xi)
Estimated via logistic regression on pre-treatment covariates X (recency, frequency, monetary, tenure, segment)

The Matching Algorithm

For each treated customer, find the untreated customer with the closest propensity score. "Closest" can be operationalized in several ways:

MethodHow It WorksProsCons
1:1 Nearest NeighborEach treated matched to closest control (with replacement or without)Simple, transparentSome matches may be poor if propensity scores don't overlap well
Caliper MatchingOnly match within a maximum distance (typically 0.2 × SD of logit propensity)Prevents bad matchesDrops treated units without close matches (bias-variance tradeoff)
Kernel MatchingUse weighted average of all controls, weighted by propensity distanceUses all data, more efficientLess intuitive, harder to check balance
StratificationDivide propensity score into 5–10 strata, compare within strataSimple, robustCoarser than exact matching

Balance Checks — Did Matching Actually Work?

Matching is only as good as its covariate balance. You must verify that after matching, the treated and control groups are statistically indistinguishable on all pre-treatment covariates. The standard diagnostic is the Standardized Mean Difference (SMD):

STANDARDIZED MEAN DIFFERENCE
SMD = (&bar;Xtreated − &bar;Xcontrol) / √[(s2treated + s2control) / 2]
Threshold: |SMD| < 0.1 for all covariates (Rosenbaum & Rubin guideline)

If any covariate has |SMD| > 0.1 after matching, the matching has failed for that variable. Common fixes: add interaction terms or nonlinear transformations to the propensity model, use a tighter caliper, or switch to kernel matching. If SMD exceeds 0.25 for multiple covariates, the overlap assumption is likely violated and PSM results should not be trusted.

Additional Diagnostics

Variance ratio: should be between 0.5 and 2.0 for each covariate post-matching. Propensity score overlap plot: the distributions of e(X) for treated and control should overlap substantially. If there's a region where only treated (or only control) customers exist, trim that region (the "common support" restriction). Love plot: visual display of SMD before and after matching for all covariates — expect dramatic improvement.

Part 4: Uplift Modeling — From Average to Individual Treatment Effects

PSM gives you the average treatment effect. Uplift modeling goes further: it estimates the individual-level treatment effect τi = Yi(1) − Yi(0) for each customer. This is what enables targeting: promote only the Persuadables, skip the Sure Things and Lost Causes, and absolutely avoid the Sleeping Dogs.

The term "uplift modeling" was coined by Radcliffe and Surry (1999) at Stochastic Solutions. It's also known as "heterogeneous treatment effect estimation" in the econometrics literature (Athey & Imbens, 2016), "incremental modeling" in marketing analytics, and "individual treatment effect estimation" in the causal ML community. The methods differ in sophistication but share the same objective: rank customers by expected responsiveness to the intervention.

Part 5: T-Learner Deep Dive — The Workhorse of Uplift

The T-Learner is the most widely used uplift architecture in industry because it's simple, flexible, and easy to audit. The procedure:

Step 1: Split your historical data into treatment group (customers who received the promotion) and control group (customers who didn't). Step 2: Train a classification model on each group separately, predicting the outcome (purchased / didn't purchase). Use the same features for both models. Step 3: For any customer, predict their purchase probability under both scenarios: &hat;P(buy | X, T=1) from the treatment model and &hat;P(buy | X, T=0) from the control model. Step 4: The uplift score is the difference: &hat;τ(X) = &hat;P(buy | X, T=1) − &hat;P(buy | X, T=0).

T-LEARNER UPLIFT SCORE
&hat;τ(Xi) = &hat;μ1(Xi) − &hat;μ0(Xi)
&hat;μ1 = prediction from treatment model | &hat;μ0 = prediction from control model

Interpreting the Uplift Score

Uplift ScoreCustomer SegmentAction
&hat;τ > 0.05Persuadable (promotion moves them from no-buy to buy)Promote aggressively — these are your ROI generators
0 ≤ &hat;τ ≤ 0.05Mild Persuadable / Lost Cause (promotion barely moves the needle)Promote only if margin permits; borderline ROI
&hat;τ ≈ 0Sure Thing (high P(buy) under both T=1 and T=0)Do NOT promote — they'll buy anyway; save your margin
&hat;τ < 0Sleeping Dog (promotion triggers negative response)Never promote — the discount reminds them to churn or switch
Ascarza's "Retention Futility" Finding (2018)

Eva Ascarza's landmark 2018 Journal of Marketing Research paper demonstrated that Sleeping Dogs are not a theoretical curiosity — they're widespread and commercially significant. Studying a telecom company's retention campaigns, she found that 10–14% of contacted customers had higher churn rates after receiving a retention offer compared to no-contact controls. The retention offer literally reminded at-risk customers that their contract was up, prompting them to shop competitors. Without uplift modeling, this iatrogenic effect is invisible — it gets averaged away in the aggregate ATT calculation.

The Uplift Curve and AUUC

The uplift equivalent of the ROC curve is the cumulative uplift curve. Sort all customers by predicted uplift (highest to lowest). At each targeting threshold k%, compute the cumulative incremental conversion in the top k%. Plot this against k%. The curve should rise steeply at first (you're targeting Persuadables), plateau in the middle (Sure Things and Lost Causes), and may decline at the tail (Sleeping Dogs).

The Area Under the Uplift Curve (AUUC) summarizes model quality. A perfect model captures all Persuadables first and all Sleeping Dogs last. A random model produces a straight line. The AUUC measures how much better your targeting is than random assignment. In practice, good uplift models achieve AUUC improvements of 20–50% over random targeting, which translates to 2–5x better promotion ROI.

Part 6: Implementation in Python

Python · Propensity Score Matching
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.neighbors import NearestNeighbors
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# =============================================
# STEP 1: Load Promotion Data
# =============================================
# Simulate: 10,000 customers, 40% treated
np.random.seed(42)
n = 10000
df = pd.DataFrame({
    'recency': np.random.exponential(30, n),
    'frequency': np.random.poisson(5, n),
    'monetary': np.random.lognormal(4, 0.8, n),
    'tenure': np.random.normal(24, 12, n).clip(1),
})
# Selection bias: marketing targets high-value customers
treat_logit = -1 + 0.3 * df['frequency'] + 0.005 * df['monetary']
df['treated'] = (np.random.rand(n) < 1/(1+np.exp(-treat_logit))).astype(int)

# True outcome: treatment effect = 5% for Persuadables only
base_buy = -2 + 0.15 * df['frequency'] + 0.002 * df['monetary'] - 0.01 * df['recency']
true_effect = 0.5 * (df['frequency'] < 5)  # only low-frequency respond
outcome_logit = base_buy + true_effect * df['treated']
df['purchased'] = (np.random.rand(n) < 1/(1+np.exp(-outcome_logit))).astype(int)

# =============================================
# STEP 2: Propensity Score Estimation
# =============================================
features = ['recency', 'frequency', 'monetary', 'tenure']
scaler = StandardScaler()
X_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(df[features])

ps_model = LogisticRegression(max_iter=1000)
ps_model.fit(X_scaled, df['treated'])
df['propensity'] = ps_model.predict_proba(X_scaled)[:, 1]

# =============================================
# STEP 3: Nearest Neighbor Matching with Caliper
# =============================================
treated = df[df['treated'] == 1]
control = df[df['treated'] == 0]

nn = NearestNeighbors(n_neighbors=1, metric='euclidean')
nn.fit(control[['propensity']])
distances, indices = nn.kneighbors(treated[['propensity']])

# Caliper: drop matches with distance > 0.05
caliper = 0.05
good_match = distances.flatten() < caliper
matched_treated = treated[good_match]
matched_control = control.iloc[indices[good_match].flatten()]

print(f"Matched: {len(matched_treated):,} of {len(treated):,} treated")
print(f"Dropped (outside caliper): {len(treated) - len(matched_treated):,}")

# =============================================
# STEP 4: Balance Check (SMD)
# =============================================
print("\\n=== Covariate Balance (SMD) ===")
for feat in features:
    smd_before = (treated[feat].mean() - control[feat].mean()) / \
                 np.sqrt((treated[feat].var() + control[feat].var()) / 2)
    smd_after = (matched_treated[feat].mean() - matched_control[feat].values.mean()) / \
                np.sqrt((matched_treated[feat].var() + matched_control[feat].values.var()) / 2)
    status = 'PASS' if abs(smd_after) < 0.1 else 'FAIL'
    print(f"  {feat:12s}  Before: {smd_before:+.3f}  After: {smd_after:+.3f}  [{status}]")

# =============================================
# STEP 5: ATT Estimation
# =============================================
naive = treated['purchased'].mean() - control['purchased'].mean()
att = matched_treated['purchased'].mean() - matched_control['purchased'].values.mean()
bias = naive - att
print(f"\\n=== Treatment Effect ===")
print(f"Naive difference:  {naive:.3f}")
print(f"PSM-adjusted ATT:  {att:.3f}")
print(f"Selection bias:    {bias:.3f} ({bias/naive*100:.0f}% of naive estimate)")
Python · T-Learner Uplift Model + Targeting
from sklearn.ensemble import GradientBoostingClassifier
from sklearn.model_selection import cross_val_predict

# =============================================
# T-LEARNER: Separate Treatment/Control Models
# =============================================
model_t = GradientBoostingClassifier(n_estimators=200, max_depth=4,
                                     learning_rate=0.1, random_state=42)
model_c = GradientBoostingClassifier(n_estimators=200, max_depth=4,
                                     learning_rate=0.1, random_state=42)
model_t.fit(treated[features], treated['purchased'])
model_c.fit(control[features], control['purchased'])

# Predict uplift for ALL customers
df['p_treat'] = model_t.predict_proba(df[features])[:, 1]
df['p_control'] = model_c.predict_proba(df[features])[:, 1]
df['uplift'] = df['p_treat'] - df['p_control']

# Classify into segments
def classify(row):
    if row['uplift'] > 0.05: return 'Persuadable'
    elif row['uplift'] < -0.01: return 'Sleeping Dog'
    elif row['p_control'] > 0.5: return 'Sure Thing'
    else: return 'Lost Cause'
df['segment'] = df.apply(classify, axis=1)

print("=== Customer Segments ===")
print(df['segment'].value_counts())
print(f"\\nPersuadable mean uplift: {df.loc[df['segment']=='Persuadable', 'uplift'].mean():.3f}")
print(f"Sleeping Dog mean uplift: {df.loc[df['segment']=='Sleeping Dog', 'uplift'].mean():.3f}")

# =============================================
# CUMULATIVE UPLIFT CURVE
# =============================================
df_sorted = df.sort_values('uplift', ascending=False)
df_sorted['cum_uplift'] = df_sorted['uplift'].cumsum()
df_sorted['pct_targeted'] = np.arange(1, len(df_sorted)+1) / len(df_sorted) * 100

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 6))
ax.plot(df_sorted['pct_targeted'], df_sorted['cum_uplift'],
        color='#264653', linewidth=2)
peak_idx = df_sorted['cum_uplift'].idxmax()
peak_pct = df_sorted.loc[peak_idx, 'pct_targeted']
ax.axvline(x=peak_pct, color='#c45d3e', linestyle='--',
           label=f'Optimal threshold: {peak_pct:.0f}%')
ax.set_title('Cumulative Uplift Curve', fontsize=14, fontweight='bold')
ax.set_xlabel('% of Customers Targeted (ordered by uplift)')
ax.set_ylabel('Cumulative Incremental Conversions')
ax.legend()
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
print(f"\\nOptimal: target top {peak_pct:.0f}% for maximum incremental lift")
