Week 09 · Q1: Customer Lifetime & Retention Economics

The Platinum Handcuffs: Measuring Switching Costs

Some customers stay not because they love you, but because leaving is painful. Contracts, data lock-in, integration complexity, and retraining costs create invisible barriers to churn. This week you'll learn to measure these switching costs statistically, understand how contract terms modify hazard functions, and design strategies that convert forced retention into genuine loyalty.

Part 1: Lock-In Is Not Loyalty

A telecom company reports 92% retention among contract customers versus 65% among month-to-month. The VP of Sales declares contracts the silver bullet. But what happens at contract expiration? The 90-day window around renewal shows a hazard spike 4-6x higher than mid-contract periods. Those "retained" customers weren't loyal — they were locked in. And the moment the handcuffs come off, they bolt.

The Renewal Cliff (Revisited from Week 2)

In contractual businesses, the hazard function is not smooth. It exhibits periodic spikes at contract boundaries — 12 months, 24 months, 36 months. Between these points, churn is artificially suppressed by early termination fees. At these points, churn is artificially elevated because customers who wanted to leave earlier now can. Standard Cox PH models that assume smooth, proportional hazards will misestimate risk for contract customers.

Part 2: Quantifying Switching Costs with Survival Data

You can't directly observe switching costs in most datasets. But you can infer them by comparing churn behavior of customers with different lock-in characteristics. The approach: use contract terms, integration depth, and data volume as proxies for switching costs, then measure their effect on the hazard function.

Proxy Variables for Switching Cost

The Integration Threshold

Analysis of a B2B SaaS company found a clear step-function in churn risk by API integration count: 0-1 integrations had 30% annual churn, 2-4 integrations had 12% annual churn, and 5+ integrations had just 4% annual churn. The effect was far stronger than contract length, NPS score, or any other predictor. The lesson: product-driven lock-in is more durable than contractual lock-in because it creates genuine switching costs that persist beyond any contract term.

Part 3: The Contract Hazard Function

For contractual customers, the hazard function has a distinctive shape: near-zero during the contract term (only involuntary churn from bankruptcy or extreme dissatisfaction willing to pay the penalty), followed by a massive spike at the renewal window, then rapid decline for those who renew.

THE BATHTUB CURVE IN CONTRACTS
h(t) = hbase(t) · exp(β'X) · φ(t, contract)
φ(t, contract) = renewal window indicator · amplification factor

This violates the proportional hazards assumption (Week 2). Two solutions: stratified Cox by contract type, or time-varying covariates that track "months to next renewal."

The Time-to-Renewal Covariate

Create a time-varying covariate months_to_renewal that counts down from 12 (or 24) to 0. As this variable approaches zero, the hazard should spike. Include it in your Cox model alongside other covariates. This elegantly captures the renewal cliff without violating proportional hazards, because the covariate itself is changing over time.

Part 4: Stratified Cox for Contract Types

When you have fundamentally different contract structures (month-to-month vs. annual vs. multi-year), the baseline hazard shape is so different across groups that a single Cox model is inappropriate. Stratified Cox allows each contract type to have its own baseline hazard while sharing the same covariate effects.

STRATIFIED COX MODEL
hg(t | X) = h0g(t) · exp(β'X)   for each stratum g
Each contract type g gets its own h0g(t) but shares β
Strategic Implication

If the stratified model shows that covariate effects (β) are consistent across strata but baseline hazards differ wildly, then contracts are modifying the timing of churn but not the drivers. Same things cause churn regardless of contract type — contracts just delay the inevitable. This means your retention strategy should be identical across contract types; only the urgency timeline differs.

Part 5: Implementation in Python

Python · Contract Hazard Analysis
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
from lifelines import CoxPHFitter, KaplanMeierFitter
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

df = pd.read_csv('contract_customers.csv')

# === Stratified Cox by contract type ===
cph = CoxPHFitter(penalizer=0.01)
covs = ['monthly_charges', 'api_integrations',
        'data_volume_gb', 'trained_users', 'nps_score']
cph.fit(
    df[covs + ['tenure', 'churned', 'contract_type']],
    duration_col='tenure', event_col='churned',
    strata=['contract_type']  # separate baselines
)
cph.print_summary()

# === KM curves by contract type ===
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10,6))
for ctype in df['contract_type'].unique():
    mask = df['contract_type'] == ctype
    kmf = KaplanMeierFitter()
    kmf.fit(df.loc[mask, 'tenure'],
           df.loc[mask, 'churned'], label=ctype)
    kmf.plot_survival_function(ax=ax)
ax.set_title('Survival by Contract Type')
plt.show()

# === Switching Cost Score ===
df['switching_cost_score'] = (
    df['api_integrations'].rank(pct=True) * 0.3 +
    df['data_volume_gb'].rank(pct=True) * 0.3 +
    df['trained_users'].rank(pct=True) * 0.2 +
    df['contract_months_remaining'].rank(pct=True) * 0.2
)
print("Churn rate by switching cost quintile:")
print(df.groupby(pd.qcut(df['switching_cost_score'],5))['churned'].mean())
