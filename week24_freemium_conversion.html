Freemium Conversion: When Free Becomes Paid

Freemium is an acquisition strategy, not a revenue model. The central challenge for SaaS, gaming, and media companies is predicting exactly who will cross the threshold into becoming a paying user. This week, we deploy Logistic Regression to build a Conversion Propensity model based on feature usage and time.

Part 1: The Business Problem

You are the Head of Growth for a B2B SaaS platform. You generate 10,000 free signups a month, but your conversion rate is hovering around 3%. Your sales team is complaining that they are wasting hours calling free users who will never buy, while highly-engaged free users are slipping through the cracks without a sales touch.

You need to transition from a "spray and pray" monetization strategy to a targeted Product-Qualified Lead (PQL) system. To do this, you must quantify exactly which feature interactions predict a user's willingness to pay.

The $5M Propensity Insight

A major streaming service realized that users who watched a show on two different devices (e.g., TV and mobile) within their first 7 days were 4x more likely to convert to a paid subscription than users who watched twice as many hours on a single device. By shifting their onboarding emails to explicitly encourage multi-device login, they increased their freemium conversion rate by 18%, driving $5M in net new ARR.

Part 2: Overcoming "The Penny Gap"

In 2007, venture capitalist Josh Kopelman coined the term "The Penny Gap." He observed that the hardest gap to cross in pricing isn't getting a customer to pay $100 instead of $10; it's getting them to pay $0.01 instead of $0.00. The transition from a non-paying user to a paying user requires a massive psychological leap.

Feature Usage Patterns vs. Vanity Metrics

To predict who will cross this gap, you must look at Feature Usage Patterns. Many analysts use vanity metrics like "Total Logins" or "Time in App." These are often terrible predictors of conversion because they fail to distinguish between habitual free-tier usage and actual intent to purchase.

Metric TypeExamplesPredictive Power
Vanity ActivityTotal Logins, Days Active, PageviewsLow. Often indicates users who are perfectly content with the free tier.
Network EffectsInvites sent, shared links generated, collaborative editsHigh. When users bring their team, switching costs rise exponentially.
Friction/Paywall HitsClicks on premium features, storage limit warningsVery High. Explicit signals of intent and constrained utility.

Part 3: Logistic Regression for Conversion Propensity

To separate the signal from the noise, we use Logistic Regression. Unlike linear regression, which predicts a continuous number, logistic regression predicts the probability of a binary outcome (Convert: 1, Do Not Convert: 0).

The Math of Log-Odds

Logistic regression works by mapping any linear combination of features to a probability between 0 and 1 using the sigmoid function.

THE LOGISTIC FUNCTION
$$P(Convert=1|X) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + ... + \beta_k X_k)}}$$

The true power of Logistic Regression lies in interpreting its coefficients via the Odds Ratio. If we exponentiate a coefficient ($e^{\beta_1}$), we get the multiplier effect of that feature on the odds of converting.

ODDS RATIO INTERPRETATION
$$OR = e^{\beta_j}$$
If OR = 1.5, a one-unit increase in the feature increases the odds of conversion by 50%.
The Data Leakage Trap

The most common mistake when building conversion models is Data Leakage. If you include a feature like "Credit Card Added" or "Viewed Billing Page" in your training data, your model will look highly predictive, but it is useless in practice. You are using the outcome to predict the outcome. You must build your model using only feature data from a specific observation window (e.g., the first 14 days of free usage) to predict conversion in the subsequent period.

Part 5: Implementation in Python

We use `statsmodels` rather than `scikit-learn` for this implementation because we prioritize inference (understanding exactly why users convert via p-values and odds ratios) over pure prediction. We are analyzing a dataset containing a 14-day observation window.

Python · statsmodels
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import statsmodels.api as sm

# ============================================
# STEP 1: Load 14-Day Observation Data
# ============================================
# Data contains features measured ONLY during days 1-14.
# Target variable is 'converted_within_90_days' (1 or 0).
df = pd.read_csv('saas_freemium_users.csv')

# Define features
features = [
    'days_active_first_14d', 
    'team_invites_sent', 
    'premium_paywall_hits', 
    'core_actions_completed'
]

X = df[features]
y = df['converted_within_90_days']

# ============================================
# STEP 2: Fit Logistic Regression
# ============================================
# statsmodels requires manually adding a constant for the intercept
X_const = sm.add_constant(X)

# Fit the Logit model
logit_model = sm.Logit(y, X_const).fit(disp=0)
print(logit_model.summary())

# ============================================
# STEP 3: Calculate and Interpret Odds Ratios
# ============================================
# Exponentiate the coefficients to get Odds Ratios
odds_ratios = np.exp(logit_model.params)
conf_int = np.exp(logit_model.conf_int())

results_df = pd.DataFrame({
    'Odds_Ratio': odds_ratios,
    'Lower_CI': conf_int[0],
    'Upper_CI': conf_int[1],
    'P_Value': logit_model.pvalues
})

print("\nFeature Impact on Conversion Odds:")
print(results_df.round(3))

# ============================================
# STEP 4: Generate PQL Propensity Scores
# ============================================
# Predict the probability of conversion for all users
df['propensity_score'] = logit_model.predict(X_const)

# Route leads to Sales if probability > 60%
df['is_pql'] = np.where(df['propensity_score'] > 0.60, 1, 0)

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Top Errors in Conversion Modeling

1. Class Imbalance Ignorance. If your conversion rate is 1%, your model can achieve 99% accuracy simply by predicting "0" for everyone. You must evaluate your model using Precision, Recall, and AUC-ROC, not raw accuracy.

2. Blurring the Observation Window. Ensure absolute strictness on time windows. If you are predicting conversion within 90 days based on the first 14 days of usage, discard any user who hasn't been in the system for at least 14 days, and do not include any activity from day 15 onward.
