Week 18 · Q2: Pricing, Promotion & Revenue Optimization

Bundling Math: When 1+1 = 3 (And When It Doesn't)

A streaming service bundles music + video + podcasts for $15.99, less than the $22.97 total if purchased separately. Are they leaving money on the table? Not if the valuations are negatively correlated. But if power users value everything highly and casual users value nothing, bundling just shifts revenue downward. This week you'll learn the mathematical conditions under which bundling creates value, how to identify natural bundles from transaction data, and how to price them optimally.

Part 1: The $2.3B Bundling Mistake

In 2018, a major US telecom launched an aggressive triple-play bundle: internet + TV + phone for $89.99/month, a 28% discount from the $124.97 sum of individual prices. The strategy team projected that the lower price point would attract 1.2 million new triple-play subscribers within 18 months. The volume uplift, they argued, would more than compensate for the per-subscriber revenue decline.

What actually happened was more painful. Within the first year, 840,000 customers adopted the bundle. But forensic analysis revealed the composition: only 180,000 were genuinely new triple-play customers. The remaining 660,000 were existing subscribers who had previously been paying full price for all three services individually. They simply switched to the cheaper bundle. The company had given a 28% discount to its most loyal, highest-value customers — the exact opposite of what good pricing strategy demands.

The financial damage was staggering. Those 660,000 existing customers each lost $35/month in revenue ($124.97 − $89.99), totaling $277 million per year. The 180,000 new customers generated only $194 million in new revenue. Net annual impact: −$83 million. Compounded over the three-year contract cycle, the total revenue destruction approached $2.3 billion. The bundle was technically "successful" by volume metrics but catastrophic by economic metrics.

The root cause was a failure to answer two analytical questions before launch: (1) What percentage of expected bundle buyers are already buying all components at full price? If this cannibalization rate exceeds ~30%, the bundle will almost certainly destroy more revenue than it creates. (2) Are customer valuations of the component products negatively correlated? If they're positively correlated (people who value internet highly also value TV highly), bundling concentrates the demand distribution in the wrong direction.

The Pre-Flight Checklist Every Bundle Needs

Before launching any bundle, you need three numbers: (a) the cross-buy rate — what percentage of your customer base currently buys both products at full price, (b) the valuation correlation — is spending on product A correlated with spending on product B, and (c) the break-even new subscriber count — how many genuinely new bundle buyers you need to offset the margin loss on cannibalized existing customers. If you can't produce these three numbers, you're not ready to launch a bundle.

Part 2: The Economics of Bundling

Adams-Yellen Framework (1976)

The foundational theory of bundling was formalized by William Adams and Janet Yellen in their 1976 Quarterly Journal of Economics paper "Commodity Bundling and the Burden of Monopoly." They established that a monopolist (or firm with market power) has three strategies available:

StrategyDefinitionWhen It Wins
Pure ComponentsSell products only separately, no bundle optionValuations positively correlated; high heterogeneity within products; marginal cost is high
Pure BundlingSell only the bundle — customers cannot buy components individuallyValuations negatively correlated; marginal cost near zero; homogeneous customer base
Mixed BundlingSell both the bundle and individual components at different pricesAlmost always dominates pure bundling; optimal when customer base is heterogeneous

Adams and Yellen's key insight was geometric: plot each customer as a point on a 2D plane where the axes represent their willingness-to-pay (WTP) for Product 1 and Product 2. The firm's pricing strategy creates "regions" on this plane — customers in different regions make different purchase decisions. The profit-maximizing strategy is the one that captures the most consumer surplus across all regions.

McAfee-McMillan-Whinston (1989): Mixed Bundling Almost Always Wins

Thirteen years after Adams-Yellen, McAfee, McMillan, and Whinston proved a striking result in Econometrica: mixed bundling weakly dominates both pure components and pure bundling for any demand distribution. The intuition is that mixed bundling gives the firm an extra degree of freedom. It can set three prices (p1, p2, pbundle) to capture four customer segments: buy-neither, buy-only-1, buy-only-2, buy-both. Pure components and pure bundling are just special cases. The only scenario where pure bundling ties (rather than loses to) mixed bundling is when marginal cost is exactly zero and valuations are perfectly negatively correlated — a theoretical curiosity, not a business reality.

Schmalensee's Gaussian Framework (1984)

Richard Schmalensee provided the first tractable model for when bundling is profitable by assuming customer valuations follow a bivariate normal (Gaussian) distribution. This matters because it gives us a closed-form relationship between the correlation of valuations and the profitability of bundling. In his 1984 Journal of Business paper, he showed that bundling is most profitable when the correlation coefficient ρ between valuations is strongly negative. As ρ approaches +1 (perfect positive correlation), the advantage of bundling vanishes entirely.

Why does negative correlation help? Consider the extreme case. Customer A values Product 1 at $100 and Product 2 at $20. Customer B values Product 1 at $20 and Product 2 at $100. If you sell separately, the profit-maximizing price for each product is $100 (capturing one customer each), for a total revenue of $200. But a bundle priced at $110 captures both customers (A values the bundle at $120, B at $120), for total revenue of $220. Bundling works because it "averages out" the heterogeneity — even though customers disagree about individual products, they agree about the bundle.

Part 3: Why Bundling Concentrates WTP — The Variance Proof

The mathematical engine that makes bundling work is a basic result from probability theory: the variance of a sum is less than or equal to the sum of variances, with equality only when variables are perfectly positively correlated.

VARIANCE OF THE BUNDLE WTP
Var(rbundle) = Var(r1) + Var(r2) + 2ρ · σ1σ2
ri = WTP for product i | ρ = correlation between valuations | σi = standard deviation of WTP for product i

Let's work through the implications with concrete numbers. Suppose:

ParameterProduct 1Product 2
Mean WTP$50$40
Std Dev of WTP$15$12

The mean bundle WTP is always $90 (the sum). But the variance depends entirely on ρ:

Correlation (ρ)Var(bundle)SD(bundle)CV(bundle)Bundling Value
+1.0729$27.0030.0%None — same dispersion as components
+0.5549$23.4326.0%Modest concentration
0.0369$19.2121.3%Good — 29% less dispersion than sum
−0.5189$13.7515.3%Excellent — 49% less dispersion
−1.09$3.003.3%Near-perfect — all customers value bundle equally

This is the key insight that Schmalensee formalized: when ρ is negative, bundling concentrates the WTP distribution. A tighter distribution means you can set a single bundle price that captures a larger share of customers than two separate prices can. At ρ = −1.0, the coefficient of variation drops to just 3.3% — almost every customer values the bundle at approximately $90, making it trivially easy to price.

The Law of Large Numbers Extension

Bakos and Brynjolfsson (1999) extended this logic to large bundles. As the number of products in a bundle grows (think Microsoft Office, Amazon Prime, cable TV packages), the law of large numbers kicks in: the per-product WTP converges to its mean regardless of individual product correlation. This is why large bundles are disproportionately powerful. With 20+ products, even mildly negative or zero correlation produces dramatic variance reduction, enabling the firm to capture nearly all consumer surplus with a single price.

Part 4: Market Basket Analysis — Finding Natural Bundles

Before optimizing bundle pricing, you need to know what to bundle. Theory tells you to look for negatively correlated valuations, but in practice you start with a simpler question: which products are frequently purchased together? Association rules mine transaction data to surface these natural product affinities.

The Three Metrics That Matter

MetricFormulaInterpretationGood Threshold
SupportP(A ∩ B) = count(A and B) / total transactionsHow frequently the pair appears together in the data> 1% (varies by catalog size)
ConfidenceP(B | A) = P(A ∩ B) / P(A)Given that A was purchased, what fraction also bought B?> 25%
LiftP(A ∩ B) / [P(A) · P(B)]How much more likely is the co-purchase vs. random chance?> 1.0 (ideally > 2.0)
Lift Is the Metric That Matters for Bundling

Support tells you items are popular, but Coca-Cola and bread both have high support without being a natural bundle. Confidence is directional — "customers who buy A also buy B" doesn't mean B buyers also buy A. Lift is the metric that identifies true complementarity: products bought together more often than random chance predicts. A lift of 3.5 means the pair co-occurs 3.5x more frequently than expected under independence. Anything above 2.0 is a strong bundle candidate. Below 1.0 means the products are actually substitutes (buying one reduces the probability of buying the other).

The Apriori Algorithm

Scanning all possible product pairs in a catalog of 10,000 SKUs means evaluating 50 million pairs. For triples, it's 166 billion combinations. The Apriori algorithm (Agrawal & Srikant, 1994) makes this computationally feasible by exploiting the anti-monotone property: if {A, B} is infrequent (below minimum support), then {A, B, C} must also be infrequent. This allows the algorithm to prune the search space aggressively, building from frequent singles to frequent pairs to frequent triples without ever evaluating combinations that can't possibly meet the support threshold.

The workflow: (1) set a minimum support threshold (typically 0.5–2% depending on catalog size), (2) find all individual items meeting the threshold, (3) generate candidate pairs from frequent items and filter, (4) generate candidate triples from frequent pairs and filter, (5) continue until no new frequent itemsets are found. In practice, meaningful bundles rarely exceed 3–4 products because customers don't buy many items simultaneously.

From Association Rules to Bundle Candidates

High-lift pairs identify products that co-occur frequently, but co-occurrence alone doesn't mean bundling them will create value. You need a second filter: does the pair show negatively correlated spending? The reason: if heavy spenders on Product A also spend heavily on Product B (positive correlation), bundling just gives your best customers a discount. If light spenders on A are heavy spenders on B (negative correlation), bundling captures customers who wouldn't buy both at full individual prices.

The complete filtering pipeline: (1) Apriori → pairs with lift > 2.0, (2) correlation analysis → pairs with ρ < 0.3 on per-customer spend, (3) cannibalization check → pairs with cross-buy rate < 30%. Pairs that pass all three filters are genuine bundle candidates.

Part 5: Conjoint-Based Bundle Pricing

Association rules tell you what to bundle; conjoint analysis (Week 16) tells you how to price the bundle. The approach: include "bundle" as a product configuration in your choice-based conjoint study, alongside individual components at various prices. The multinomial logit model then estimates the incremental WTP for the bundle relative to individual products.

ApproachData SourceWhat It AnswersLimitation
Association rulesHistorical transactionsWhat products naturally co-occur?Doesn't tell you about WTP or optimal price
Conjoint surveyStated preferenceHow much will customers pay for the bundle?Hypothetical bias (discount by 10–15%)
Correlation analysisCustomer-level spend dataAre valuations positively or negatively correlated?Requires granular spend data by product
Cannibalization modelPre/post bundle launch dataHow many bundle buyers would have bought both anyway?Only available retrospectively (or via conjoint)

The optimal bundle price lives in a narrow range. Too high, and you don't attract enough new customers to justify the bundle's existence. Too low, and you cannibalize full-price purchasers without adequate compensation. The conjoint approach lets you simulate the entire market at various bundle prices before launching, estimating both uptake and cannibalization for each price point.

BUNDLE RESERVATION PRICE
rbundle = r1 + r2 − δ
ri = WTP for product i | δ = complementarity discount (decreasing marginal utility of additional components)

The δ term captures the reality that a customer's WTP for the second product is typically lower when they already have the first. If internet already satisfies 80% of a customer's entertainment needs, their marginal WTP for TV is lower than if they had no internet. Conjoint studies estimate δ directly through the part-worth utilities of "bundle" vs. component configurations.

Part 6: The Cannibalization Test

This is where most bundling analyses fail. The projected revenue from a bundle has two sources: genuinely new customers who wouldn't have purchased both products, and existing customers who switch from full-price component purchases to the discounted bundle. The second group represents pure revenue destruction.

NET BUNDLE IMPACT
Πnet = Nnew · (pbundle − MC) − Ncannibal · (p1 + p2 − pbundle)
Nnew = genuinely new bundle buyers | Ncannibal = existing cross-buyers who switch | pbundle = bundle price

Let's work through a concrete example. Your company sells Internet ($50/mo) and TV ($40/mo) separately. You're considering a bundle at $75 (17% discount from the $90 sum).

ScenarioCustomersRevenue Impact
New bundle buyers (didn't have both before)2,000+$150,000/mo
Existing cross-buyers who switch to bundle5,000−$75,000/mo (lost $15/mo each)
Existing cross-buyers who stay at full price3,000$0 (no change)
Net monthly impact+$75,000/mo

In this scenario, the bundle is profitable — but only because the new-to-full-price ratio (2,000 new vs. 5,000 cannibalized) is favorable enough. If the cannibalized count had been 8,000 instead of 5,000 (a very common real-world scenario), the net impact would be −$30,000/mo.

The 30% Rule of Thumb

If more than 30% of your expected bundle buyers are currently purchasing both components at full price, the bundle will almost certainly destroy more revenue than it creates unless the discount is very small (<10%). This isn't a hard mathematical threshold — it depends on marginal cost and the discount depth — but empirically, 30% is the inflection point where cannibalization starts to dominate. Measure this number before you set the bundle price, not after launch.

Part 7: Implementation in Python

Python · Market Basket Analysis
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
from mlxtend.frequent_patterns import apriori, association_rules
from mlxtend.preprocessing import TransactionEncoder
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# =============================================
# STEP 1: Mine Transaction Data for Co-Purchases
# =============================================
# Each row: list of products in one transaction
transactions = [
    ['Internet', 'Phone'],
    ['Internet', 'TV', 'Phone'],
    ['Internet', 'TV'],
    ['Phone'],
    ['Internet', 'Phone', 'TV', 'Security'],
    ['Internet', 'TV', 'Security'],
    ['Phone', 'Security'],
    ['Internet'],
    ['Internet', 'Phone', 'TV'],
    ['TV', 'Security'],
    # ... in practice, thousands of rows
]

te = TransactionEncoder()
te_array = te.fit_transform(transactions)
basket = pd.DataFrame(te_array, columns=te.columns_)

# Find frequent itemsets (min 20% support for small demo)
freq_items = apriori(basket, min_support=0.20, use_colnames=True)
rules = association_rules(freq_items, metric='lift', min_threshold=1.0)

# Filter: lift > 1.5 for genuine complementarity
bundle_candidates = rules[rules['lift'] > 1.5].sort_values('lift', ascending=False)
print("=== Bundle Candidates (Lift > 1.5) ===")
print(bundle_candidates[['antecedents', 'consequents',
    'support', 'confidence', 'lift']].to_string())
Python · Valuation Correlation + Cannibalization
# =============================================
# STEP 2: Check Valuation Correlation
# =============================================
# Customer-level monthly spend by product
np.random.seed(42)
n_cust = 5000
customer_spend = pd.DataFrame({
    'customer_id': range(n_cust),
    'internet_spend': np.random.normal(50, 15, n_cust).clip(0),
    'tv_spend': np.random.normal(40, 12, n_cust).clip(0),
})
# Inject negative correlation (realistic for telecom)
customer_spend['tv_spend'] += -0.3 * (customer_spend['internet_spend'] - 50)

rho = customer_spend[['internet_spend', 'tv_spend']].corr().iloc[0,1]
print(f"Valuation correlation (rho): {rho:.3f}")
print(f"Bundling value: {'STRONG' if rho < -0.2 else 'MODERATE' if rho < 0.3 else 'WEAK'}")

# Variance reduction proof
var_1 = customer_spend['internet_spend'].var()
var_2 = customer_spend['tv_spend'].var()
customer_spend['bundle_wtp'] = customer_spend['internet_spend'] + customer_spend['tv_spend']
var_bundle = customer_spend['bundle_wtp'].var()
print(f"\nVar(Internet): {var_1:.0f}  |  Var(TV): {var_2:.0f}")
print(f"Sum of variances: {var_1 + var_2:.0f}")
print(f"Var(Bundle WTP):  {var_bundle:.0f}")
print(f"Variance reduction: {(1 - var_bundle/(var_1+var_2))*100:.1f}%")

# =============================================
# STEP 3: Cannibalization Analysis
# =============================================
buys_internet = customer_spend['internet_spend'] > 30
buys_tv = customer_spend['tv_spend'] > 25
cross_buy_rate = (buys_internet & buys_tv).mean()
print(f"\nCross-buy rate: {cross_buy_rate:.1%}")
print(f"Cannibalization risk: {'HIGH (>30%)' if cross_buy_rate > 0.30 else 'MODERATE' if cross_buy_rate > 0.15 else 'LOW'}")
Python · Bundle P&L Simulation
# =============================================
# STEP 4: Bundle Price Optimization via Grid Search
# =============================================
p_internet = 49.99
p_tv = 39.99
sum_components = p_internet + p_tv
MC = 20

# For each discount level, estimate new vs. cannibalized
results = []
for discount in np.arange(0.05, 0.36, 0.05):
    bundle_price = sum_components * (1 - discount)
    
    # New buyers: customers whose bundle WTP > bundle price
    # but individual WTP < individual prices (wouldn't buy both)
    new_mask = ((customer_spend['bundle_wtp'] >= bundle_price) &
                ~(buys_internet & buys_tv))
    n_new = new_mask.sum()
    
    # Cannibalized: already buying both, now switch to cheaper bundle
    cannibal_mask = ((customer_spend['bundle_wtp'] >= bundle_price) &
                     (buys_internet & buys_tv))
    n_cannibal = cannibal_mask.sum()
    
    revenue_new = n_new * (bundle_price - MC)
    revenue_lost = n_cannibal * (sum_components - bundle_price)
    net = revenue_new - revenue_lost
    
    results.append({'discount': discount, 'price': bundle_price,
                   'new': n_new, 'cannibalized': n_cannibal,
                   'net_monthly': net})

result_df = pd.DataFrame(results)
print("=== Bundle P&L by Discount Level ===")
print(result_df.to_string(index=False))

optimal = result_df.loc[result_df['net_monthly'].idxmax()]
print(f"\nOptimal: {optimal['discount']:.0%} discount at ${optimal['price']:.2f}")
print(f"Net monthly profit: ${optimal['net_monthly']:+,.0f}")
