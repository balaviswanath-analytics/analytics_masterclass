Week 11 · Q1: Customer Lifetime & Retention Economics

Contractual vs. Non-Contractual: Why Your CLV Model Choice Matters

The #1 mistake in CLV modeling isn't a technical error — it's applying the wrong model class entirely. A subscription business and an e-commerce business have fundamentally different data structures. Using BG/NBD (designed for non-contractual) on subscription data, or using a churn model (designed for contractual) on e-commerce data, produces garbage estimates. This week teaches you to classify your business correctly and pick the right model every time.

Part 1: The Most Common CLV Mistake

A subscription box company (contractual, continuous) spent 3 months building a BG/NBD model (designed for non-contractual, discrete settings). The model produced "P(Alive)" estimates for subscribers — but these subscribers were either active or cancelled. There was no ambiguity about their status. The BG/NBD model was solving a problem that didn't exist while ignoring the actual problem (predicting renewal at the next contract boundary). They scrapped 3 months of work and started over.

Why This Happens

Most CLV tutorials teach BG/NBD because it's elegant and well-documented. Analysts learn the tool and apply it everywhere, regardless of business context. But BG/NBD was specifically designed for non-contractual, continuous settings (like e-commerce) where you don't observe the moment of churn. Applying it to contractual settings where churn is observed is like using a metal detector to find a car parked in your driveway — the tool works perfectly, but you already know where the car is.

Part 2: Fader's 2x2 Framework

Peter Fader and Bruce Hardie proposed a classification framework based on two dimensions:

Continuous (any time)Discrete (specific opportunities)
Contractual (churn is observed)Subscriptions, SaaS, gym memberships, insurance. Customer actively cancels.Magazine subscriptions, annual memberships. Renewal decision at fixed points.
Non-Contractual (churn is unobserved)E-commerce, retail, restaurants, apps. Customer just stops showing up.Event attendance, seasonal purchases. Buy at periodic opportunities.
The Key Question

Ask yourself: "Do I know when a customer has churned?" If yes (they cancelled, didn't renew, closed their account), it's contractual. If no (they just haven't bought in a while, but might come back), it's non-contractual. This single question determines your entire modeling strategy.

Why the Distinction Matters for CLV

In contractual settings, the key modeling challenge is predicting the probability of renewal at the next contract boundary. You observe churn directly. The survival analysis tools from Weeks 1-2 (KM, Cox) apply naturally.

In non-contractual settings, the key challenge is determining whether a customer is still "alive" when you can't observe death. This is exactly what BG/NBD (Week 1) and Pareto/NBD (Week 4) solve — they probabilistically infer aliveness from purchase patterns.

Part 4: The sBG Model for Contractual Settings

The shifted Beta-Geometric (sBG) model is the contractual analog of BG/NBD. It models the probability that a customer renews at each contract boundary, allowing for heterogeneity across the customer base.

THE sBG MODEL
P(churn at renewal t) = θi  |  θi ~ Beta(a, b)
Each customer has a personal churn probability θ drawn from a Beta distribution

With just two parameters (a, b), the sBG model captures the entire heterogeneity of churn propensity across your customer base. Customers with low θ are the loyal ones who renew almost automatically; customers with high θ are the ones who churn quickly. The population-level retention curve emerges from this mixture.

The sBG Retention Curve

The Power of Two Parameters

The sBG model produces a retention curve from just two numbers: the shape parameters of the Beta distribution. Fit these two parameters to your observed retention data (% of cohort surviving at each renewal point), and the model gives you: projected retention for future periods, expected customer lifetime, CLV per cohort, and the heterogeneity structure (what % are loyal vs. at-risk).

Hybrid Businesses

Many real businesses are hybrids. A SaaS company has contractual subscriptions (contractual, continuous) but also tracks product usage frequency (non-contractual signal). The best approach: use sBG or Cox for the contractual churn prediction, but incorporate non-contractual signals (usage frequency, login cadence) as covariates. Don't force a pure non-contractual model on a fundamentally contractual relationship.

Part 5: Implementation in Python

Python · sBG + Model Selection
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import minimize
from scipy.special import beta as beta_fn

# === sBG Model: Shifted Beta-Geometric ===
# Input: observed retention rates by renewal period
# retention[t] = fraction surviving through renewal t

retention_observed = np.array([
    1.0, 0.631, 0.468, 0.382, 0.326,
    0.289, 0.262, 0.241  # 7 renewal periods
])

def sbg_survival(params, T):
    """sBG survival function: P(alive at renewal T)"""
    a, b = params
    S = np.ones(T + 1)
    for t in range(1, T + 1):
        # P(survive through renewal t) = Beta(a, b+t) / Beta(a, b)
        S[t] = beta_fn(a, b + t) / beta_fn(a, b)
    return S

def sbg_neg_loglik(params, retention):
    a, b = params
    if a <= 0 or b <= 0: return 1e10
    T = len(retention) - 1
    S_pred = sbg_survival((a, b), T)
    # Minimize squared error between predicted and observed
    sse = np.sum((S_pred - retention) ** 2)
    return sse

result = minimize(sbg_neg_loglik, x0=[1.0, 1.0],
                   args=(retention_observed,), method='Nelder-Mead')
a_hat, b_hat = result.x
print(f"sBG Parameters: a={a_hat:.3f}, b={b_hat:.3f}")

# === Project Future Retention ===
S_projected = sbg_survival((a_hat, b_hat), T=20)
print("Projected retention (next 20 renewals):")
for t in range(0, 21, 5):
    print(f"  Renewal {t}: {S_projected[t]:.1%}")

# === Expected Lifetime ===
E_lifetime = S_projected[1:].sum()
print(f"Expected customer lifetime: {E_lifetime:.1f} renewals")

# === CLV ===
ARPU = 99  # per renewal period
MARGIN = 0.70
DISCOUNT = 0.01  # per period
clv = sum(S_projected[t] * ARPU * MARGIN / (1+DISCOUNT)**t
         for t in range(1, 21))
print(f"20-period CLV: ${clv:.0f}")

Part 6: Your Week 11 Playbook

QuestionAnswer
"Which CLV model for my business?"Ask: do I observe churn? If yes → contractual (sBG/Cox). If no → non-contractual (BG/NBD).
"Can I use BG/NBD for SaaS?"No. P(Alive) is meaningless when you directly observe cancellations. Use sBG or Cox PH.
"What about hybrid businesses?"Model the contractual decision (renew/cancel) with sBG/Cox; use non-contractual signals as covariates.
"How does sBG compare to Cox?"sBG is simpler (2 parameters, cohort-level). Cox is richer (covariates, individual-level). sBG for projections, Cox for understanding drivers.
