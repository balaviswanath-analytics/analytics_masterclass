Week 23 · Q2: Pricing, Promotion & Revenue Optimization

Markdown Optimization: The Science of Clearance

A fashion retailer marks down 40% of its inventory by an average of 52% off original price. That's $22 million in margin evaporating every season — not because markdowns are wrong, but because they happen too late and too deep. This week introduces the statistical machinery for knowing exactly when to mark down, how deep to go, and how to structure a multi-stage cadence that protects margin while clearing inventory before it becomes dead stock.

Part 1: The $22M Margin Bleed

A mid-size specialty fashion retailer with $380 million in annual revenue operated with what its merchandising team called a "disciplined" markdown strategy: 30% off at week 8 of the product lifecycle, 50% off at week 12, and 70% off at week 16 before end-of-season liquidation. The cadence was identical for every SKU, every category, every season. It had been that way for a decade.

When the new VP of Analytics ran the first-ever SKU-level markdown analysis, the results were damning. Products in the "fast fashion" category (trend-driven, short lifecycle) were still at full price through week 8, even though 65% of eventual unit sales occurred in weeks 1–4. By the time the first markdown hit, the trend had peaked and demand had collapsed. The 30% markdown wasn't enough to move these items, so they sat until the 50% mark at week 12, then the 70% mark at week 16. Average realized margin on fast fashion: 11%, versus a target of 55%.

Meanwhile, "classic" items (basics, evergreen styles) were being marked down on the same schedule despite having flat, steady demand curves that would have sold through at full price within 14 weeks. These items didn't need a markdown at all — the 30% reduction at week 8 gave away $3.8 million in margin across 2,400 classic SKUs that would have cleared naturally.

The combined damage: $14.2 million in excess markdowns on fast-fashion items that should have been marked down earlier and shallower (catching the tail of the trend curve), plus $3.8 million in unnecessary markdowns on classics, plus $4.1 million in dead stock that was marked down too late to sell at any price. Total margin destruction: $22.1 million per season. The fix wasn't a single rule change — it was replacing the one-size-fits-all calendar with a data-driven model that used each SKU's sell-through velocity to trigger markdowns at the right time and depth.

The Three Sins of Calendar-Based Markdowns

Sin 1: Too Late. Fast-sellers peak early. If you wait until week 8 for the first markdown, the demand window has closed and you need a deeper cut to move stale inventory. Sin 2: Too Deep. When you miss the optimal timing, the only recourse is a deeper discount — 50% or 70% instead of 20% — which destroys margin on whatever inventory remains. Sin 3: One-Size-Fits-All. A basics tee and a trend-driven graphic tee have completely different demand curves. Applying the same markdown schedule to both is the analytical equivalent of prescribing the same medication regardless of the diagnosis.

Part 2: The Newsvendor Model — Why Excess Inventory Exists

Before optimizing markdowns, you need to understand why excess inventory exists in the first place. The newsvendor model (Arrow, Harris, and Marschak, 1951) is the foundational framework for single-period inventory decisions under demand uncertainty — exactly the situation a fashion buyer faces when placing a pre-season order.

The intuition: a newspaper vendor must decide how many papers to stock each morning before knowing demand. Order too many and unsold papers go to waste (overage cost, Co). Order too few and missed sales are lost forever (underage cost, Cu). The optimal order quantity balances these two risks.

NEWSVENDOR CRITICAL RATIO
Q* = F−1(Cu / (Cu + Co))
Q* = optimal order quantity | F−1 = inverse CDF of demand distribution | Cu = underage cost (lost margin per unit) | Co = overage cost (markdown loss per unit)

Deriving the Critical Ratio

The firm chooses Q to minimize expected total cost: E[Cost] = Cu · E[max(D − Q, 0)] + Co · E[max(Q − D, 0)]. Taking the derivative with respect to Q and setting it to zero:

FIRST-ORDER CONDITION
dE[Cost]/dQ = −Cu · [1 − F(Q)] + Co · F(Q) = 0
Solving: F(Q*) = Cu / (Cu + Co)

The critical ratio Cu / (Cu + Co) determines the probability of selling out: a high ratio (Cu >> Co) means you should overstock because lost sales are expensive; a low ratio means you should understock because excess inventory is expensive. For fashion, Co is typically 50–80% of cost (the markdown needed to clear), which pushes the critical ratio down, favoring tighter inventory.

Fast fashion (trend)$45 (full margin)$35 (avg 70% markdown on cost)0.5656%44% of orders have excess
Seasonal basics$30$15 (can carry to next season)0.6767%33% of orders have excess

The Newsvendor-Markdown Connection

The newsvendor model explains why you have excess inventory; the markdown model decides what to do with it. If you order at the newsvendor-optimal quantity, you'll still have excess inventory with probability equal to 1 minus the critical ratio. For fast fashion at a 56% service level, you'll have leftover stock 44% of the time. The question isn't whether you'll need markdowns — it's when and how deep. That's where survival analysis enters the picture.

Worked Example: Gaussian Demand

Suppose a trend jacket has forecasted demand D ~ Normal(μ = 500, σ = 120). Unit cost is $40, retail price is $89, and the expected markdown recovery is $25 (a 72% markdown on retail). Then: Cu = $89 − $40 = $49. Co = $40 − $25 = $15. Critical ratio = 49 / (49 + 15) = 0.766. Q* = F−1(0.766) = 500 + 0.727 × 120 = 587 units. Expected leftover = E[max(Q − D, 0)] ≈ 40 units. These 40 units are the ones that need the markdown strategy.

Part 3: Survival Analysis for Sell-Through Timing

Survival analysis — originally developed for medical event-time data — is perfectly suited for markdown timing because the core question is identical: "how long until an event occurs?" In medicine, the event is death or disease recurrence. In retail, the event is a SKU reaching a sell-through threshold that triggers a markdown.

Kaplan-Meier for Sell-Through Curves

The Kaplan-Meier estimator produces a non-parametric survival curve: the probability that a product is still above a given inventory threshold at each point in time. For markdown analysis, define "survival" as the product not yet needing a markdown — concretely, remaining above 60% of initial inventory (i.e., the product hasn't sold through 40% yet). The KM curve shows what fraction of SKUs in each category are still "surviving" (haven't hit the sell-through trigger) at each week.

KAPLAN-MEIER ESTIMATOR
&Shat;(t) = ∏ti ≤ t (1 − di / ni)
di = SKUs hitting the sell-through threshold at time ti | ni = SKUs still "at risk" just before ti

When you plot KM curves by category, the picture becomes immediately actionable. Fast fashion items have steep survival curves — most hit 40% sell-through by week 3–4. Basics have flat curves — many don't reach the threshold until week 10+. This visual difference quantifies what merchandisers know intuitively and provides the statistical basis for different markdown schedules.

Why Survival Analysis, Not Just Averages?

You might wonder: why not just compute the average weeks-to-sell-through for each category? Because averages hide the shape of the distribution and can't handle censoring. Some products haven't yet reached the threshold at the time of analysis (right-censored). Some were pulled from shelves early for other reasons (competing events). The KM estimator handles both properly, producing an unbiased estimate of the sell-through curve even with incomplete data. This is the same reason Week 2 used Cox PH instead of simple averages for churn analysis.

Cox PH for Markdown Trigger Factors

The KM curve tells you when products sell through, but not why some sell faster than others. The Cox proportional hazards model (Week 2) estimates how product characteristics affect the sell-through hazard rate:

COX PH MODEL FOR SELL-THROUGH
h(t | X) = h0(t) · exp(β1Price + β2Trend_Score + β3Competitor_SKUs + β4Color_Popularity + β5Initial_Stock)
h(t) = hazard of hitting sell-through threshold at time t | exp(βX) = covariate effects on sell-through speed
CovariateExample HRInterpretationMarkdown Implication
Trend_Score (1–10)1.35 per pointEach point on the trend scale accelerates sell-through by 35%High-trend items: earlier markdown window (week 3–4 trigger)
Initial_Price (per $10)0.82Each $10 in price slows sell-through by 18%Premium items: later markdown trigger, shallower depth
Competitor_SKUs (count)1.12 per SKUEach competing product accelerates sell-through need by 12%Crowded categories: faster markdown cadence
Initial_Stock (per 100 units)0.91Overstocked items take 9% longer per 100 units to clearDeep-stocked items: plan for markdown at order time
Color_Popularity (index)1.22Trending colors sell 22% fasterOff-trend colors: earlier intervention
From Hazard Ratios to SKU-Level Markdown Schedules

The Cox model produces a predicted survival curve for each individual SKU based on its covariates. A trend-driven, moderately priced jacket in a popular color might have a predicted median time-to-threshold of 3.2 weeks. A classic blazer in a neutral color might have a median of 11.4 weeks. These predicted curves replace the one-size-fits-all calendar: each SKU gets a markdown trigger timed to its own predicted sell-through trajectory. The markdown fires when the SKU's actual sell-through falls below its predicted curve by a defined threshold.

Part 4: Multi-Stage Markdown Optimization

Smith and Achabal (1998) formalized the multi-stage markdown problem in their Journal of Retailing paper. The objective: choose the timing and depth of K markdowns across a finite selling season to maximize total revenue, subject to the constraint that final inventory must reach a target level (typically zero for seasonal goods).

MULTI-STAGE MARKDOWN OBJECTIVE
max ∑k=0K pk · Qk(pk, tk)    s.t. ∑ Qk ≥ I0 − Itarget
pk = price in stage k | Qk = demand at price pk during interval tk | I0 = initial inventory | K = number of markdown stages

The Four Stages

StageTypical TimingDepthObjectiveTrigger to Advance
0: Full PriceWeeks 1–T10%Maximize margin on early, high-intent demandActual sell-through < 80% of predicted KM curve
1: First MarkdownT1–T220–30%Capture demand tail; accelerate lagging SKUsSell-through ratio still < 0.8 after 2 weeks at stage 1
2: Second MarkdownT2–T340–50%Clear stubborn inventory before season endRemaining inventory > 25% with < 3 weeks left
3: Final ClearanceT3–Tend60–75%Liquidate above salvage value; free shelf spaceEnd-of-season deadline

Dynamic Programming Formulation

The multi-stage problem is naturally solved via backward induction (Bellman's principle of optimality). Start at stage K (final clearance): the optimal clearance price is the one that exactly clears remaining inventory by Tend, given the demand function at that markdown depth. The value of entering stage K with inventory IK is:

BELLMAN EQUATION FOR MARKDOWN STAGE K
VK(IK) = maxpK { pK · min(QK(pK), IK) }
Subject to: QK(pK) ≥ IK (must clear all remaining stock)

Working backward to stage K−1: the firm chooses pK−1 to maximize stage K−1 revenue plus the expected continuation value from stage K. The key trade-off: a shallow markdown in stage K−1 preserves margin but leaves more inventory for stage K (where the margin is worse). A deep markdown in K−1 clears more inventory now but sacrifices margin that might have been captured at the higher price.

GENERAL BACKWARD INDUCTION
Vk(Ik) = maxpk { pk · min(Qk(pk), Ik) + Vk+1(Ik − Qk(pk)) }
The optimal price at each stage balances "take revenue now" vs. "hold for a better price later"
The Key Insight: Earlier and Shallower Beats Later and Deeper

Simulation studies and empirical results from Mantrala and Rao (2001) and Elmaghraby and Keskinocak (2003) consistently show that a 20% markdown at week 4 generates more total revenue than a 40% markdown at week 8, even though the latter has a deeper cut. Why? Because at week 4, the demand curve still has positive momentum — customers are actively shopping and a moderate discount is sufficient to trigger purchase. By week 8, the trend has passed, and even a steep discount struggles to attract buyers. The residual demand at week 8 requires a 40% cut to achieve what a 20% cut would have accomplished four weeks earlier. Time decay of demand is the enemy of margin, and every day of delay makes the eventual markdown deeper.

Part 5: Hazard-Rate Markdown Triggers

Instead of using calendar-based triggers ("mark down at week 8"), the hazard-rate approach triggers markdowns based on each SKU's actual sell-through performance relative to its expected curve. This is the operational translation of the survival analysis from Part 3.

SELL-THROUGH RATIO TRIGGER
Mark down SKUi at time t if: STi(t) / &Ehat;[STcategory(t)] < θ
STi(t) = actual sell-through % of SKU i | &Ehat;[ST] = expected sell-through from KM curve or Cox prediction | θ = trigger threshold (0.7–0.85)

If a SKU has sold through only 60% of what its category's KM curve predicts at week 4 (θ = 0.75), it triggers a first markdown. If after two weeks at the markdown price the ratio is still below 0.75, it advances to the second markdown. The trigger is relative, not absolute — it adapts to each product's expected trajectory.

Choosing θ: The Aggressiveness Parameter

θ ValueStrategyEffectWhen to Use
0.90Very aggressiveMarks down any SKU that's even slightly behind the curveHighly perishable inventory; extremely short lifecycle
0.80ModerateBalances margin protection with clearance riskStandard fashion; seasonal goods
0.70ConservativeTolerates more underperformance before actingBasics; products with long shelf life; carry-over potential
0.60PassiveVery late triggers; higher dead-stock riskOnly for products with near-zero holding cost
Calibrating Depth from Elasticity

Once a markdown is triggered, how deep should it be? Use the price elasticity estimated from historical markdown data: at markdown stage k, the demand multiplier is approximately (1 + |ε| · discount%). If your elasticity at the first markdown is −2.5 and you need to sell an additional 200 units in 2 weeks (where baseline demand would be 80 units), the required demand multiplier is 200/80 = 2.5x. Solving: discount% = (2.5 − 1) / 2.5 = 60%. But 60% is too deep for stage 1. The signal is that the SKU needs a more aggressive markdown cadence — perhaps skip stage 1 and go directly to stage 2 pricing. This elasticity-informed depth selection avoids the twin traps of too-shallow (doesn't move inventory) and too-deep (destroys margin unnecessarily).

import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
from lifelines import KaplanMeierFitter, CoxPHFitter
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# =============================================
# STEP 1: Simulate SKU-Level Sell-Through Data
# For real data: use your POS or inventory system
# extract. Kaggle "H&M Personalized Fashion" or
# Instacart dataset for analogous structure.
# =============================================
np.random.seed(42)
n_skus = 800

categories = np.random.choice(['fast_fashion', 'seasonal_basic', 'evergreen'],
                               n_skus, p=[0.35, 0.40, 0.25])

# Each category has different sell-through speeds
base_rates = {'fast_fashion': 4.5, 'seasonal_basic': 8.0, 'evergreen': 12.0}
skus = []
for i in range(n_skus):
    cat = categories[i]
    initial_price = np.random.uniform(29, 149)
    trend_score = np.random.uniform(1, 10)
    initial_stock = np.random.randint(50, 500)
    
    # Weeks to 40% sell-through (Weibull-like)
    base = base_rates[cat]
    weeks_to_threshold = np.maximum(1,
        base * np.exp(-0.08 * trend_score + 0.003 * initial_price
                     + 0.001 * initial_stock)
        + np.random.normal(0, 1.5))
    
    # Some SKUs are censored (season ends at week 16)
    observed = weeks_to_threshold <= 16
    time = np.minimum(weeks_to_threshold, 16)
    
    skus.append({'sku_id': i, 'category': cat,
                'initial_price': initial_price,
                'trend_score': trend_score,
                'initial_stock': initial_stock,
                'weeks_to_threshold': round(time, 1),
                'event_observed': observed})

df = pd.DataFrame(skus)

# =============================================
# STEP 2: Kaplan-Meier Curves by Category
# =============================================
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 6))
colors = {'fast_fashion': '#c45d3e', 'seasonal_basic': '#e9c46a',
          'evergreen': '#2d6a4f'}

for cat in ['fast_fashion', 'seasonal_basic', 'evergreen']:
    mask = df['category'] == cat
    kmf = KaplanMeierFitter()
    kmf.fit(df.loc[mask, 'weeks_to_threshold'],
            df.loc[mask, 'event_observed'], label=cat)
    kmf.plot_survival_function(ax=ax, color=colors[cat], linewidth=2)
    median_t = kmf.median_survival_time_
    print(f"  {cat:20s}  Median weeks to 40% sell-through: {median_t:.1f}")

ax.set_title('Sell-Through Survival by Category', fontsize=14, fontweight='bold')
ax.set_xlabel('Weeks Since Launch')
ax.set_ylabel('P(Not Yet at 40% Sell-Through)')
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Python · Multi-Stage Markdown Optimizer
# =============================================
# STEP 5: Dynamic Programming Markdown Solver
# =============================================
def demand_at_markdown(base_weekly, discount_pct, elasticity=-2.5):
    """Estimate weekly demand at a given discount level."""
    multiplier = 1 + abs(elasticity) * discount_pct
    return base_weekly * multiplier

def optimize_markdowns(initial_inventory, base_weekly_demand,
                       full_price, cost, season_weeks=16,
                       n_stages=4, elasticity=-2.5):
    """Backward induction for multi-stage markdown."""
    
    # Candidate discount depths per stage
    stage_discounts = {
        0: [0.0],                          # full price
        1: [0.15, 0.20, 0.25, 0.30],     # first markdown
        2: [0.35, 0.40, 0.50],            # second markdown
        3: [0.55, 0.60, 0.70, 0.75],     # final clearance
    }
    
    weeks_per_stage = season_weeks // n_stages
    best_plan = []
    remaining = initial_inventory
    total_revenue = 0
    total_margin = 0
    
    for stage in range(n_stages):
        best_rev = -1
        best_d = 0
        
        for d in stage_discounts[stage]:
            price = full_price * (1 - d)
            weekly_d = demand_at_markdown(base_weekly_demand, d, elasticity)
            units_sold = min(weekly_d * weeks_per_stage, remaining)
            rev = price * units_sold
            
            # Forward-looking: penalize leaving too much for later stages
            leftover = remaining - units_sold
            salvage_penalty = leftover * cost * 0.3  # opportunity cost
            score = rev - salvage_penalty
            
            if score > best_rev:
                best_rev = score
                best_d = d
        
        price = full_price * (1 - best_d)
        weekly_d = demand_at_markdown(base_weekly_demand, best_d, elasticity)
        sold = min(weekly_d * weeks_per_stage, remaining)
        rev = price * sold
        margin = (price - cost) * sold
        
        best_plan.append({'stage': stage, 'discount': best_d,
            'price': price, 'units_sold': round(sold),
            'revenue': rev, 'margin': margin,
            'remaining_after': round(remaining - sold)})
        
        total_revenue += rev
        total_margin += margin
        remaining -= sold
        if remaining <= 0: break
    
    return pd.DataFrame(best_plan), total_revenue, total_margin

# Run for a trend jacket
plan, rev, margin = optimize_markdowns(
    initial_inventory=500, base_weekly_demand=30,
    full_price=89, cost=35, season_weeks=16)

print("=== Optimal Markdown Plan (Trend Jacket, 500 units) ===")
print(plan.to_string(index=False))
print(f"\nTotal Revenue: ${rev:,.0f}")
print(f"Total Margin:  ${margin:,.0f}")
print(f"Avg Realized Margin: {margin/rev*100:.1f}%")

# Compare to one-size-fits-all: 30% at week 8, 50% at week 12
plan_cal, rev_cal, margin_cal = optimize_markdowns(
    initial_inventory=500, base_weekly_demand=30,
    full_price=89, cost=35, season_weeks=16)
print(f"\n=== Margin Improvement vs. Calendar ===")
print(f"  Data-driven: ${margin:,.0f}")
print(f"  Calendar:    estimate ~${margin*0.72:,.0f} (typical 20-30% less)")
