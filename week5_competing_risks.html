Week 05 · Q1: Customer Lifetime & Retention Economics

Competing Risks: When Customers Leave for Different Reasons

A customer who leaves because your price is too high requires a completely different retention strategy than one who leaves because a competitor poached them. Standard survival analysis lumps all churn into one bucket. Competing risks analysis separates it into distinct failure types — and changes your strategic playbook entirely.

Part 1: Why "Churn" Is Not One Thing

Imagine you run a B2B SaaS company. Your annual churn rate is 15%. The CEO says "fix it." But when you dig into exit interviews and cancellation reasons, you discover three entirely different phenomena:

Churn Type% of ChurnersRoot CauseCorrect Intervention
Price-driven35%Budget cuts, sticker shock at renewalFlexible pricing, downgrade options
Competitor-driven25%Poached by rival with superior featureFeature parity, switching-cost reinforcement
Value-driven40%Customer never achieved ROI, low adoptionBetter onboarding, success management
The $4M Misallocation

A mid-market SaaS firm spent $2M annually on a blanket "retention discount" program — offering 20% off at renewal to anyone flagged as at-risk. Post-mortem analysis showed the discounts were effective for price-driven churners (40% retention lift) but completely useless for value-driven churners (2% lift — they didn't see value at any price). By separating churn types using competing risks analysis and targeting discounts only at price-sensitive churners while redirecting budget to customer success for value-driven churners, they achieved a net 28% churn reduction versus the previous 11%.

The problem is structural: when one type of churn event occurs, the other types become impossible for that customer. A customer who leaves for price reasons can't also leave for competitor reasons — they're already gone. This mutual exclusivity is exactly what competing risks models are designed to handle.

Part 2: Why Kaplan-Meier Overestimates Risk

Here's the critical statistical issue: when you use standard Kaplan-Meier or Cox regression on a single churn type (say, competitor-driven churn) while treating other churn types as censored, you overestimate the probability of that specific event. The reason is subtle but important.

KM assumes censoring is "non-informative" — that censored individuals have the same future risk as those still being observed. But when a customer churns for price reasons, they've been removed from the pool of people who could ever churn for competitor reasons. They aren't random dropouts; their departure changes the composition of the remaining risk set.

THE OVERESTIMATION PROBLEM
1 − KMk(t) ≥ CIFk(t)   always
The complement of the cause-specific KM always overestimates the true cumulative incidence
A Real-World Magnitude

A study on cardiac patients found that the standard Cox model estimated 18% as "high risk" for coronary heart disease death. The Fine-Gray competing risks model estimated only 8% as high risk, because the Cox model was ignoring that many patients die from non-cardiac causes first, removing them from the cardiac risk pool. That's a 2.25x overestimation. In customer analytics, similar magnitudes are common when competing churn types are ignored.

Part 3: The Cumulative Incidence Function (CIF)

The CIF is the correct way to estimate the probability of a specific event type in the presence of competing risks. Unlike KM (which conditions on never experiencing any event), CIF accounts for the fact that competing events remove people from risk.

CUMULATIVE INCIDENCE FUNCTION
CIFk(t) = P(T ≤ t, cause = k) = ∫0t hk(u) · S(u) du
hk(u) = cause-specific hazard | S(u) = overall survival (no event of any type)

The key property that makes CIF mathematically sound: the CIFs for all competing events sum to the overall failure probability. At any time t, CIFprice(t) + CIFcompetitor(t) + CIFvalue(t) = total churn probability. This "stacking" property lets you decompose total churn into its constituent parts, each properly estimated.

Reading CIF Curves Strategically

Visual Interpretation

When you plot CIF curves for each churn type, the area between curves tells you the relative prevalence at each time point. If the price-driven CIF is steep early but flattens while the value-driven CIF accelerates later, it tells you: price problems hit at renewal (month 12), but value erosion is a slow burn that peaks at month 18-24. This changes your intervention timing entirely.

Use Gray's test (the competing risks analog of the log-rank test) to formally compare CIF curves between segments. "Do enterprise customers have a significantly different competitor-driven churn incidence than SMB customers?"

Part 4: Cause-Specific Hazard Models

The simplest regression approach: fit a separate Cox PH model for each churn type, treating all other event types as censored. This gives you cause-specific hazard ratios — the effect of covariates on the rate at which each type of churn occurs.

CAUSE-SPECIFIC HAZARD MODEL
hk(t | X) = h0k(t) · exp(βk'X)
One separate Cox model per cause k, with competing events treated as censored

When to Use Cause-Specific Hazards

Use this approach when your question is etiological — "what factors drive each type of churn?" You want to understand the causal mechanism, not just predict the probability. For example: "Does poor NPS score increase the rate of value-driven churn specifically, after controlling for the fact that some customers leave for price reasons first?"

Notice how different covariates drive different churn types. High monthly charges mainly drive price-driven churn (HR=1.85) but barely affect competitor or value churn. Feature adoption is the strongest protector against value-driven churn (HR=0.45, a 55% risk reduction) but matters less for the other types. A detected competitor RFP triples the rate of competitor-driven churn (HR=3.20) — an obvious early warning signal. And executive sponsor departure doubles the rate of value-driven churn — because the internal champion who understood the ROI is gone.

Part 5: The Fine-Gray Subdistribution Model

The alternative approach: rather than modeling the rate (hazard), model the cumulative probability directly. Fine and Gray (1999) proposed a proportional hazards model for the subdistribution hazard, which directly governs the CIF.

FINE-GRAY SUBDISTRIBUTION HAZARD
λksub(t | X) = λ0ksub(t) · exp(γk'X)
The subdistribution hazard governs the CIF directly: CIFk(t) = 1 − exp(−∫ λksub)

The Key Difference: Risk Set Definition

In cause-specific hazard models, individuals who experience a competing event are removed from the risk set (censored). In the Fine-Gray model, they remain in the risk set indefinitely. This sounds paradoxical — how can a customer who already left for price reasons still be "at risk" of competitor churn? They can't, in reality. But mathematically, keeping them in the risk set is what allows the model to directly estimate the cumulative probability (CIF), not just the rate.

Cause-Specific vs. Fine-Gray: The Decision Framework

Question TypeUse This ModelWhy
"What factors drive each type of churn?" (Etiology)Cause-Specific HazardProper causal interpretation of hazard ratios
"What is the probability of price-churn by month 12?" (Prognosis)Fine-GrayDirectly estimates cumulative incidence
"Which customers are at highest risk of competitor churn?" (Prediction)Fine-GrayIndividual-level CIF predictions
The Causal Inference Warning

Recent methodological work (notably by Paul Allison, 2024) argues that Fine-Gray subdistribution hazard ratios should not be interpreted causally. They can produce misleading results when competing event rates differ between treatment groups. For causal questions, always use cause-specific hazard models. Use Fine-Gray only for prediction and prognosis. Reporting both side-by-side is considered best practice in current methodology.

Part 6: Implementation in Python

Python · Competing Risks
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from lifelines import CoxPHFitter, KaplanMeierFitter

# ============================================
# STEP 1: Prepare Competing Risks Data
# event_type: 0=censored, 1=price, 2=competitor, 3=value
# ============================================

df = pd.read_csv('churn_with_reasons.csv')

# ============================================
# STEP 2: Cause-Specific Hazard Models
# Fit separate Cox models per churn type
# ============================================

covariates = ['monthly_charges', 'feature_adoption',
              'competitor_rfp', 'sponsor_left']

results = {}
for cause, label in [(1,'Price'), (2,'Competitor'), (3,'Value')]:
    # Treat THIS cause as event, all others as censored
    df_cause = df.copy()
    df_cause['event'] = (df['event_type'] == cause).astype(int)

    cph = CoxPHFitter(penalizer=0.01)
    cph.fit(
        df_cause[covariates + ['duration', 'event']],
        duration_col='duration',
        event_col='event'
    )
    results[label] = cph
    print(f"\n=== {label} Churn ===")
    cph.print_summary()


# ============================================
# STEP 3: Cumulative Incidence Functions
# Non-parametric CIF estimation
# ============================================

# Using the Aalen-Johansen estimator approach
# First get overall KM survival
kmf_all = KaplanMeierFitter()
kmf_all.fit(df['duration'], event_observed=(df['event_type'] > 0))

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 6))
colors = {'Price': '#c45d3e', 'Competitor': '#264653', 'Value': '#2d6a4f'}

# Approximate CIF via cause-specific hazards + overall survival
for cause, label in [(1,'Price'), (2,'Competitor'), (3,'Value')]:
    mask = df['event_type'] == cause
    times = np.sort(df.loc[mask, 'duration'].unique())

    # Simple CIF approximation
    cif = []
    n_total = len(df)
    for t in times:
        n_event_k = ((df['event_type'] == cause) & (df['duration'] <= t)).sum()
        cif.append(n_event_k / n_total)

    ax.step(times, cif, where='post', label=label, color=colors[label], linewidth=2)

ax.set_xlabel('Months')
ax.set_ylabel('Cumulative Incidence')
ax.set_title('Cumulative Incidence by Churn Type')
ax.legend(); plt.show()

Top 5 Week 5 Mistakes

1. Using 1 - KM for cause-specific probabilities. This always overestimates. Use CIF instead.

2. Only fitting one model. Best practice is reporting cause-specific AND Fine-Gray side-by-side.

3. Interpreting Fine-Gray HRs causally. Subdistribution HRs have no clean causal interpretation. For "why" questions, use cause-specific.

4. Poor event coding. If your "churn reason" data is noisy or self-reported, competing risks analysis inherits that noise. Invest in clean exit categorization.

5. Ignoring that CIFs must sum correctly. Individual CIFs should stack to the overall failure probability. If they don't, something is wrong with your event coding.
