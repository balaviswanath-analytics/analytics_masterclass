Week 07 · Q1: Customer Lifetime & Retention Economics

Cohort Economics: The Truth Behind Your Growth Story

Revenue is growing 30% year-over-year. The board is thrilled. But hidden beneath that topline number might be a terrifying truth: each successive cohort of customers is worse than the last, and your growth is an illusion fueled by acquisition spending. Cohort analysis is the X-ray that reveals the skeletal structure of your business.

Part 1: The Growth Illusion

Here's a scenario that plays out more often than most founders want to admit. Company XYZ reports 40% revenue growth. Investors celebrate. But look at the cohort-level data:

Cohort (Signup Quarter)Month 1 RevenueMonth 6 RevenueMonth 12 Revenue12-Month Retention
Q1 2024$500K$380K$320K64%
Q2 2024$650K$455K$345K53%
Q3 2024$900K$540K$360K40%
Q4 2024$1.2M$600K??????
The Leaky Bucket

Total revenue is growing because Q4's massive acquisition (1,200 new customers) overwhelms the decay from earlier cohorts. But look at the retention trajectory: 64% → 53% → 40%. Each new cohort is worse than the last. The company is acquiring lower-quality customers (possibly through broader targeting or aggressive discounting), and the bucket is leaking faster with each fill. If acquisition slows even slightly, the declining retention will cause total revenue to plateau or decline — and the board will be blindsided.

This is why cohort analysis isn't optional. It's the only way to separate growth from acquisition (new customers pouring in) versus growth from retention (existing customers staying and spending). Healthy businesses have both. Fragile businesses have only the first.

Part 2: The Cohort Revenue Triangle

The cohort triangle (also called a retention triangle or revenue triangle) is the fundamental visualization. Rows represent cohorts (grouped by signup month/quarter). Columns represent periods since signup. Each cell shows a metric: revenue, active customers, or retention rate.

Reading the Triangle

Read across rows to see how a single cohort decays over time. The shape tells you the retention curve.

Read down columns to compare cohort quality at the same lifecycle stage. Are Month-3 retention rates improving or declining across cohorts?

Read diagonals to see what happened at a specific calendar date. A diagonal dip might indicate a product issue or competitor launch that affected everyone simultaneously.

Three Critical Metrics Per Cohort

COHORT METRICS
Retention Ratet = Activet / Active0
Revenue Retentiont = Revenuet / Revenue0
Net Revenue Retention (NRR) = (Revenuet + Expansion) / Revenue0
NRR > 100% means expansion revenue exceeds churn losses

Net Revenue Retention (NRR) is arguably the single most important metric in SaaS. NRR > 100% means your existing customers are spending more over time than you're losing to churn. The best SaaS companies operate at 120-140% NRR, meaning even with zero new customer acquisition, revenue would grow 20-40% annually from the existing base alone.

Part 4: Cohort Diagnostic Framework

Are Newer Cohorts Better or Worse?

Compare retention rates at the same lifecycle stage across cohorts. If Month-6 retention is 55% for Jan cohort, 50% for Apr cohort, and 42% for Jul cohort, you have a cohort quality degradation problem. Common causes: broader acquisition targeting (reaching less-ideal customers), aggressive discounting (attracting deal-seekers), or product-market fit erosion.

Separating Cohort Effects from Period Effects

A diagonal dip in the triangle (all cohorts performing worse in the same calendar month) suggests a period effect: something external happened (competitor launch, seasonal slowdown, product outage). A row-by-row decline suggests a cohort effect: the customers themselves are changing.

The Board Presentation Framework

When presenting cohort analysis to leadership, always show three things: (1) The topline growth curve (they already know this). (2) The cohort triangle underneath it, revealing the composition. (3) A "same-stage comparison" chart showing how Month-3 and Month-6 retention are trending across cohorts. If cohort quality is declining while topline grows, the message is: "We're growing, but the foundation is weakening. Without intervention, growth will stall in Q[X]."

Part 5: Implementation in Python

Python · Cohort Analysis
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns

# ============================================
# STEP 1: Build Cohort Triangle from Transactions
# ============================================

txns = pd.read_csv('transactions.csv', parse_dates=['date'])

# Assign cohort (month of first purchase)
first_purchase = txns.groupby('customer_id')['date'].min().reset_index()
first_purchase.columns = ['customer_id', 'cohort_date']
first_purchase['cohort'] = first_purchase['cohort_date'].dt.to_period('M')

txns = txns.merge(first_purchase[['customer_id', 'cohort']], on='customer_id')
txns['period'] = txns['date'].dt.to_period('M')
txns['cohort_age'] = (txns['period'] - txns['cohort']).apply(lambda x: x.n)

# Count active customers per cohort-age
cohort_data = txns.groupby(['cohort', 'cohort_age'])['customer_id'].nunique()
cohort_data = cohort_data.reset_index()
cohort_data.columns = ['cohort', 'age', 'customers']

# Pivot to triangle
cohort_sizes = cohort_data[cohort_data['age'] == 0].set_index('cohort')['customers']
triangle = cohort_data.pivot(index='cohort', columns='age', values='customers')

# Retention rate triangle
retention = triangle.divide(cohort_sizes, axis=0)


# ============================================
# STEP 2: Visualize as Heatmap
# ============================================

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(14, 8))
sns.heatmap(
    retention.iloc[:12, :12],  # first 12 cohorts, 12 months
    annot=True, fmt='.0%',
    cmap='RdYlGn',
    vmin=0, vmax=1,
    linewidths=0.5
)
ax.set_title('Cohort Retention Triangle')
ax.set_xlabel('Months Since First Purchase')
ax.set_ylabel('Cohort (Signup Month)')
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()


# ============================================
# STEP 3: Same-Stage Comparison
# ============================================

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 5))
for age in [1, 3, 6]:
    vals = retention[age].dropna()
    ax.plot(vals.index.astype(str), vals.values,
           marker='o', label=f'Month {age}')
ax.set_title('Cohort Quality Trend: Retention at Same Lifecycle Stage')
ax.set_ylabel('Retention Rate')
ax.legend(); plt.xticks(rotation=45)
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()
