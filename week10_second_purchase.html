Week 10 · Q1: Customer Lifetime & Retention Economics

Time-to-Second-Purchase: The Make-or-Break Moment

The gap between a customer's first and second purchase is the single most predictive window for long-term value. This week you'll learn Accelerated Failure Time (AFT) models — a powerful alternative to Cox PH that directly models "how long until the next event" rather than hazard rates. The shift in perspective changes how you think about interventions.

Part 1: Why the Second Purchase Changes Everything

Here's an empirical pattern that holds across industries: customers who make a second purchase within a specific window become 3-5x more likely to become long-term, high-value customers. Miss that window, and the probability of ever seeing them again drops below 10%.

The $2.4M Insight

An e-commerce company found that moving 10% of first-time buyers from the "31-60 day" window into the "15-30 day" window (via targeted post-purchase emails with personalized recommendations) increased 12-month revenue by $2.4M. The intervention cost $180K. That's a 13x ROI — and it was only possible because they modeled the time-to-second-purchase distribution and identified the critical window.

This is where Accelerated Failure Time models shine. Instead of modeling the hazard rate (how likely is the event right now?), AFT models directly answer: "how long until the event?" This framing aligns perfectly with the business question: "when will this first-time buyer come back?"

Part 2: Accelerated Failure Time Models — An Alternative to Cox

THE AFT MODEL
log(Ti) = β0 + β1X1 + ... + βpXp + σε
T = time to event | σ = scale | ε ~ distribution (Weibull, log-normal, log-logistic)

In plain English: the logarithm of survival time is a linear function of covariates plus random error. This is fundamentally different from Cox PH, which models the hazard multiplier. AFT models are parametric — you must choose a distribution for the error term — but in return you get a much more intuitive interpretation.

Cox PH vs. AFT: The Interpretation Difference

AspectCox PHAFT

The Communication Advantage

Tell a product manager "low NPS customers have a hazard ratio of 1.8 for churn" and they nod politely. Tell them "low NPS customers reach their second purchase 40% slower, which means they fall outside the critical 30-day window" and they immediately understand the action: fix the post-purchase experience to accelerate the second transaction.

Model Selection: The AIC Tournament

Fit all three distributions. Compare AIC (Akaike Information Criterion). Lowest AIC wins. Also check: does the Weibull shape parameter ρ suggest monotone hazard (if so, Weibull is appropriate) or non-monotone (consider log-logistic or log-normal)?

When Cox Still Wins

If you only care about ranking customers by risk (not predicting actual time), Cox PH is often sufficient and avoids the distribution assumption. AFT models are superior when: (a) you need to predict actual time-to-event, (b) the proportional hazards assumption fails, (c) you need to communicate findings in time units rather than risk ratios, or (d) you have frailty (Week 8) — AFT is more robust to omitted covariates than Cox.

Part 4: Reading AFT Coefficients

In an AFT model, exp(β) is the acceleration factor (also called time ratio). If exp(β) = 1.5 for a covariate, a one-unit increase in that covariate multiplies the expected survival time by 1.5 (50% longer). If exp(β) = 0.7, it reduces survival time by 30% (accelerates the event).

Part 5: Implementation in Python

Python · AFT Models
import pandas as pd
from lifelines import (WeibullAFTFitter, LogNormalAFTFitter,
                      LogLogisticAFTFitter)

df = pd.read_csv('first_to_second_purchase.csv')
# days_to_2nd: time from 1st to 2nd purchase
# converted: 1=made 2nd purchase, 0=censored (still waiting)

covs = ['post_purchase_email', 'first_order_value',
        'mobile_app_user', 'used_discount', 'product_category_electronics']

# === AIC Tournament ===
models = {
    'Weibull': WeibullAFTFitter(penalizer=0.01),
    'LogNormal': LogNormalAFTFitter(penalizer=0.01),
    'LogLogistic': LogLogisticAFTFitter(penalizer=0.01),
}

for name, fitter in models.items():
    fitter.fit(df[covs + ['days_to_2nd', 'converted']],
              duration_col='days_to_2nd', event_col='converted')
    print(f"{name} AIC: {fitter.AIC_:.1f}")

# === Fit Best Model (assume Weibull wins) ===
best = models['Weibull']
best.print_summary()

# === Predict median time to 2nd purchase ===
new_customers = pd.read_csv('new_first_buyers.csv')
new_customers['predicted_median_days'] = best.predict_median(
    new_customers[covs]
)

# Flag at-risk: predicted > 30 days
at_risk = new_customers[new_customers['predicted_median_days'] > 30]
print(f"At-risk first-buyers (predicted >30 days): {len(at_risk)}")
print("These need immediate post-purchase intervention")
