Week 21 · Q2: Pricing, Promotion & Revenue Optimization

Price Discrimination That Works (And Stays Legal)

Every SaaS company with a "Contact Sales" enterprise tier is practicing price discrimination. Every airline with 26 fare classes is practicing price discrimination. Every student discount, senior discount, and volume discount is price discrimination. This week formalizes the economics of charging different prices to different segments, derives the optimal price for each segment from elasticity data, quantifies the surplus extraction opportunity, and maps the legal boundaries you can't cross.

Part 1: The $180M Uniform Pricing Gap

A mid-market SaaS company selling project management software had a single pricing tier: $49/user/month for everyone. Engineering teams of 5, marketing agencies of 50, and Fortune 500 enterprises with 2,000 seats all paid the same rate. The simplicity felt fair. It was also leaving approximately $180 million per year on the table.

Here's why. An internal conjoint study (using the methodology from Week 16) revealed that enterprise customers with 500+ seats had a median willingness-to-pay of $112/user/month. They valued the software that much because it replaced three separate tools, integrated with their SSO infrastructure, and reduced project delivery time by an estimated 18%. The $49 price captured only 44% of their WTP. Meanwhile, freelancers and small teams (1–5 users) had a median WTP of just $22/user/month — meaning the $49 price was actually excluding them entirely from the customer base.

The pricing team modeled a three-tier structure based on segment elasticities: a Starter tier at $19/user/month (targeting freelancers and small teams, elasticity = −5.2), a Professional tier at $49/user/month (mid-market, elasticity = −2.8), and an Enterprise tier at $89/user/month (large organizations, elasticity = −1.4). Each tier included genuinely differentiated features: Starter had limited integrations, Professional added reporting and collaboration, and Enterprise included SSO, audit logs, dedicated support, and an SLA.

The results after 18 months: the Starter tier added 34,000 new users who would never have paid $49. The Enterprise tier captured an additional $63/user/month from 28,000 existing enterprise users who had been getting a windfall at $49. The Professional tier held steady. Net impact: +$184 million in ARR, with a 2.6x improvement in the surplus extraction ratio.

Why "Fair" Uniform Pricing Is Actually Unfair

Uniform pricing feels equitable — everyone pays the same. But it's actually a subsidy running in the wrong direction. Price-insensitive enterprise customers (who can afford more and derive more value) pay less than their WTP, while price-sensitive small customers (who derive less value and have less budget) are priced out of the market entirely. Segment-based pricing is more economically efficient: it captures more total surplus AND serves more customers. The key is that the price differences must be justified by genuine value differences (features, support, SLA), not arbitrary discrimination.

Part 2: Pigou's Three Degrees of Price Discrimination

Arthur Cecil Pigou formalized the taxonomy in The Economics of Welfare (1920), establishing three "degrees" of price discrimination that remain the foundational framework a century later. Understanding which degree you're practicing — and which is feasible in your market — determines the analytical approach.

DegreeWhat the Firm KnowsMechanismReal-World ExampleData Requirement
First Degree
(Perfect PD)		Each customer's exact WTPCharge each customer their maximum — extract all consumer surplusAuction models (eBay), negotiated enterprise contracts, car dealerships, bespoke consultingIndividual-level WTP — essentially impossible at scale without auctions
Second Degree
(Self-Selection / Menu Pricing)		That different customer types exist but not which customer is which typeDesign a menu of options; customers reveal their type by choosingSaaS tiers (Basic/Pro/Enterprise), airline fare classes, quantity discounts, early-bird pricingPackage design + price fences that prevent high-WTP customers from choosing the cheap option

The Welfare Economics

A critical question in economics and policy: does price discrimination help or hurt society? The answer depends on the degree and context.

First-degree PD maximizes total surplus (producer + consumer) because it serves every customer whose WTP exceeds marginal cost. But all surplus goes to the producer — consumer surplus is zero. It's efficient but inequitable.

Third-degree PD has an ambiguous welfare effect. It can increase total welfare if it brings new customers into the market (the "output expansion" effect) — for example, student discounts allow students to purchase who couldn't afford full price. But if it merely redistributes purchases among existing customers without expanding the market, total welfare may decrease due to inefficient allocation (some high-WTP customers in the elastic segment buy more than optimal, while low-WTP customers in the inelastic segment buy less).

Varian's (1985) Welfare Condition

Hal Varian proved that third-degree price discrimination increases total welfare if and only if it increases total output relative to uniform pricing. This is the key analytical test: after implementing segment pricing, is the total number of units sold higher than under uniform pricing? If yes, total welfare increased (even though consumer surplus may have shifted to producer surplus). If no, welfare decreased and the only effect was redistribution from consumers to the firm.

Part 3: The Inverse Elasticity Pricing Rule

For third-degree price discrimination, the profit-maximizing price for each segment can be derived from first principles. The firm maximizes profit Π = ∑k (pk − MC) · Qk(pk) where Qk(pk) is the demand function for segment k. Taking the first-order condition dΠ/dpk = 0 for each segment independently:

DERIVATION
dΠ/dpk = Qk + (pk − MC) · dQk/dpk = 0
Divide both sides by Qk and rearrange:
LERNER INDEX / INVERSE ELASTICITY RULE
(pk − MC) / pk = −1 / εk
The markup over marginal cost is inversely proportional to the absolute elasticity of the segment

Solving for the optimal price:

OPTIMAL SEGMENT PRICE
pk* = MC · εk / (1 + εk)
Where εk is the own-price elasticity of demand for segment k (negative number)

Let's work through a concrete numerical example. Suppose MC = $20 per user per month and you've estimated segment elasticities using the log-log models from Week 14:

SegmentElasticity (ε)Lerner Index (markup %)Optimal PriceMargin per User

Mid-Market (50–499)−2.540.0%$33.33$13.33
SMB (10–49)−4.025.0%$26.67$6.67
Starter (1–9)−7.014.3%$23.33$3.33
Why Enterprise Pays 3x More — And Why That's Rational

Enterprise customers have low elasticity (ε = −1.4) because switching costs are enormous (data migration, retraining, procurement cycles), the software is deeply embedded in their workflows, the price is a tiny fraction of their budget, and there are few substitutes that meet their compliance requirements. Starter customers have high elasticity (ε = −7.0) because they can switch to free alternatives, have minimal data lock-in, and the price is a meaningful portion of their budget. The 3x price difference isn't arbitrary — it's a direct mathematical consequence of how sensitive each segment is to price changes.

The Elasticity > −1 Trap

Notice that the Lerner formula requires |ε| > 1 (elastic demand). If a segment has |ε| ≤ 1 (inelastic demand), the profit-maximizing price is technically infinite — you should keep raising the price because total revenue increases even as quantity falls. In practice, this means either: (a) your elasticity estimate is wrong (the linear model breaks down at extreme prices), or (b) you're in a monopoly position for that segment and should test higher prices. Never set prices based on elasticities measured only near the current price; re-estimate at candidate price points using conjoint or experimental methods.

Part 4: Quantifying the Surplus Extraction Opportunity

Consumer surplus is the area between the demand curve and the market price — it represents the aggregate "bargain" that customers get when they pay less than their maximum WTP. Under uniform pricing, this area can be enormous. Price discrimination captures a portion of it as additional profit.

CONSUMER SURPLUS UNDER UNIFORM PRICE
CS = ∫p*pmax Q(p) dp
Area between the demand curve and the price line, from market price to the maximum WTP

The Surplus Extraction Ratio (SER) measures how much additional profit segmented pricing captures relative to uniform pricing:

SURPLUS EXTRACTION RATIO
SER = Πsegmented / Πuniform
SER > 1 always. Typical range: 1.15–1.40 for well-executed third-degree PD.

In practice, lower prices in elastic segments increase quantity (SMB expands from 3,000 to 3,500, Starter enters at 1,500), while higher enterprise prices lose some customers (200 → 180). The Full Optimization: Monte Carlo Simulation

In practice, you don't use the analytical formula in isolation. You build a simulation: (1) estimate demand functions Qk(pk) for each segment using historical data or conjoint-derived elasticities, (2) simulate 10,000 scenarios with uncertainty around the elasticity estimates (±0.3 around central estimate), (3) for each scenario, compute the optimal price per segment using the Lerner formula, (4) compute profit under both uniform and segmented pricing, (5) report the distribution of SER across scenarios. This gives you not just a point estimate but a confidence interval: "Segmented pricing is expected to increase profit by 22–38% (95% CI)."

Part 5: Designing Price Fences

Price fences are the features, restrictions, or conditions that prevent high-WTP customers from choosing the low-price option. Without effective fences, segmented pricing collapses into a single low price — everyone chooses the cheapest tier. Fence design is where economic theory meets product strategy.

Fence TypeMechanismExampleEffectiveness
Feature-BasedHigher tier includes features that high-WTP customers needSSO, audit logs, API access, advanced analyticsHigh — enterprise genuinely needs these features
Volume-BasedPer-unit price decreases with quantity"$49/user for 1–49 users, $39/user for 50+"Moderate — prevents small customers from paying enterprise rate but not vice versa
Time-BasedHigher price for immediate access, lower for delayedAirline early-booking vs. last-minute pricingHigh for travel, moderate for SaaS
Channel-BasedDifferent prices through different sales channelsSelf-serve web vs. sales-assistedHigh — enterprise buying through sales naturally selects for higher WTP
Commitment-BasedLower price for longer contractsMonthly vs. annual billing (20% discount for annual)High — doubles as retention mechanism
Verification-BasedRequire proof of segment membershipStudent ID for student discount, .edu emailVery high — hard to fake; but limited to verifiable groups
The Incentive Compatibility Constraint

In mechanism design theory (Myerson, 1981), a pricing menu is incentive compatible if each customer type voluntarily selects the option designed for them. The formal condition: the surplus a customer gets from choosing "their" tier must be greater than or equal to the surplus from choosing any other tier. If an enterprise customer gets more surplus from the Pro tier ($49) than the Enterprise tier ($89), they'll downgrade — and your segmented pricing collapses. Testing incentive compatibility is done via conjoint simulation: predict choice probabilities for each segment across all tiers. If more than 20% of a high-WTP segment prefers a lower tier, your fence is too weak.

Part 6: The Legal & Ethical Framework

Price discrimination is not inherently illegal. In fact, most forms are perfectly legal and economically efficient. The legal landscape varies by context:

Robinson-Patman Act: The B2B Minefield

The Robinson-Patman Act (1936) is the most commonly relevant statute for B2B price discrimination. It prohibits sellers from charging different prices to different buyers of "commodities of like grade and quality" where the effect "may be substantially to lessen competition." Three critical defenses exist:

Cost Justification Defense: The price difference reflects genuine cost differences in serving the customer. Enterprise customers who require dedicated support, custom integrations, and SLA monitoring genuinely cost more to serve — and this cost difference justifies a higher price (not lower). Conversely, volume discounts are justified if the per-unit cost of serving a large customer is genuinely lower (bulk shipping, reduced transaction overhead).

Meeting Competition Defense: The lower price was offered in good faith to match a competitor's offer to that specific customer. This requires documented evidence of the competitive offer.

Functional Discount Defense: Different prices reflect different functions in the distribution chain (wholesaler vs. retailer). This primarily applies to physical goods distribution.

The Reputational Risk Is Greater Than Legal Risk

For most SaaS and digital businesses, the legal boundaries are clear and permissive. The real constraint is customer perception. If customers discover they're paying different prices based on opaque criteria, the trust damage can far exceed the pricing gains. Amazon famously abandoned a dynamic pricing experiment in 2000 after customers discovered they were being charged different prices for the same DVD based on browsing history. The ensuing PR crisis cost far more than the experiment gained. Best practices: (a) make tier structure publicly visible, (b) justify price differences with genuine value differences (features, SLA, support), (c) never price based on demographic proxies that could be perceived as discriminatory, and (d) let customers self-select into tiers rather than assigning them.

Part 7: Implementation in Python

Python · Segment Elasticity Estimation + Optimal Pricing
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import minimize
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# =============================================
# STEP 1: Define Segments with Estimated Elasticities
# (In practice, these come from Week 14 log-log models)
# =============================================
segments = pd.DataFrame({
    'segment': ['Enterprise', 'Mid-Market', 'SMB', 'Starter'],
    'elasticity': [-1.4, -2.5, -4.0, -7.0],
    'n_customers': [200, 800, 3000, 0],  # Starter=0 at $49
    'current_price': [49, 49, 49, 49],
    'potential_starter': [0, 0, 0, 1500]  # addressable if priced right
})
MC = 20  # marginal cost per user/month

# =============================================
# STEP 2: Inverse Elasticity Pricing Rule
# p* = MC * epsilon / (1 + epsilon)
# =============================================
segments['optimal_price'] = MC * segments['elasticity'] / (1 + segments['elasticity'])
segments['lerner_index'] = -1 / segments['elasticity']

print("=== Inverse Elasticity Pricing ===")
print(segments[['segment', 'elasticity', 'lerner_index',
                'optimal_price']].to_string(index=False))

# =============================================
# STEP 3: Demand Response at New Prices
# Q(p_new) = Q(p_old) * (p_new / p_old) ^ elasticity
# =============================================
p_old = 49
segments['q_uniform'] = segments['n_customers']
segments['q_segmented'] = segments.apply(
    lambda r: r['n_customers'] * (r['optimal_price'] / p_old) ** r['elasticity']
    if r['n_customers'] > 0 else r['potential_starter'], axis=1)

# =============================================
# STEP 4: Profit Comparison
# =============================================
segments['profit_uniform'] = (p_old - MC) * segments['q_uniform']
segments['profit_segmented'] = (segments['optimal_price'] - MC) * segments['q_segmented']

print("\\n=== Profit Comparison (Monthly) ===")
for _, row in segments.iterrows():
    print(f"  {row['segment']:12s}  Uniform: ${row['profit_uniform']:>10,.0f}"
          f"  Segmented: ${row['profit_segmented']:>10,.0f}"
          f"  @ ${row['optimal_price']:.2f}/user")

total_uniform = segments['profit_uniform'].sum()
total_segmented = segments['profit_segmented'].sum()
ser = total_segmented / total_uniform

print(f"\\n  {'TOTAL':12s}  Uniform: ${total_uniform:>10,.0f}"
      f"  Segmented: ${total_segmented:>10,.0f}")
print(f"\\n  Surplus Extraction Ratio (SER): {ser:.2f}x")
print(f"  Additional monthly profit:       ${total_segmented - total_uniform:>+10,.0f}")
print(f"  Additional annual profit:         ${(total_segmented - total_uniform)*12:>+10,.0f}")
Python · Monte Carlo SER Simulation
# =============================================
# STEP 5: Monte Carlo Uncertainty Quantification
# Elasticities are estimates with uncertainty
# =============================================
np.random.seed(42)
n_simulations = 10000
elasticity_se = 0.3  # standard error of elasticity estimates

sim_results = []
for _ in range(n_simulations):
    sim_profit_uniform = 0
    sim_profit_segmented = 0
    
    for _, row in segments.iterrows():
        # Draw elasticity from normal distribution around estimate
        e = np.random.normal(row['elasticity'], elasticity_se)
        e = min(e, -1.05)  # ensure |e| > 1
        
        p_opt = MC * e / (1 + e)
        q_base = row['n_customers'] if row['n_customers'] > 0 else row['potential_starter']
        q_seg = q_base * (p_opt / p_old) ** e if row['n_customers'] > 0 else q_base
        
        sim_profit_uniform += (p_old - MC) * row['n_customers']
        sim_profit_segmented += (p_opt - MC) * q_seg
    
    sim_results.append(sim_profit_segmented / sim_profit_uniform
                       if sim_profit_uniform > 0 else 1)

ser_array = np.array(sim_results)
print(f"\\n=== SER Distribution (n={n_simulations:,} simulations) ===")
print(f"  Mean SER:   {ser_array.mean():.2f}x")
print(f"  Median SER: {np.median(ser_array):.2f}x")
print(f"  95% CI:     [{np.percentile(ser_array, 2.5):.2f}x, {np.percentile(ser_array, 97.5):.2f}x]")
print(f"  P(SER > 1): {(ser_array > 1).mean()*100:.1f}%")
Python · Incentive Compatibility Check
# =============================================
# STEP 6: Does the Menu Self-Select Correctly?
# Check: would enterprise choose Pro tier instead?
# =============================================
tiers = pd.DataFrame({
    'tier': ['Starter', 'Pro', 'Enterprise'],
    'price': [23.33, 33.33, 70.00],
    'value_enterprise': [40, 80, 120],   # WTP by enterprise users
    'value_midmarket': [30, 55, 65],    # WTP by mid-market
    'value_smb': [25, 35, 38],          # WTP by SMB
    'value_starter': [22, 24, 25],      # WTP by starter
})

print("=== Incentive Compatibility Check ===")
for seg in ['enterprise', 'midmarket', 'smb', 'starter']:
    val_col = f'value_{seg}'
    tiers['surplus'] = tiers[val_col] - tiers['price']
    best_tier = tiers.loc[tiers['surplus'].idxmax(), 'tier']
    best_surplus = tiers['surplus'].max()
    print(f"  {seg:12s} → chooses {best_tier:12s} (surplus: ${best_surplus:.2f})")
print("\\nIf each segment chooses the tier designed for them → IC satisfied")
